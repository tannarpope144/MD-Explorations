# Law 39 — Stir Up Waters to Catch Fish

**The Law:** Anger and emotion are strategically counterproductive. You must always stay calm and
objective. But if you can make your enemies angry while staying calm yourself, you gain a decided
advantage. Put your enemies off-balance: find the chink in their vanity through which you can rattle them
and you hold the reins. An angry opponent makes mistakes.

**Essence:** Emotion clouds judgment — so master your own and provoke your enemy's. The cool head in
turbulent water reaches in and takes the fish while the agitated thrash and blunder.

**How a character enacts it:** The cold provocateur stays serene while deliberately needling a rival's
pride, patience, or temper — a calculated insult, a smug smile, a touch on the sore spot (Law 33) — until
the enemy explodes and makes the fatal error. They feed on the contrast: their own glacial calm against
the opponent's red-faced loss of control, which discredits the rival and reveals their hand.

**Tells, tactics & dialogue:** A maddening, unflappable composure. *"You seem upset. Is everything all
right?"* — delivered to someone they've spent the whole scene provoking. A character who wins by being the
last calm person in the room.

**Counter & backfire:** A target who recognizes the provocation refuses to take the bait and lets the
provocateur look petty for trying. Stir up too much chaos and it engulfs the schemer too — agitated water
drowns everyone. And a provoked enemy doesn't always blunder; sometimes rage makes them suddenly,
unpredictably dangerous (Law 15's cornered foe).
