# Law 4 — Always Say Less Than Necessary

**The Law:** When you are trying to impress people with words, the more you say, the more
common you appear and the less in control. Even if you are saying something banal, it will
seem original if you make it vague, open-ended, and sphinxlike. Powerful people impress and
intimidate by saying less.

**Essence:** Silence and brevity project control and force others to fill the void, exposing
themselves. The over-talker hands away power with every extra word.

**How a character enacts it:** The powerful figure answers a flood of questions with a single
measured line, or a long silence, and lets it hang. They withhold reaction — neither approval
nor anger — so others scramble to read them and reveal their own hand. The cryptic remark that
others spend the evening interpreting. Composure as dominance.

**Tells, tactics & dialogue:** A pause where others expect a speech. *"We'll see."* The boss
who lets a nervous subordinate babble into the silence and incriminate themselves. Short,
weighted sentences. The character everyone leans in to hear because they make you work for it.

**Counter & backfire:** Silence can be misread — as agreement, contempt, or weakness — and a
character who never explains can be outmaneuvered by one who controls the narrative aloud. Taken
too far it reads as evasive or cold, and an opponent who simply refuses to fill the silence turns
the tactic back: now *two* people are saying nothing and the quiet one looks merely awkward.
