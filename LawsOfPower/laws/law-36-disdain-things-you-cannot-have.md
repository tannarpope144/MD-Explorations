# Law 36 — Disdain Things You Cannot Have: Ignoring Them Is the Best Revenge

**The Law:** By acknowledging a petty problem you give it existence and credibility. The more attention
you pay an enemy, the stronger you make them; and a small mistake is often made worse and more visible
when you try to fix it. It is sometimes best to leave things alone. If there is something you want but
cannot have, show contempt for it. The less interest you reveal, the more superior you seem.

**Essence:** Attention magnifies whatever it touches. Denying a rival, an insult, or an unattainable
prize your attention shrinks it — and disdain wounds far more than engagement.

**How a character enacts it:** The figure of icy disdain refuses to dignify an attack, a rival, or a
loss with any reaction — treating it as beneath notice. By withholding the recognition the antagonist
craves, they starve it of power and appear untouchable. The lover who walks away without a backward glance;
the ruler who lets a slander die by never acknowledging it.

**Tells, tactics & dialogue:** A serene, devastating indifference. *"I hadn't given it a moment's
thought."* The pointed non-reaction. A character who makes an enemy irrelevant simply by refusing to
treat them as a threat.

**Counter & backfire:** Studied indifference can be a mask for real, festering desire — and the strain of
feigning disdain for what you desperately want can crack. Ignoring a genuine threat because acknowledging
it feels weak is how characters get blindsided. And a clever rival reads the *performance* of disdain as
proof they've struck a nerve, and presses harder.
