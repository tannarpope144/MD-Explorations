# Law 32 — Play to People's Fantasies

**The Law:** The truth is often avoided because it is ugly and unpleasant. Never appeal to truth and
reality unless you are prepared for the anger that comes from disenchantment. Life is so harsh and
distressing that people who can manufacture romance or conjure up fantasy are like oases in the desert:
everyone flocks to them. There is great power in tapping into the fantasies of the masses.

**Essence:** People will follow and reward whoever feeds their dreams over whoever tells them the truth.
Sell the glittering fantasy; the ugly reality wins no devotion.

**How a character enacts it:** The charlatan, demagogue, or seducer reads what people desperately wish
were true — easy riches, lost glory restored, a romance, a miracle cure — and sells exactly that. They
wrap a lie in the shape of the audience's deepest longing, knowing no one scrutinizes a dream they want
to believe. The con works because the mark is complicit in their own deception.

**Tells, tactics & dialogue:** *"I can give you the life you've always dreamed of."* The pitch that's
suspiciously perfectly tailored to the listener's yearning. A character who never sells the product —
only the fantasy the product promises.

**Counter & backfire:** Fantasies collide with reality eventually, and the disenchantment is violent —
the crowd that adored the dream-seller turns murderous when the dream proves empty. A grounded character
who insists on hard truth can puncture the fantasy and expose the seller. And the fantasist who peddles
dreams long enough may lose their own grip on what's real.
