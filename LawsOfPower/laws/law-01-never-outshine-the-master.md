# Law 1 — Never Outshine the Master

**The Law:** Always make those above you feel comfortably superior. In your desire to
please and impress them, do not go too far in displaying your talents, or you might
accomplish the opposite — inspire fear and insecurity. Make your masters appear more
brilliant than they are and you will attain the heights of power.

**Essence:** Power flows to those who flatter their patrons' sense of superiority.
Outshining a master breeds an insecurity that curdles into your destruction.

**How a character enacts it:** The shrewd subordinate dims their own shine on purpose —
crediting the master's "vision" for their own ideas, asking advice they don't need,
losing gracefully at the master's favored game. They calibrate constantly: how much
brilliance can I show before the king's smile tightens? The ambitious fool, by contrast,
dazzles to win favor and earns a knife instead.

**Tells, tactics & dialogue:** Deference laced with strategy. *"It was your insight that
made it possible, my lord — I merely carried it out."* Letting the master deliver the
clever line. Public humility, private calculation. A subordinate who suddenly downplays a
triumph is often protecting themselves, not being modest.

**Counter & backfire:** A master who senses the act feels patronized — worse than
outshone. A subordinate can dim *too* far and be dismissed as useless. And the masked
talent festers: the resentful genius who's spent years making a lesser man look good is a
classic powder keg, primed to betray the moment the balance shifts (see Law 2).
