# Law 47 — Do Not Go Past the Mark You Aimed For; In Victory, Know When to Stop

**The Law:** The moment of victory is often the moment of greatest peril. In the heat of victory, arrogance
and overconfidence can push you past the goal you had aimed for, and by going too far, you make more enemies
than you defeat. Do not allow success to go to your head. There is no substitute for strategy and careful
planning. Set a goal, and when you reach it, stop.

**Essence:** Victory intoxicates, and intoxication breeds the overreach that turns triumph into ruin. The
master sets a mark, reaches it, and has the discipline to stop.

**How a character enacts it:** The disciplined victor banks the win and withdraws while ahead — resisting the
heady urge to press on for total domination. They know that each conquest past the goal creates fresh enemies
and tempts the reversal of fortune. The contrast is the tragic overreacher: the conqueror who, drunk on
success, marches one campaign too far and loses everything they'd won.

**Tells, tactics & dialogue:** *"We've won what we came for. We stop here."* The leader who pulls back at the
peak while subordinates clamor for more. Or, as cautionary tale: *"Just one more, and it will all be mine"* —
the last words before the fall.

**Counter & backfire:** Stopping too soon leaves an enemy alive to recover and avenge (the direct tension with
Law 15 — knowing whether to *crush totally* or *stop at the mark* is the character's defining judgment call).
Restraint can read as weakness to hungry followers, inviting a challenge. And the disciplined victor who stops
may simply hand the momentum to a bolder rival willing to seize what they left on the table.
