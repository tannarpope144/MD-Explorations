# Law 18 — Do Not Build Fortresses to Protect Yourself; Isolation Is Dangerous

**The Law:** The world is dangerous and enemies are everywhere — everyone has to protect themselves.
A fortress seems the safest. But isolation exposes you to more dangers than it protects you from — it
cuts you off from valuable information, it makes you conspicuous and an easy target. Better to
circulate among people, find allies, mingle.

**Essence:** Walls that keep enemies out also keep information out and trap you within. The isolated
figure is blind, conspicuous, and easy to besiege.

**How a character enacts it:** The wiser power-player resists the urge to retreat into a guarded inner
circle. They move through the world, gather intelligence by contact (Law 14), and keep a web of allies.
The contrast character — the paranoid king who walls himself in his castle — illustrates the law by
violating it: cut off from truth, fed only flattery, blindsided by the coup he never saw coming.

**Tells, tactics & dialogue:** *"Up there in his tower, he had no idea the city had already turned."*
The ruler surrounded only by yes-men. The retreat into seclusion that looks like safety and is actually
a slow-motion trap.

**Counter & backfire:** This law's *failure* mode is the whole drama: the character who fortifies
themselves becomes paranoid, misinformed, and ripe for overthrow. But circulating freely has its own
risk — exposure to the very assassins and schemers the fortress was meant to bar. The balance between
openness and safety is itself a source of tension.
