# Worked Character Sketches

Casting references that show how clusters of laws combine into a coherent power-player.
Each sketch names the **core laws** that drive the character, how they **operate**, a
**signature scene**, and the **fatal flaw / arc** — the tension between their laws that
plot can exploit. Mix and remix these; the most memorable characters violate one law
spectacularly while mastering the rest.

---

## 1. The Spider — court schemer / grand strategist

*The advisor who never holds the throne but decides who does.*

- **Core laws:** 3 (conceal intentions), 14 (pose as friend, spy), 26 (keep hands clean),
  29 (plan to the end), 31 (control the options), 33 (find the thumbscrew).
- **How they operate:** Never acts directly. Gathers everyone's secrets through warm,
  patient friendship, then arranges events so rivals destroy each other while the Spider
  appears to mourn. Every "free choice" the court makes was a door the Spider built. Their
  true aim is invisible until it's already accomplished.
- **Signature scene:** Consoling the very person they've ruined, offering wise counsel that
  steers the victim's revenge toward a *second* enemy — solving two problems with one
  whispered suggestion.
- **Fatal flaw / arc:** Plans for everything except the irrational (Law 29's brittleness vs.
  Law 48). A single player who acts from pure emotion — love, grief, madness — breaks a web
  built for rational actors. Or a discarded cat's-paw (Law 26's backfire) lives to name them.

---

## 2. The Sun King — magnetic tyrant / showman

*Power as performance; the room reorganizes around them.*

- **Core laws:** 6 (court attention), 17 (suspended terror), 28 (boldness), 34 (be royal),
  37 (compelling spectacles), 27 (need to believe).
- **How they operate:** Rules by dazzle and dread in equal measure — lavish spectacle that
  binds devotion, unpredictable cruelty that keeps everyone anxious to please. Projects total
  certainty; the boldness is contagious and the crowd mistakes it for destiny. Demands belief,
  and gets it.
- **Signature scene:** A grand public ceremony — gifts, symbols, overwhelming theater — during
  which they quietly destroy a rival, the crowd too dazzled to notice the knife.
- **Fatal flaw / arc:** Lives on attention and belief, both of which must escalate or starve
  (Laws 6, 27 backfires). Surrounds themselves with terrified yes-men and walls out the truth
  (violates Law 18) — blindsided by the coup they never saw because no one dared warn them. The
  spectacle that fails publicly humiliates as hard as it once exalted.

## 3. The Mirror — charming con artist / seducer

*Becomes whatever you most want to see.*

- **Core laws:** 12 (selective honesty to disarm), 32 (play to fantasies), 43 (work on hearts
  and minds), 44 (mirror effect), 25 (re-create yourself).
- **How they operate:** Reads a mark's deepest longing and reflects it back until the mark feels
  they've met a soulmate. Disarms with one striking act of genuine honesty or generosity, then
  walks through the lowered guard. Sells the dream, never the truth. Has no fixed self — only the
  shape the current mark desires.
- **Signature scene:** The mark, defenses fully down, *insists* on giving the con artist what
  they wanted all along — believing it was their own idea, their own desire (Law 43).
- **Fatal flaw / arc:** Two marks compare notes and the contradictory performances collapse the
  spell (Law 43's backfire). Or — the richer arc — the Mirror reflects so many people they lose
  their own face (Law 25's cost), and when they finally feel something real, they no longer know
  if it's genuine or just another reflection.

## 4. The Stone — ruthless conqueror

*Wins totally, and that is exactly the problem.*

- **Core laws:** 15 (crush totally), 23 (concentrate forces), 28 (boldness), 5 (reputation),
  9 (win through actions).
- **How they operate:** Massed force at the single decisive point, no hesitation, no half-measures.
  A reputation for total destruction so fearsome that most enemies fold without a fight. Doesn't
  argue — demonstrates, with overwhelming results. Leaves no ember of an enemy alive.
- **Signature scene:** Sparing no one in a defeated faction — methodical, unsentimental — while a
  subordinate begs for mercy the Stone considers a strategic blunder.
- **Fatal flaw / arc:** The defining judgment they keep getting wrong is Law 15 vs. Law 47 — *crush
  totally* or *stop at the mark?* Drunk on victory, they march one conquest too far and make more
  enemies than they defeat (Law 47's backfire). Or their very ruthlessness births a survivor's heir
  who carries the vendetta to the final act.

## 5. The Reed — survivor / chameleon

*Bends in every storm, outlasts everyone.*

- **Core laws:** 22 (surrender tactic), 38 (behave like others), 48 (formlessness), 20 (commit to
  no one), 1 (never outshine the master).
- **How they operate:** Never fights when weak — kneels, flatters, becomes useful and unthreatening,
  and waits. Wears the mask of orthodoxy over private heresy. Commits to no side, so every regime
  finds them serviceable. Survives kings, coups, and revolutions by having no fixed shape to break.
- **Signature scene:** Swearing fervent loyalty to the new regime that just overthrew the old one
  they served just as fervently yesterday — and being believed, because they're so useful.
- **Fatal flaw / arc:** Bends so long the mask becomes the face (Laws 22, 38 backfires) — survives
  every storm but stands for nothing, with no real ally when they finally need one (Law 20's cost).
  Their arc is often the quiet tragedy of the man who lived through everything and was loved by no
  one. Or: forced at last to take a shape they cannot flow away from (Law 48's counter).

## 6. The Heir — protégé in a giant's shadow

*Must surpass a legend, or be devoured by it.*

- **Core laws:** 41 (escape the great man's shadow), 25 (re-create yourself), 1 (don't outshine the
  master — yet), 35 (timing), 2 (use enemies).
- **How they operate:** Serves the great predecessor while it's necessary (dimming their own light,
  Law 1), studying, waiting (Law 35), quietly building a base of their own — including from the
  mentor's *enemies* (Law 2). When the moment ripens, they break from the legacy and claim a name of
  their own, sometimes by tearing the old hero down.
- **Signature scene:** The public moment of repudiation — symbolically rejecting the mentor's way to
  step into the light — that either crowns them or brands them an ingrate.
- **Fatal flaw / arc:** Defines themselves so entirely *against* the predecessor that they never
  escape the shadow at all (Law 41's backfire). Or repudiates a beloved legacy too openly and the
  mentor's loyalists destroy them. The richest version: the moment they surpass the giant, they
  discover they've *become* them — and now there's a new Heir watching from the wings.

---

## Using these

- **For a protagonist,** give them mastery of a few laws and a genuine struggle with the
  *Counter & backfire* of their signature law — that tension is their inner conflict.
- **For an antagonist,** let them run their laws nearly flawlessly; the plot is the hero finding
  the one crack the backfire section names.
- **For a scene,** put two sketches in a room running incompatible laws (the Stone vs. the Reed; the
  Spider vs. the Sun King) and let the collision write itself.
- **Pull the detail** from the individual `laws/law-NN-*.md` files when you need a specific move,
  line, or failure mode for whatever a character does next.
