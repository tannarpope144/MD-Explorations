# P058 — Cartels Are Inherently Unstable

**Category:** Industry & Commerce
**Tags:** cartels, collusion, oligopoly, cheating, self-interest
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 6 — The Economics of Big Business

## Principle
A cartel succeeds only if all members restrict output to maintain the agreed-upon price.
But each individual member can profit by secretly selling more than its quota at the
cartel price — undercutting the cartel while it holds up prices. Since every member
faces this same incentive, cartels are chronically subject to defection and collapse.
Sustained cartels typically require either legal enforcement (government-sanctioned
cartels) or conditions that make defection easy to detect and punish.

## In plain English
Cartels are agreements to act as a monopolist together — but each member can make
more money by cheating on the agreement. Self-interest pulls them apart from the
inside.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Large oligopolies will naturally collude and maintain monopoly prices indefinitely."
The same self-interest that makes collusion attractive to a group makes defection
attractive to each individual member. Private cartels without enforcement mechanisms
tend to break down.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell discusses how private cartels in 19th-century American
  industry repeatedly formed and collapsed as members cheated on price agreements.
  The railroad rate agreements of the 1870s–80s were notorious for breaking down
  as individual railroads cut rates to capture more traffic.
- **Broader cases:** OPEC has repeatedly experienced member cheating on production
  quotas, causing oil prices to fall from their cartel peaks. The cartel has been
  most effective when Saudi Arabia acted as a swing producer willing to punish
  defectors by flooding the market. Private industrial cartels (vitamins in the
  1990s, auto parts in the 2000s) required secret, legally enforceable agreements
  among participants and still eventually collapsed under government investigation.
