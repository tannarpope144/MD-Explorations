# P094 — Job-Security Mandates Reduce Hiring

**Category:** Work & Pay
**Tags:** job-security, hiring, mandates, labor-market, unintended-consequences
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 9 — Labor and Pay

## Principle
Laws that make it costly to dismiss workers — mandatory severance, lengthy notice
periods, legal obstacles to layoffs — raise the cost of hiring because they raise
the expected cost of un-hiring. Employers anticipating these obligations hire fewer
workers in the first place, rely more on overtime and contractors, and use temporary
arrangements to avoid triggering the mandate. The result is less employment flexibility
and, in many cases, a smaller formal labor market.

## In plain English
Making it expensive to fire workers makes employers reluctant to hire them. If you
can't let someone go without a large severance, you think carefully before bringing
them on — and sometimes you don't.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Job-security laws protect workers by guaranteeing employment." They protect incumbent
workers who already have jobs but reduce opportunities for workers seeking to enter
the labor market — often the young and those transitioning between jobs. Employment
protection is a transfer from job-seekers to job-holders.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell cites highly regulated European labor markets — notably
  France and Spain — where strong employment protection laws are associated with
  persistently higher youth unemployment and a large divide between protected
  "insiders" with permanent contracts and unprotected "outsiders" on temporary ones.
- **Broader cases:** France's two-tier labor market (CDI permanent contracts vs. CDD
  temporary contracts) reflects employers' preference for the more flexible arrangement
  when hiring new workers, in direct response to the high cost of dismissing CDI
  workers. Spain's youth unemployment exceeding 40% in the aftermath of the 2008
  financial crisis was partly attributed by economists to the high cost of adjusting
  permanent headcount.
