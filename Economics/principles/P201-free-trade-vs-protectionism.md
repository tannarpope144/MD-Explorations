# P201 — Free Trade vs. Protectionism

**Category:** International Economy
**Tags:** free-trade, protectionism, trade-policy, consumers, producers
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 15 — International Trade

## Principle
Free trade maximizes total economic output by allowing specialization according to
comparative advantage. Protectionist policies (tariffs, quotas, subsidies) preserve
specific industries at the cost of higher prices for consumers and misallocation of
resources toward industries where the country has no comparative advantage. The
gains from free trade are diffuse (many consumers pay slightly less) while the
losses are concentrated (workers in protected industries are visible), creating
a political economy that systematically biases policy toward protection.

## In plain English
Free trade makes the whole country richer; protectionism makes specific industries
richer at everyone else's expense. The political economy favors protection because
the winners are organized and loud, and the losers (consumers paying a bit more
for everything) are dispersed and silent.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Protecting domestic industries from foreign competition is good for the economy."
Protection preserves specific jobs at high per-job cost, raises prices for all
consumers, misallocates resources, and typically triggers retaliation that reduces
export markets. The economy as a whole loses; only the protected industry wins.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell documents how U.S. textile and apparel tariffs have
  kept prices for clothing higher than free-trade prices, transferring income from
  consumers to domestic producers — a regressive transfer since clothing is a
  larger share of low-income budgets.
- **Broader cases:** The Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act of 1930 raised U.S. tariffs to
  historic highs, triggering retaliation from trading partners, reducing global
  trade by roughly one-third, and deepening the Great Depression. Post-WWII trade
  liberalization under GATT/WTO is broadly credited with enabling the global
  growth of the 1950s–90s.
