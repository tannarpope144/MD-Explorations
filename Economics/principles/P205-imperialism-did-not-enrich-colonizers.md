# P205 — Imperialism Did Not Generally Enrich the Colonizers

**Category:** International Economy
**Tags:** imperialism, colonialism, history, wealth, trade
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 16 — International Trade Restrictions

## Principle
The standard narrative that colonial powers grew wealthy by extracting wealth from
colonies is contradicted by the economic evidence. Many colonies were costly to
administer and defend, with the financial benefits going primarily to specific
companies or politically connected groups rather than to the colonizing nation as
a whole. Countries that gave up colonies after WWII (Britain, France, the Netherlands)
grew faster economically in the decades that followed than during the colonial period.

## In plain English
Colonies were often a net cost to the colonizing country as a whole — even if they
enriched specific interests. The countries that let their empires go after WWII
mostly grew faster afterward, not slower.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Rich countries are wealthy because they extracted wealth from poor countries through
colonialism." While colonialism caused real harm in many places, its contribution to
the wealth of the colonizing nations as a whole is not clearly positive. The economic
growth of Western Europe and the United States owed far more to productivity growth,
institutions, and technology than to colonial extraction.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell examines the historical record and finds that the costs
  of empire — military, administrative — were substantial and largely socialized, while
  the benefits were largely privatized. He cites the rapid economic growth of Britain,
  France, Germany, and Japan after decolonization and defeat, suggesting empire was
  not the source of their prosperity.
- **Broader cases:** The Economic historians' debate over whether Britain's Industrial
  Revolution was funded by colonial profits has largely concluded that domestic
  savings, agricultural productivity, and institutional changes were far larger
  contributors than colonial extraction. Netherlands, Belgium, and Portugal — all
  with significant colonial histories — saw their sharpest economic growth only
  after losing their empires.
