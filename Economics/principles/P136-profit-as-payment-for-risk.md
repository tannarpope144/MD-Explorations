# P136 — Profit as Payment for Bearing Risk

**Category:** Time & Risk
**Tags:** profit, risk, uncertainty, entrepreneurship
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 12 — Investment, Gambling, and Insurance

## Principle
A portion of profit in market economies compensates entrepreneurs and investors for
bearing the risk of loss — investing resources in ventures whose outcomes are uncertain.
Higher expected profits are required to attract investment to riskier projects. Without
this risk premium, capital would not flow to uncertain, potentially high-return ventures,
and innovation would slow. The ex-post profits of successful ventures are large partly
because many similar ventures fail, and the successful outcomes must compensate for
the unsuccessful ones.

## In plain English
High profits from successful ventures look excessive in isolation. But you're seeing
the winners; the losers already lost their investment. The high profit on winners is
partly compensation for the risk of being one of the losers — without it, no one
would take that risk.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Profits from successful businesses are too high and should be taxed away." Taxing
profits asymmetrically (taking more of gains while not compensating losses equally)
reduces the expected return from risk-bearing, discourages investment in uncertain
ventures, and reduces innovation. The risk premium is a real economic cost, not
mere extraction.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell discusses the pharmaceutical industry, where the high
  profitability of blockbuster drugs is partly compensation for the many drug
  candidates that fail to reach market after enormous R&D investment. The winners
  must pay for the losers, or the R&D pipeline dries up.
- **Broader cases:** Venture capital returns are highly skewed: a small fraction of
  investments produce most of the returns, compensating for the majority that fail.
  Studies of U.S. venture capital suggest that most individual investments lose money,
  and the overall return comes from a handful of large successes — consistent with
  profit as a risk premium.
