# P009 — Black Markets Arise from Price Controls

**Category:** Prices & Markets
**Tags:** black-markets, price-controls, unintended-consequences, enforcement
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 3 — Price Controls

## Principle
When a price ceiling prevents a legal transaction at the market-clearing price, buyers
who value the good above the ceiling price and sellers who can supply it above cost have
mutual incentive to transact outside the law. The result is a black market — illegal
trade at or near the market-clearing price — whose existence undermines the stated goals
of the price control and often introduces additional social costs (crime, corruption, unsafe
products).

## In plain English
You can make a price illegal, but you cannot make the underlying incentive to trade
disappear. Suppress the legal market and the transaction moves underground.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Strict enforcement can make price controls effective." Enforcement raises the cost of
black-market transactions but does not eliminate them as long as the spread between the
controlled price and the market-clearing price is large enough to compensate for the risk.
More often, aggressive enforcement diverts resources and increases corruption.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** WWII-era price controls on meat in the United States generated
  an extensive black market, with controlled goods diverted to illegal channels.
  New York City's rent control is associated with under-the-table "key money" payments
  to landlords — an informal black-market rent premium paid by tenants to secure a
  below-market apartment.
- **Broader cases:** Soviet-era price controls on consumer goods produced a pervasive
  "second economy" (black and gray markets) that was estimated to account for a
  substantial fraction of actual consumption. Drug prohibition is a long-running example
  of how criminalizing a transaction drives it underground without eliminating demand.
