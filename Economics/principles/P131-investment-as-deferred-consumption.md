# P131 — Investment as Deferred Consumption

**Category:** Time & Risk
**Tags:** investment, saving, deferred-consumption, capital-formation
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 12 — Investment, Gambling, and Insurance

## Principle
Investment is the use of resources today to create more resources in the future —
whether physical capital (machinery, buildings), human capital (education, skills),
or financial capital. Investment is possible only if current consumption is reduced
(deferred) to free resources for capital formation. The level of investment a society
makes determines its future productive capacity and living standards; high consumption
today crowds out investment and reduces future growth.

## In plain English
You can only build something for tomorrow by not consuming everything today.
Investment is giving something up now to have more later — savings and capital
formation are the mechanism that makes economic growth possible.

## The fallacy it corrects
"We can have maximum consumption now and maximum investment for the future at the
same time." Resources are scarce; consuming them now means they cannot be invested
for later growth. There is a genuine trade-off between present and future consumption,
and societies that save and invest more grow faster than those that do not.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell traces how the high savings rates in post-WWII Japan
  and later in East Asian "tiger" economies (South Korea, Taiwan, Singapore) were
  associated with very high rates of capital formation and rapid economic growth —
  a direct expression of the investment-as-deferred-consumption principle at a
  national scale.
- **Broader cases:** The dramatic decline in the U.S. personal savings rate from
  the 1970s through the 2000s, combined with capital inflows from high-saving
  countries, illustrates the relationship: Americans consumed more relative to
  income and relied on foreign investment to fund domestic capital formation.
