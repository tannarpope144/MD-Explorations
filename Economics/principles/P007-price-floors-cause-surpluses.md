# P007 — Price Floors Cause Surpluses

**Category:** Prices & Markets
**Tags:** price-controls, price-floors, surpluses, agricultural-policy
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 3 — Price Controls

## Principle
A price floor set above the market-clearing price simultaneously decreases quantity
demanded (fewer buyers want the good at the higher price) and increases quantity
supplied (producers find it more profitable to produce). The resulting gap between
quantity supplied and quantity demanded is a surplus that persists as long as the
floor is in place.

## In plain English
Force the price of anything above what the market would set, and you will get more
sellers and fewer buyers — that gap is a surplus. Agricultural price supports create
exactly this: mountains of unsold grain, milk lakes, and butter mountains.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Higher prices help producers by giving them more income." A price floor benefits
only those who can actually sell their output. When demand falls and supply rises,
much of what is produced goes unsold — or must be bought and destroyed by the
government at taxpayer expense.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** U.S. agricultural price supports since the 1930s have repeatedly
  generated surpluses of corn, wheat, dairy products, and tobacco — often purchased
  and stored (or destroyed) by the government. European Union farm subsidies similarly
  produced the famous "butter mountains" and "wine lakes" of the 1970s–80s.
- **Broader cases:** The EU's Common Agricultural Policy has cost consumers and taxpayers
  large sums while generating chronic surpluses requiring export subsidies or destruction.
  Minimum-wage laws are price floors on labor and produce the analogous result in the
  labor market: a surplus of workers seeking employment at the mandated wage.
