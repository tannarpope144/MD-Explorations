# P052 — Profit Rate vs. Total Profit

**Category:** Industry & Commerce
**Tags:** profit, rate-of-return, capital, misunderstanding
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 5 — The Rise and Fall of Businesses

## Principle
The relevant measure for understanding whether a business is earning excessive returns
is the rate of profit on capital invested — not the absolute dollar amount of profit.
A large company earning a modest rate of return is not "gouging" consumers; a small
company earning a high rate of return may be. Comparisons of raw profit totals across
industries of different sizes are economically meaningless.

## In plain English
A billion-dollar profit sounds large, but if it is earned on fifty billion dollars of
invested capital, the return is modest. Total profit numbers make headlines; profit
rates tell you whether capital is being unusually rewarded.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Company X made $Y billion in profit — that's obscene." Profit totals are uninformative
without knowing the capital base. Many industries with large absolute profits (oil,
retail, insurance) earn modest rates of return on capital; many small businesses earn
far higher rates.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell notes that grocery stores, which handle enormous revenue
  volumes, typically earn profit margins of around one to two cents per dollar of sales
  — modest by any measure. "Windfall profits" in oil frequently reflect capital
  intensity more than unusual returns.
- **Broader cases:** During periods of high oil prices, U.S. oil companies have reported
  record dollar profits while earning rates of return roughly comparable to other capital-
  intensive industries. Congressional windfall-profit taxes have been imposed based on
  total profit figures, discouraging investment and reducing domestic output.
