# P093 — Employers Substitute Capital for Labor When Labor Is Made More Expensive

**Category:** Work & Pay
**Tags:** capital-substitution, labor-costs, automation, minimum-wage
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 9 — Labor and Pay

## Principle
Labor and capital are substitutes in production. When the cost of labor rises —
through minimum wage increases, mandated benefits, or payroll taxes — employers
have incentive to substitute capital (machines, automation, software) for labor,
reducing employment more than they would have otherwise. The substitution does
not happen instantaneously and may be incomplete, but it is a systematic response
to relative price changes.

## In plain English
When you make workers more expensive, employers look for ways to use fewer workers —
machines, automation, restructured processes. It's not vindictive; it's the same
logic as any input substitution when a price rises.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Raising labor costs won't reduce employment because employers need workers to operate."
Employers need labor services, not a fixed number of workers. They can get the same
services with fewer workers if each produces more (through capital investment) or if
they redesign the production process.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell points to the substitution of automated teller machines
  for bank tellers and self-checkout lanes for cashiers as examples of capital
  substituting for labor in response to rising labor costs and improving technology.
- **Broader cases:** Fast-food chains accelerated investment in self-ordering kiosks
  and automated cooking equipment in U.S. cities that raised minimum wages sharply
  in the 2010s. Agricultural mechanization in the American South accelerated after
  WWII when labor market conditions tightened and wages rose — a large-scale historical
  example of capital substitution for labor in response to relative price changes.
