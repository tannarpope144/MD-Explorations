# P002 — Prices as Signals

**Category:** Prices & Markets
**Tags:** prices, information, coordination, signals
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 2 — The Role of Prices

## Principle
Prices transmit information about relative scarcity and value across an economy without
requiring any central authority to gather or distribute that information. A rising price
signals that a resource has become scarcer or more valued; a falling price signals the
opposite. These signals guide producers and consumers toward or away from particular
activities.

## In plain English
When something becomes scarcer, its price rises — and that single number tells everyone
who might buy or produce it to adjust, without anyone needing to explain why.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Prices are just costs imposed on buyers." Prices are not merely costs — they are
carriers of information. Suppressing them (via controls) doesn't remove the underlying
scarcity; it just destroys the signal, leaving everyone to act in the dark.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell describes how a drought in Brazil causing coffee scarcity
  is communicated to consumers worldwide through a single price rise, prompting them
  to economize without knowing anything about Brazil. No government decree or awareness
  campaign was needed.
- **Broader cases:** During the 1970s OPEC oil embargo, price controls in the United
  States suppressed the signal that oil had become scarce, leading to long gas lines
  and misallocation. Countries that allowed prices to rise (or rose faster to remove
  controls) adjusted supply and demand without the visible shortages. The price signal,
  when allowed to function, transmitted scarcity information globally and instantly.
