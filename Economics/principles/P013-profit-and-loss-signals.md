# P013 — Profit and Loss as Guidance Signals

**Category:** Prices & Markets
**Tags:** profit, loss, signals, resource-allocation, incentives
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 2 — The Role of Prices

## Principle
Profits signal that an activity is creating value exceeding its costs — that resources
are being used in ways consumers value highly — and attract more resources into that
activity. Losses signal the opposite and drive resources away from activities consumers
value less than their cost. Together, profit and loss guide resource allocation without
central direction, rewarding what works and penalizing what does not.

## In plain English
Profit means "do more of this"; loss means "do less of this." These signals direct
capital, labor, and entrepreneurial effort toward their most valued uses — no central
planner required.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Profit is just money taken from consumers or workers; it serves no social purpose."
Profit is a signal and an incentive, not merely a transfer. Eliminating profits
(through price controls or nationalization) destroys the information that tells
producers what to produce and in what quantities.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell contrasts the rapid adaptation of private firms to consumer
  preferences with the chronic misalignment of Soviet state enterprises that faced no
  profit-and-loss test. Soviet enterprises produced what the plan dictated, not what
  consumers wanted, leading to famous surpluses of unwanted goods and shortages of
  desired ones.
- **Broader cases:** The entry of new firms into profitable industries (personal
  computers in the 1980s, smartphones in the 2000s) and the exit of firms from
  loss-making ones (U.S. steel mini-mills displacing integrated mills) demonstrate
  the allocative function of profit and loss operating in real time.
