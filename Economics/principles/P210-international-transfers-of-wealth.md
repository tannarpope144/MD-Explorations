# P210 — International Transfers of Wealth (Foreign Aid, Investment) — Effects

**Category:** International Economy
**Tags:** foreign-aid, investment, development, transfers
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 16 — International Transfers of Wealth

## Principle
Foreign aid transfers purchasing power from donor to recipient governments, but its
effect on long-run development depends entirely on how the receiving government uses
it. Aid that supports consumption or enables governments to avoid necessary reforms
has little growth effect; aid that funds genuinely productive investment may accelerate
growth. Foreign direct investment tends to be more effective than aid because it is
conditional on the investor's own profit, aligning incentives with productive use.

## In plain English
Giving money to a poor country doesn't make it rich. What matters is whether the
institutions and incentives are in place to use it productively. Investment (where
the investor profits only if the investment works) is better aligned with development
than aid (where the donor does not share in the outcome).

## The fallacy it corrects
"Poverty in developing countries could be solved if rich countries gave more aid."
Decades of large-scale foreign aid have not reliably produced sustained development.
Countries that grew rapidly (South Korea, Taiwan, Singapore, China) did so primarily
through investment, institutional improvement, and trade integration — not through
aid dependence.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell discusses how much foreign aid has been absorbed by
  government bureaucracies, diverted to politically connected groups, or used to
  finance consumption rather than investment — producing dependency rather than development.
- **Broader cases:** William Easterly's research (*The White Man's Burden*, 2006)
  documents that roughly $2.3 trillion in foreign aid to Africa over 50 years had
  not produced sustained growth across the continent. Countries that received less
  aid but pursued market-oriented reforms (Botswana, Mauritius) grew far faster than
  heavy aid recipients.
