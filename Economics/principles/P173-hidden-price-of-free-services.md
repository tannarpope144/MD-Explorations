# P173 — The Hidden Price of "Free" Government Services

**Category:** National Economy
**Tags:** free-services, opportunity-cost, government-spending, taxation
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 14 — The Role of Government

## Principle
Services provided "free" by government are not actually costless — their production
consumes real resources (labor, capital, materials) that have alternative uses.
The cost is borne by taxpayers and by everyone who is denied the goods and services
those resources would have produced in their next-best use. "Free" government
services also tend to be overdemanded (because the price to the user is zero)
and underpriced (because there is no market feedback on whether the service is
worth its cost), leading to waste and misallocation.

## In plain English
Free doesn't mean cheap — it means the cost is hidden and paid by someone else.
When government provides something at zero price, people demand more of it than
if they paid for it, and there is no way to tell whether it is worth what it costs.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Providing services for free ensures everyone gets what they need regardless of ability
to pay." Zero price generates excess demand, misallocates resources toward uses that
may not be the most valuable, and hides the true cost from both users and policymakers,
making it impossible to determine whether the service is worth its cost.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell discusses how "free" public parking lots, roads, and
  services generate excess demand, congestion, and waste. He applies the same logic
  to price-controlled healthcare: services priced below cost are overdemanded,
  leading to waiting lists and rationing by other means.
- **Broader cases:** The U.S. Interstate Highway System, largely "free" to users,
  has generated chronic congestion that could be reduced by pricing (toll roads).
  The introduction of congestion pricing in Stockholm (2006) and London (2003)
  reduced traffic by roughly 20–30%, demonstrating that the zero-price subsidy
  had been generating excess demand.
