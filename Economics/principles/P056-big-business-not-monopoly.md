# P056 — Big Business Is Not the Same as Monopoly

**Category:** Industry & Commerce
**Tags:** big-business, monopoly, competition, market-power
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 6 — The Economics of Big Business

## Principle
A large firm is not necessarily a monopoly, and a monopoly need not be large. Monopoly
is defined by the absence of effective competition and the resulting ability to raise
prices above cost without losing customers — not by absolute size. Large firms in
competitive industries (Walmart, Toyota) face vigorous competition and cannot earn
monopoly returns; small firms with a unique local franchise can.

## In plain English
Being big doesn't mean having monopoly power. What matters is whether competitors
exist and customers have alternatives — not the company's size.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Large corporations dominate the economy and must be broken up to restore competition."
The relevant question is not how big a company is but whether it faces effective
competition. Breaking up a large competitive firm merely creates smaller firms in the
same competitive market — it does not increase competition if competition already existed.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell points out that large American retailers like Sears (in
  an earlier era) and Walmart face constant pressure from competitors and do not earn
  monopoly returns. Their size reflects efficiency gains passed to consumers, not
  market power extracted from them.
- **Broader cases:** The U.S. steel industry in the early 20th century saw U.S. Steel
  controlling a large share of domestic production, yet prices did not rise to monopoly
  levels because competition from smaller domestic mills and (eventually) imports
  remained a disciplining force. Microsoft's antitrust case in the late 1990s illustrated
  the difficulty of defining market power in technology markets where substitutes evolve rapidly.
