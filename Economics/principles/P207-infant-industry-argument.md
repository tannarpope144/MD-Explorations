# P207 — The "Infant Industry" Argument and Its Problems

**Category:** International Economy
**Tags:** infant-industry, protectionism, trade-policy, industrial-policy
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 15 — International Trade

## Principle
The infant-industry argument holds that new industries unable to compete with
established foreign producers should receive temporary protection until they
develop enough to compete on their own. The economic logic requires that: (1)
there are genuine learning-by-doing gains not captured by the firm itself; (2)
the industry will become competitive after a finite period; and (3) the future
gains exceed the current cost of protection. In practice, "temporary" protection
is almost never temporary, and protected industries rarely become competitive.

## In plain English
"Protect us now and we'll compete later" is the argument every struggling industry
makes — but the protection rarely ends, the industry rarely becomes competitive,
and consumers pay the cost indefinitely.

## The fallacy it corrects
"New industries need protection to get started; after they develop, they'll compete
without help." The political economy makes this outcome nearly impossible: once an
industry has protection, it has both the incentive and the resources to lobby for
continued protection. The "infant" never grows up because it doesn't have to.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell notes that the steel industry in the United States
  received protection for over a century under various "temporary" infant-industry
  and national-security rationales — it never became competitive enough to end
  its dependence on protection.
- **Broader cases:** Latin American import-substitution industrialization, practiced
  widely in the 1950s–70s, used infant-industry protection to build domestic
  manufacturing. By the 1980s, most of these protected industries had not achieved
  competitiveness; growth rates lagged those of outward-oriented East Asian economies
  that competed on world markets without protection.
