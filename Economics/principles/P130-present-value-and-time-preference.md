# P130 — Present Value and Time Preference

**Category:** Time & Risk
**Tags:** present-value, time-preference, discounting, investment
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 12 — Investment, Gambling, and Insurance

## Principle
A dollar received today is worth more than a dollar received in the future, because
the present dollar can be invested to earn a return, and because people generally
prefer earlier consumption to later. This time preference means that future benefits
and costs must be discounted to their present value to make meaningful comparisons.
Decisions that appear to create value when future costs and benefits are compared at
face value often destroy value once proper discounting is applied, and vice versa.

## In plain English
Money now is worth more than the same money later. Anyone making decisions about
investments, loans, or policy that ignore this fact is systematically misjudging
whether they are creating or destroying value.

## The fallacy it corrects
"A policy that costs $X now but saves $5X in forty years is obviously a good deal."
Costs paid today and benefits received in forty years must both be expressed in
present-value terms before comparing. A benefit forty years away, discounted at
even a modest rate, may be worth far less in present-value terms than it appears.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell uses the concept of present value to evaluate claims about
  natural-resource depletion and long-term investments: the expected future value of a
  resource must be discounted to determine how much it is worth saving (not extracting)
  today. High discount rates imply low present value of distant future benefits.
- **Broader cases:** Cost-benefit analyses of climate change policy turn critically
  on the discount rate chosen. Economists who use higher discount rates (Nordhaus)
  find less justification for immediate costly action; those using near-zero discount
  rates (Stern) find much more. The disagreement is substantially about the appropriate
  present value of future benefits — not just about the physical science.
