# P100 — "The Rich" and "The Poor" Are Often the Same People at Different Ages

**Category:** Work & Pay
**Tags:** income, age, life-cycle, statistics, inequality
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 11 — Income and Wealth

## Principle
People's incomes typically follow a life-cycle pattern: low in early adulthood (education,
entry-level work), rising through prime working years, then falling in retirement. Because
of this, a snapshot of income distribution at any moment shows young workers and retirees
in lower income brackets and middle-aged workers in higher ones — even in a society with
perfect lifetime income equality. Treating the young poor and the elderly poor as a
persistent underclass identical to the bracket's composition confuses the age distribution
of income with persistent inequality between people.

## In plain English
A 22-year-old and a 55-year-old may both be in the "bottom 20%" in a given year for
completely different reasons. The 22-year-old is probably on their way up; the retiree
has already had their high-earning years. These are not the same phenomenon.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Income inequality statistics show that some people are permanently consigned to poverty
while others are permanently wealthy." Much of what appears as cross-sectional income
inequality is life-cycle variation — the same people at different ages — not permanent
stratification between different classes of people.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell points out that people in their forties and fifties tend
  to have higher incomes than people in their twenties not because they are different
  kinds of people but because they are the same kinds of people at different points
  in their careers. Cross-sectional inequality statistics do not adjust for this.
- **Broader cases:** Rising student debt and delayed career starts for millennials
  vs. higher incomes for baby boomers in their 50s is partly a life-cycle phenomenon.
  When researchers tracked the same cohort of Americans over decades, they found
  income trajectories consistent with life-cycle patterns rather than permanent class
  positions for most individuals.
