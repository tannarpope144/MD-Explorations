# P016 — Knowledge Coordination Through Prices

**Category:** Prices & Markets
**Tags:** knowledge, coordination, prices, Hayek, dispersed-information
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 2 — The Role of Prices

## Principle
No single person or institution possesses all the knowledge required to coordinate an
economy — knowledge of local conditions, individual preferences, and particular
circumstances is dispersed among millions of people. The price system aggregates
and transmits this dispersed knowledge in condensed form (a single number) without
requiring anyone to communicate it explicitly. Central planning fails partly because
it cannot replicate this function: planners cannot know what prices know.

## In plain English
Prices are a compressed, constantly updated summary of billions of pieces of local
knowledge that no central planner could ever collect or process. When you see a
price rise, you don't need to know why — the price tells you to adjust, and that's
enough.

## The fallacy it corrects
"A sufficiently sophisticated central planner with enough data could allocate resources
as well as markets." The knowledge problem is not computational — it is epistemological.
Much of the relevant knowledge is tacit, local, and constantly changing; it cannot be
extracted and fed into a planning system because it only exists in the minds of
actors in real time.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell (drawing on Hayek) uses the example of a tin shortage
  somewhere in the world: the price of tin rises globally, and without anyone knowing
  the cause, users of tin economize, producers of tin expand output, and substitutes
  are sought — all simultaneously, as if by a coordinating intelligence that does not exist.
- **Broader cases:** Soviet planners faced the socialist calculation problem directly:
  without genuine market prices, they could not determine the relative scarcity and
  value of millions of intermediate goods. The result was systematic misallocation —
  factories producing inputs not needed elsewhere while other inputs were chronically
  short — documented extensively in post-Soviet economic analyses.
