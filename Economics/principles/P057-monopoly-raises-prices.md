# P057 — Monopoly Raises Prices and Restricts Output

**Category:** Industry & Commerce
**Tags:** monopoly, prices, output, market-power
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 6 — The Economics of Big Business

## Principle
A monopolist, facing no effective competition, can raise prices above competitive
levels and restrict output below what a competitive market would produce — earning
above-normal profits at consumers' expense. The harm from monopoly is not just
the higher price paid by those who buy; it also includes the goods not produced
and trades not made because the monopoly price priced some buyers out of the market
(deadweight loss).

## In plain English
When there's no competition, the seller can charge more and produce less than is
socially optimal. Buyers pay more; some buyers don't buy at all; the economy produces
less than it could.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Monopolies are beneficial because they achieve scale and stability." Monopoly pricing
and restricted output represent a transfer from consumers to the monopolist plus an
outright loss of economic value. Scale benefits (if any) are separate from the harm
of market power and should be weighed against it.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell uses historical railroad monopolies in the American West
  as an example of how monopoly power — in the absence of competing rail lines or
  alternative transportation — enabled pricing well above competitive levels for
  farmers shipping grain to markets.
- **Broader cases:** OPEC's oil cartel during the 1970s demonstrated classical monopoly
  (or oligopoly) behavior: restricting output to raise prices. The result was a fourfold
  oil price increase in 1973 and a dramatic transfer of wealth from oil-importing
  countries to oil exporters, alongside significant global economic disruption.
