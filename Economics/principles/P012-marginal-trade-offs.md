# P012 — There Are No "Needs," Only Trade-offs at the Margin

**Category:** Prices & Markets
**Tags:** marginalism, trade-offs, needs, decision-making
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 2 — The Role of Prices

## Principle
People do not have absolute "needs" that must be met regardless of cost; they have
preferences that are continuously adjusted in response to costs and benefits at the
margin. Every decision involves incremental substitution: a little more of X in exchange
for a little less of Y. The concept of an inelastic "need" — requiring a fixed quantity
regardless of price — does not describe actual human behavior or economic reality.

## In plain English
No one actually needs a fixed amount of anything. Change the price and people adjust
how much they use, even for things labeled necessities. There is always a margin of
adjustment.

## The fallacy it corrects
"We can't cut spending on X — it's a need, not a want." Every budget constraint forces
trade-offs; describing something as a "need" is a rhetorical move to exempt it from
cost-benefit analysis. In practice, people and governments always substitute at the
margin when costs rise enough.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell points out that even water — the paradigmatic "necessity" —
  is used differently depending on price. Where water is cheap, people water lawns and
  wash cars; where it is expensive, they don't. The "need" adjusts to cost.
- **Broader cases:** After the 1973 oil embargo, American consumers reduced driving,
  bought smaller cars, and improved home insulation — despite oil being described as
  an absolute necessity of modern life. Gasoline consumption did fall in response
  to higher prices, demonstrating that the "need" was price-responsive.
