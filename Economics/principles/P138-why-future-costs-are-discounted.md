# P138 — Why Future Costs and Benefits Are Discounted in Decisions

**Category:** Time & Risk
**Tags:** discounting, decision-making, future, present-value, policy
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 12 — Investment, Gambling, and Insurance

## Principle
Rational decision-making discounts future costs and benefits relative to present
ones, not merely out of impatience, but because present resources can be invested
to produce more future resources. A cost borne today is larger in real terms than
the same nominal cost deferred, because deferral allows the resources to compound
elsewhere. Failing to discount — treating a dollar in 2080 as equivalent to a dollar
today — implies a zero opportunity cost of capital, which is unrealistic.

## In plain English
A benefit far in the future is worth less today than the same benefit received soon —
not because the future doesn't matter, but because money invested today will grow.
Treating future and present benefits as equally valuable ignores that present resources
can compound.

## The fallacy it corrects
"We should do anything necessary to prevent a harm fifty years from now, regardless
of cost today." Future harms must be expressed in present-value terms. Spending a
great deal today to prevent a modest future harm may be a poor use of resources if
those resources invested elsewhere would produce far more value that could then
address the harm — or any other problem — in the future.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell applies discounting arguments to environmental regulations
  and resource-conservation policies, noting that costs imposed on current generations
  must be weighed against benefits discounted back to the present to determine whether
  the policy passes a cost-benefit test.
- **Broader cases:** Cost-benefit analyses of long-lived infrastructure (dams, airports,
  transit systems) routinely use discount rates of 3–7% per year, reflecting the
  opportunity cost of capital. At 5%, a $1 benefit in 50 years is worth roughly $0.09
  today — meaning the upfront investment must be small relative to the nominal future
  benefit to be worth undertaking.
