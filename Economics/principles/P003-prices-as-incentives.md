# P003 — Prices as Incentives

**Category:** Prices & Markets
**Tags:** prices, incentives, supply, behavior
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 2 — The Role of Prices

## Principle
Prices do not merely report conditions — they motivate action. A higher price for a good
incentivizes producers to supply more of it and consumers to economize on it; a lower
price does the reverse. This incentive function is automatic and does not require
government direction or individual altruism.

## In plain English
High prices pull resources toward a shortage and push consumers to cut back — at the
same time, through no coordinating hand, which is why markets tend to correct imbalances.

## The fallacy it corrects
"If prices rise during a shortage, producers are just exploiting consumers." Rising prices
are also incentive signals that call forth additional supply, attract competitors, and
encourage conservation — effects that charitable distribution or price controls do not
produce.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell shows that after a natural disaster, higher prices for
  plywood, generators, and ice attract supply from distant regions. When politicians
  ban "price gouging," the incentive disappears and shelves remain empty.
- **Broader cases:** The 1970s U.S. natural-gas price controls kept prices below market,
  removing the incentive to explore for new gas. After deregulation in the 1980s, supply
  increased substantially. Similarly, agricultural price supports that held prices above
  market created incentives for farmers to overproduce, generating chronic surpluses
  that required additional government intervention.
