# P005 — Price Ceilings Cause Shortages

**Category:** Prices & Markets
**Tags:** price-controls, price-ceilings, shortages, supply-and-demand
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 3 — Price Controls

## Principle
A price ceiling set below the market-clearing price simultaneously increases quantity
demanded (more people want the good at the lower price) and decreases quantity supplied
(producers find it less profitable or unprofitable to produce). The resulting gap between
quantity demanded and quantity supplied is a shortage that persists as long as the
ceiling is in place.

## In plain English
Force the price of anything below what the market would set, and you will get more buyers
and fewer sellers — that gap is a shortage. The shortage is not a coincidence; it is the
direct, predictable result of the control.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Lower prices help consumers by making goods more affordable." A price ceiling helps
only those who can actually obtain the good. When supply shrinks and demand rises,
many consumers who want the good at the lower price cannot get it at all.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell documents U.S. wartime price controls during WWII producing
  shortages of meat, butter, and other controlled goods, requiring ration books to
  allocate what remained. The New York City housing shortage is directly tied to
  decades of rent ceilings.
- **Broader cases:** Venezuela's price controls on food staples (2003–2019) produced
  widespread shortages, empty supermarket shelves, and eventually malnutrition. Zimbabwe's
  price controls in the 2000s intensified food shortages during its hyperinflation crisis.
  Economists broadly agree that binding price ceilings reduce available supply.
