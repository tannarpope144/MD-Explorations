# P235 — Myths About Markets (e.g., "Trickle-Down" Is a Straw Man)

**Category:** Special Issues
**Tags:** trickle-down, markets, myths, straw-man, capitalism
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 18 — Special Economic Issues

## Principle
Several widely repeated claims about markets misrepresent what market economics
actually argues. "Trickle-down economics" — the claim that market advocates believe
benefits to the wealthy eventually trickle down to the poor — is a straw man:
no serious economist argues that wealth trickles down from the rich; the argument
is that policies enabling investment and productivity growth raise overall output
from which everyone benefits. Many other market "myths" similarly misrepresent the
mechanism being argued.

## In plain English
"Trickle-down economics" is a phrase used to criticize a position that no economist
actually holds. The real argument for market-oriented policies is that they create
wealth broadly — not that rich people's wealth drips down to others.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Market advocates believe benefits to the rich automatically trickle down to the poor."
No mainstream economist argues this mechanism. The argument is that investment,
innovation, and productivity growth (which require returns to capital) produce
economic expansion from which workers benefit through employment and higher wages —
a positive-sum outcome, not a trickle from a zero-sum pie.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell dedicates substantial attention to debunking "trickle-
  down" as a label for an actual economic theory, noting that he has never found
  the argument articulated in this form in any economics text or serious economic
  argument. It is a rhetorical device, not an economic position being contested.
- **Broader cases:** The growth of real incomes across the income distribution in
  the United States from the 1940s to the 1970s (a period of relatively high
  investment and productivity growth) benefited workers at all income levels —
  consistent with the "rising tide lifts all boats" mechanism, not with income
  trickling from the rich to the poor.
