# P095 — Unions: Gains to Members Can Come at the Expense of Non-Members

**Category:** Work & Pay
**Tags:** unions, labor-market, wages, non-members, outsiders
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 9 — Labor and Pay

## Principle
Unions raise wages for their members by restricting labor supply to unionized firms —
through closed shops, seniority rules, and collective bargaining. The workers excluded
from unionized jobs must seek employment in non-union sectors, increasing labor supply
there and reducing non-union wages. The union wage premium comes partly from a
redistribution from non-union workers and consumers (through higher product prices)
to union members — not purely from efficiency gains.

## In plain English
Union wage gains for members don't come from nowhere. Some of the gain comes from
excluding non-members from those jobs, pushing them into other work and lowering wages
there. The union raises its members' wages partly by reducing opportunities for others.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Unions raise wages for all workers, creating a more equal economy." Unions raise wages
for their members; evidence on the effect on overall wage inequality is mixed, and
the mechanism works partly through redistribution from non-members. Unions may reduce
inequality among union-sector workers while increasing inequality between union and
non-union workers.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell shows that union wage premiums in the U.S. were substantial
  during the peak of unionization (1950s–70s) and that non-union wages in competitive
  sectors were lower than they would have been if the displaced workers had not flooded
  non-union labor markets.
- **Broader cases:** The decline in union membership in the U.S. private sector (from
  around 35% in the mid-1950s to under 10% by the 2010s) has coincided with rising
  wage inequality within sectors — suggesting the wage compression unions produced for
  their members was not being extended more broadly.
