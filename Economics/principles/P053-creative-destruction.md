# P053 — Creative Destruction

**Category:** Industry & Commerce
**Tags:** creative-destruction, competition, innovation, business-failure
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 5 — The Rise and Fall of Businesses

## Principle
Economic progress requires the continuous displacement of less efficient producers,
technologies, and business models by more efficient ones. This process — "creative
destruction" — means that the rise of new industries and the decline of old ones are
two sides of the same coin. Protecting declining industries from failure preserves
their resource use at the expense of the new, more productive activities those
resources would otherwise support.

## In plain English
Every industry that dies makes room for one that is more productive. Saving old
industries from competition doesn't preserve wealth — it prevents the creation of more.

## The fallacy it corrects
"We must protect declining industries to preserve jobs." Jobs in protected declining
industries are maintained by preventing resources and workers from moving to higher-
value activities. The economy as a whole is made poorer by the protection, even though
the visible cost falls on the new entrants who never appear.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell traces how industries rise and fall — from horse-drawn
  transport displaced by automobiles, to typewriters displaced by computers — and how
  at each stage the "job destruction" was accompanied by the creation of far more jobs
  and wealth in the new industry.
- **Broader cases:** The decline of U.S. steel employment (roughly 500,000 workers in
  the 1950s to under 100,000 by the 2010s) was painful for affected workers but
  coincided with large gains in steel-using industries and in overall employment. Steel
  tariffs in 2002 saved an estimated 3,500 steel jobs while destroying roughly 12,000
  jobs in steel-consuming industries, according to contemporary analyses.
