# P233 — Third-Party Decision-Making vs. Decisions by Those Who Bear the Consequences

**Category:** Special Issues
**Tags:** third-party, incentives, accountability, moral-hazard, government
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 18 — Special Economic Issues

## Principle
Decisions made by people who bear the costs and benefits themselves are typically
better than decisions made by third parties who face different incentives. When
someone spends their own money on themselves, they consider both quality and cost.
When they spend someone else's money on themselves (insurance, subsidy), they over-
weight quality and ignore cost. When they spend their own money on someone else
(charity, gifts), they consider cost but may mis-assess the recipient's preferences.
When they spend someone else's money on someone else (government spending), neither
cost discipline nor recipient-preference alignment operates.

## In plain English
The best decisions are made by those who bear the consequences. When a third party
decides for you — with your money or theirs — they have different incentives than
you do, and the decisions tend to be worse.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Experts and government officials can make better decisions for people than people
can for themselves." Even if the third party has more technical knowledge, they
lack the information about individual preferences and they face weaker cost
discipline — two deficiencies that typically outweigh any expertise advantage.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell applies this analysis to healthcare (third-party payment
  through insurance removes cost discipline from the patient's decisions), education
  (government funding reduces the link between school quality and revenue), and
  foreign aid (governments spending taxpayer money on other countries' populations
  face neither cost discipline nor preference alignment).
- **Broader cases:** The explosion of U.S. healthcare costs since the expansion of
  third-party payment (insurance and Medicare/Medicaid) is consistent with reduced
  cost discipline at the point of service. Countries that introduced patient cost-
  sharing saw reduced healthcare utilization without commensurate health outcome
  deterioration, consistent with the third-party-payment theory.
