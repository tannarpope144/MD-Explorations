# P055 — Economies of Scale (and Diseconomies of Scale)

**Category:** Industry & Commerce
**Tags:** economies-of-scale, firm-size, costs, efficiency
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 6 — The Economics of Big Business

## Principle
As output expands, average costs per unit often fall — economies of scale — because
fixed costs are spread over more units and because larger-scale production enables
more efficient specialized inputs and processes. However, beyond some point,
coordination and management complexity cause average costs to rise — diseconomies
of scale. The optimal firm size is therefore not "as large as possible" but rather
the size at which average costs are minimized for the given industry's technology.

## In plain English
Bigger is cheaper, up to a point. Past that point, the complexity of running a huge
organization starts to cost more than the savings from scale — which is why giant
firms don't take over everything.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Bigger companies are always more efficient, so we should expect monopoly everywhere."
Diseconomies of scale limit how large efficient firms can grow in most industries.
Where economies of scale are exhausted at a relatively small share of the market,
many competing firms can coexist. Natural monopoly arises only where economies of
scale persist across the full range of market demand.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell notes that industries with large minimum efficient scale
  (steel mills, automobile assembly) naturally tend toward fewer, larger firms, while
  industries with small minimum efficient scale (restaurants, hair salons) support many
  small competitors. Neither structure requires explanation by reference to anything
  other than technology and costs.
- **Broader cases:** The failure of many large conglomerates assembled in the 1960s
  and 1970s (ITT, Gulf+Western) to outperform focused competitors reflects
  diseconomies of scale in coordination-intensive businesses. Conversely, container
  shipping achieved dramatic cost reductions as vessel size increased, enabling the
  globalization of trade.
