# P104 — Child Labor and Economic Development

**Category:** Work & Pay
**Tags:** child-labor, development, poverty, history, trade-offs
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 9 — Labor and Pay

## Principle
Child labor declines as economies develop and family incomes rise — not primarily
because of laws prohibiting it. When families are desperately poor, a child's
labor may be essential to survival; as incomes rise and education's returns become
clear, families voluntarily withdraw children from the labor force. Imposing child
labor prohibitions on poor economies before incomes have risen enough may push
children into worse situations (subsistence agriculture, illegal work, prostitution)
rather than into schools.

## In plain English
Child labor is a symptom of poverty, not its cause. It falls as countries get
richer. Outlawing it before the underlying poverty is addressed can make children's
lives worse, not better.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Child labor persists because of greedy employers and weak laws; strict prohibition
will end it." The demand for child labor and the family's willingness to supply it
are driven by economic necessity. Prohibition without rising incomes displaces
children to less visible and often worse alternatives.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell traces the history of child labor in now-wealthy countries
  (including Britain and the United States), where it declined sharply as industrialization
  and rising wages made adult earnings sufficient to support families and made
  education an affordable investment.
- **Broader cases:** A widely discussed 1990s case: when the U.S. Congress threatened
  Bangladesh garment factories with trade sanctions over child labor, factories
  dismissed thousands of child workers. Follow-up research found many ended up in
  worse conditions — including prostitution — rather than in school. Better outcomes
  followed programs that combined income support with school attendance requirements.
