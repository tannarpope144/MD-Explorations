# P238 — "Social Justice" Claims and Economic Trade-offs

**Category:** Special Issues
**Tags:** social-justice, trade-offs, redistribution, unintended-consequences
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 18 — Special Economic Issues

## Principle
Policies pursued under the banner of "social justice" — redistribution, price controls
for the poor, mandated benefits — are not exempt from economic trade-off analysis.
They have costs as well as benefits, and the costs often fall on the intended
beneficiaries or on uninvolved parties. Evaluating these policies requires the same
cost-benefit analysis, attention to unintended consequences, and distinction between
intentions and outcomes that applies to any other policy.

## In plain English
Good intentions don't exempt a policy from having bad consequences. A policy aimed
at helping the poor can hurt the poor if it produces the wrong incentives or wrong
outcomes. Labeling a policy "social justice" does not resolve the economic questions
it raises.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Opposing a social justice policy means you don't care about the poor." One can care
deeply about the poor while opposing policies that demonstrably make them worse off.
The relevant question is not what the policy intends but what it actually produces.
Many policies that harm low-income people are enacted and defended with the
explicit goal of helping them.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell applies this throughout the book: rent control
  (intended to help renters) reduces housing supply and harms renters long-term;
  minimum wages (intended to help low-skill workers) reduce employment for the
  least-skilled; anti-discrimination rules enforced through litigation (intended
  to help minorities) can make employers reluctant to hire members of groups that
  might sue.
- **Broader cases:** The Community Reinvestment Act and associated government
  pressure to extend mortgages to lower-income borrowers (intended to expand
  homeownership among the disadvantaged) contributed to the subprime mortgage
  crisis; when home prices fell, the intended beneficiaries suffered high rates
  of foreclosure — an outcome made worse by the very policy meant to help them.
