# P008 — Scarcity vs. Shortage

**Category:** Prices & Markets
**Tags:** scarcity, shortage, price-controls, definitions
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 3 — Price Controls

## Principle
Scarcity is the universal condition that wants exceed available resources — it is always
present for any good that is not a free good. A shortage is a specific market condition
in which the quantity demanded exceeds the quantity supplied at the prevailing price —
it is not inevitable, and it is typically caused by price controls preventing the price
from rising to clear the market.

## In plain English
Scarcity means there isn't enough for everyone who might want it at any price.
A shortage means there isn't enough at the current (controlled) price — raise the
price and the shortage disappears even though scarcity remains.

## The fallacy it corrects
"There is a shortage of housing / doctors / water — we need government to address the
scarcity." Confusing shortage with scarcity leads to the wrong remedy. Shortages are
typically created by keeping prices below market; removing the price control removes
the shortage even though the underlying scarcity of the resource is unchanged.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell explicitly distinguishes the two concepts: diamonds are
  scarce but not in short supply (you can buy one — at a price); gasoline was not
  suddenly scarcer during the 1973 Arab oil embargo, but price controls turned a
  manageable scarcity into visible shortages and lines.
- **Broader cases:** Water is scarce in the American West, but there is rarely a "water
  shortage" for urban residents who pay market-adjacent prices. When cities price water
  below cost (as many do), shortages and overuse result. The solution is not more water
  (physical scarcity) but prices that reflect the real cost (eliminating the induced shortage).
