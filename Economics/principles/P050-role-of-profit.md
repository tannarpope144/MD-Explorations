# P050 — The Role of Profit

**Category:** Industry & Commerce
**Tags:** profit, incentives, consumer-satisfaction, resource-allocation
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 4 — An Overview of Prices

## Principle
Profit is not a reward for mere ownership or for raising prices; it is the residual
earned by enterprises that produce goods and services consumers value more than the
resources consumed to produce them. The pursuit of profit therefore aligns producer
incentives with consumer satisfaction: firms that serve consumers well earn profit;
firms that do not, lose money and eventually exit.

## In plain English
Profit is the score that tells you whether you are creating more value than you are
consuming. It rewards serving customers well, not just existing.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Profit is money extracted from consumers and workers at society's expense." Profit
earned in competitive markets reflects value created, not value transferred. Suppressing
profit removes the incentive to serve consumers and the signal that directs resources
to their most valued uses.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell contrasts privately run enterprises that respond quickly
  to consumer preferences with nationalized industries that survive without profit and
  therefore have no imperative to satisfy customers. State-owned airlines and telephone
  companies in pre-deregulation eras are recurring examples.
- **Broader cases:** The privatization of British Telecom (1984) and British Airways
  (1987) was followed by improvements in service quality and efficiency as profit
  incentives replaced state subsidy. Similar patterns appeared across Eastern Europe
  after 1989, where privatized firms rapidly shifted product lines toward what consumers
  actually wanted.
