# P133 — Speculation Transfers and Reduces Risk; Speculators Tend to Stabilize Prices

**Category:** Time & Risk
**Tags:** speculation, risk, price-stabilization, futures, commodity-markets
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 12 — Investment, Gambling, and Insurance

## Principle
Speculators buy when they expect prices to rise and sell when they expect prices to
fall. If their expectations are on average correct, this activity smooths price
fluctuations over time — buying in periods of low prices (pushing them up) and selling
in periods of high prices (pushing them down). By absorbing risk that producers and
consumers do not want to bear, speculators enable these parties to hedge, reducing
uncertainty for productive enterprises. Speculators who are consistently wrong lose
money and exit the market; the survivors tend to be stabilizing.

## In plain English
Speculators do not create price swings — they trade against them. A speculator who
consistently buys cheap and sells dear smooths prices for everyone else. The ones
who get it wrong lose money and stop speculating.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Speculators cause price volatility and should be banned or taxed to stabilize markets."
Restricting speculation removes the parties most willing to absorb price risk, reducing
liquidity and hedging opportunities for producers and consumers. Markets with deep
speculative participation tend to have smaller price swings than those without.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell explains how grain futures markets allow farmers to lock
  in prices before harvest, transferring the price risk to speculators who are willing
  to bear it. The result is more stable incomes for farmers and more predictable
  food prices.
- **Broader cases:** After the 2008 financial crisis, commodity speculation was blamed
  for food price spikes. Subsequent research found that prices in commodities where
  speculation was heaviest (with deep futures markets) were not more volatile than
  commodities with thin futures markets, and some evidence suggested that futures
  markets moderated physical-market volatility.
