# P200 — Comparative Advantage and the Gains from Trade

**Category:** International Economy
**Tags:** comparative-advantage, trade, specialization, gains-from-trade
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 15 — International Trade

## Principle
Mutual gains from trade arise from comparative advantage — each party specializing
in what they produce at relatively lower opportunity cost and trading for the rest —
not from absolute advantage. Even if one country (or person) is better at producing
everything, both parties gain from trade as long as their relative costs differ.
The gains from trade are real and documented: trade expands total output beyond what
any participant could achieve in autarky.

## In plain English
Trade creates wealth because each side does what it does relatively best. Even if
one country is better at making everything, specializing in its comparative
advantage and trading makes both countries richer than if they tried to produce
everything themselves.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Trade only benefits us if we are competitive — if other countries can make things
more cheaply, free trade just destroys our industries." This confuses absolute with
comparative advantage. As long as relative costs differ (and they always do),
specialization and trade expand total output for both parties.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell uses Ricardo's classic example — Portugal and England
  trading wine and cloth — and updates it to modern manufacturing, showing that
  even countries with lower productivity in all sectors gain from specializing in
  their comparative-advantage industries.
- **Broader cases:** The dramatic growth of East Asian economies (South Korea,
  Taiwan, China) occurred alongside, and partly because of, deepening integration
  into global trade. Bangladesh's garment industry and Vietnam's electronics
  manufacturing both represent comparative advantage in labor-intensive production
  that, despite low wages by rich-country standards, raised real incomes substantially
  for their workforces.
