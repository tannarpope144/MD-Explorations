# P230 — The Seen and the Unseen (Opportunity Cost; Bastiat)

**Category:** Special Issues
**Tags:** seen-and-unseen, opportunity-cost, Bastiat, policy-analysis
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 18 — Special Economic Issues

## Principle
Every economic action produces both visible effects (the seen) and invisible effects —
the foregone alternatives (the unseen). Good economic reasoning requires accounting
for both. A policy that creates visible benefits for an identifiable group always
has costs that are harder to see: the taxpayers who pay for it, the industries
crowded out, the workers not hired elsewhere, the goods not produced. Focusing only
on the seen while ignoring the unseen produces systematically biased conclusions.

## In plain English
What you see when a policy works is the benefit. What you don't see is what would
have happened with those resources instead. Ignoring the unseen makes every
intervention look free.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Government programs that create visible jobs and benefits at low visible cost are
clear gains." Every government program has opportunity costs — the private activities
displaced, the taxes that reduced other spending, the debt that crowds out investment.
Counting only the seen effects and ignoring the unseen systematically overstates
the benefits of government intervention.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell draws directly on Bastiat's 1850 essay to frame much of
  his analysis: the broken window, the soldier whose military pay is visible while
  the civilian job he could have held is invisible, the subsidy to industry A that
  crowds out industry B.
- **Broader cases:** Cash-for-Clunkers (2009) destroyed functional cars (seen:
  new car sales, auto industry employment) while eliminating the supply of affordable
  used vehicles and destroying the value of vehicles that low-income buyers relied on
  (unseen: harm to used-car buyers, destruction of serviceable capital). Studies found
  the program mostly accelerated purchases that would have happened anyway, at high
  fiscal cost per car sold.
