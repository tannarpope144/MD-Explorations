# P169 — The Fallacy of Composition

**Category:** National Economy
**Tags:** fallacy-of-composition, macroeconomics, aggregation, paradox-of-thrift
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 13 — The National Economy

## Principle
What is true for an individual actor is not necessarily true when all actors do the
same thing simultaneously. This fallacy of composition appears repeatedly in economics:
an individual can save more by spending less, but if everyone tries to save more at
the same time, total spending falls, incomes fall, and people may end up saving less
(the paradox of thrift). An individual firm can sell more by cutting prices, but if
all firms cut prices, the result is deflation, not more real sales for anyone.

## In plain English
What works for one person can fail — or produce the opposite — when everyone does it
at once. Individual and aggregate behavior can diverge dramatically in economics,
which is why intuitions from personal experience can mislead about macroeconomic policy.

## The fallacy it corrects
"If one firm can compete by cutting wages, all firms can improve the economy by
cutting wages." When all firms cut wages simultaneously, purchasing power falls,
demand for output falls, and the firms are no better off in real terms. Policies
that work at the individual level can be counterproductive at the aggregate level.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell explains the paradox of thrift — Keynes's observation
  that individually rational saving during a recession can be collectively self-
  defeating — while also noting the limits of this reasoning when extended indefinitely.
- **Broader cases:** During the Great Depression, competitive wage cuts by firms —
  rational individually — contributed to the downward spiral of demand, incomes,
  and prices. Conversely, countries that tried to "beggar thy neighbor" through
  competitive currency devaluations in the 1930s reduced global trade without
  producing lasting competitive advantage for any individual country.
