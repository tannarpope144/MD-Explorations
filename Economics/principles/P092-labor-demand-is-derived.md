# P092 — Labor Demand Is a Derived Demand

**Category:** Work & Pay
**Tags:** derived-demand, labor-market, wages, productivity
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 9 — Labor and Pay

## Principle
Employers do not hire workers because they like having employees; they hire workers
because workers help produce goods and services that consumers want to buy. Labor
demand is therefore derived from (dependent on) the demand for output. When consumer
demand for a product falls, demand for the labor that makes it falls too. When
productivity rises, demand for labor rises because each worker now produces more
of what consumers want.

## In plain English
The demand for workers comes from the demand for what they make. More customers
wanting the product means more demand for the workers who produce it — the labor
market reflects the goods market.

## The fallacy it corrects
"We can increase employment and wages simply by mandating higher wages or more hiring."
Because labor demand is derived from output demand and productivity, employment and
wages can only be sustainably raised by raising productivity or output demand — not
by mandating prices or quantities in the labor market independently of these fundamentals.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell traces declining employment in industries like steel and
  textiles not to employer greed or mismanagement but to falling consumer demand
  for domestic output (displaced by cheaper foreign production) and to productivity
  changes that allowed the same output with fewer workers.
- **Broader cases:** The decline in manufacturing employment across high-income
  countries since the 1970s reflected both increased productivity (fewer workers needed
  per unit of output) and substitution toward lower-cost producers — both of which
  reduce derived demand for domestic manufacturing labor regardless of wage policy.
