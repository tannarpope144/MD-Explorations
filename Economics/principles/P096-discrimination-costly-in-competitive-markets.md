# P096 — Discrimination Is Costly to the Discriminator in Competitive Markets

**Category:** Work & Pay
**Tags:** discrimination, competition, labor-market, racism, gender
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 10 — Discrimination

## Principle
In competitive markets, an employer who refuses to hire qualified workers on the basis
of race, sex, or other non-productivity characteristics passes up workers available at
below-market wages — because discrimination by other employers reduces demand for
those workers. Non-discriminating competitors who hire them gain a cost advantage.
Over time, competitive pressure penalizes discriminating employers with higher labor
costs or with less productive workforces. Discrimination persists most easily where
competition is weakest.

## In plain English
Discrimination in hiring has a price: you forgo cheaper or better workers that your
non-discriminating competitors can hire. In competitive markets, those competitors
will beat you. Discrimination is most durable where competition cannot discipline it.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Market competition cannot eliminate discrimination — only law can." Markets do not
eliminate discrimination instantly, but they impose a cost on it and provide incentive
to reduce it. Legal barriers to discrimination reinforce market forces; they are not
the only mechanism, and where markets are most competitive, discrimination tends to
be least persistent.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell documents that Black baseball players, once allowed into
  Major League Baseball, were paid less than equivalent White players in the short run —
  but teams willing to hire Black players gained competitive advantage, accelerating
  integration. Similar patterns appeared in other competitive arenas when barriers to
  entry fell.
- **Broader cases:** The integration of the U.S. military and professional sports in
  the late 1940s and the subsequent narrowing of racial wage gaps in competitive labor
  markets (compared to government-regulated markets like some craft unions) provide
  evidence that competition reduces the sustainability of wage discrimination.
