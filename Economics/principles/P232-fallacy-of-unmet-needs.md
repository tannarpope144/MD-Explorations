# P232 — The Fallacy of "Unmet Needs"

**Category:** Special Issues
**Tags:** unmet-needs, scarcity, trade-offs, resources
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 18 — Special Economic Issues

## Principle
Claims that unmet needs justify additional spending or programs ignore that all uses
of resources compete with each other. In a world of scarcity, every resource used to
meet one need is unavailable to meet another. There is no resource pool that lies
idle, waiting to be directed to unmet needs; resources devoted to one cause reduce
what is available for others. "Unmet needs" rhetoric is a way of exempting a
preferred cause from cost-benefit analysis.

## In plain English
Everything competes for the same limited resources. Saying we must meet an "unmet
need" sounds compelling, but it always means taking resources from somewhere else.
The question is never whether a need exists — it is whether this use is the best use.

## The fallacy it corrects
"We can afford to meet unmet needs in X because we have the resources if we only
prioritize differently." Prioritizing X means deprioritizing Y. There is no costless
reallocation available; every additional dollar spent on one need is a dollar not
spent on another. Treating needs as exempt from this logic produces systematic
misallocation.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell applies this argument to healthcare, education, and
  social programs: the claim that unmet needs justify unlimited spending ignores the
  increasing costs of each additional unit of improvement (diminishing returns) and
  the value of the resources diverted from alternative uses.
- **Broader cases:** The expansion of U.S. federal healthcare spending from
  Medicare/Medicaid's introduction in 1965 to the present illustrates how "unmet
  needs" framing has consistently driven spending without a corresponding assessment
  of the value of resources consumed relative to alternatives. Each program is
  defended on the grounds that needs exist, while the cost in foregone alternatives
  is not comparably highlighted.
