# P097 — Discrimination Is Cheaper Under Regulation, Monopoly, or Government

**Category:** Work & Pay
**Tags:** discrimination, monopoly, regulation, government, labor-market
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 10 — Discrimination

## Principle
Where competition is suppressed — by monopoly, regulation, or government operation —
the cost of discrimination falls. A monopolist or regulated utility has profit margins
from which to absorb the cost of discriminatory practices without being undercut by
rivals. Government employers face no competitive profit discipline at all. Historically,
some of the most entrenched and durable discrimination occurred in regulated industries
and government employment, not in unregulated competitive markets.

## In plain English
Competition makes discrimination expensive; monopoly makes it affordable. When there's
no competitor to take your business, you can act on prejudice without losing out.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Market competition is the source of discrimination; government regulation is the cure."
In fact, protected industries and government employment have often been the sites of the
most durable discrimination, because the economic cost of discrimination is borne by
captive customers or taxpayers rather than by the discriminator's bottom line.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell documents that municipal streetcar companies in the American
  South opposed racial segregation laws because serving Black customers was profitable;
  it was the government-mandated segregation that imposed discrimination on profit-
  seeking enterprises. Similarly, many craft unions — insulated from competitive
  pressure by licensing and closed-shop arrangements — maintained racial exclusions
  far longer than competitive industries.
- **Broader cases:** Government employment in South Africa under apartheid enforced
  racial job reservation by law, not by market forces. The most rigidly segregated
  occupations in the U.S. through the mid-20th century were often those with
  government-granted monopoly status or strong union control.
