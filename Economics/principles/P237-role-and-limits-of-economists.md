# P237 — The Role and Limits of Economists

**Category:** Special Issues
**Tags:** economists, expertise, policy, limits, positive-normative
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 18 — Special Economic Issues

## Principle
Economists can analyze the consequences of different policies — who bears costs,
what trade-offs are involved, what historical evidence shows — but they cannot
determine which goals a society should pursue. That is a question of values, not
economics. Economists who present policy recommendations as if they followed
inevitably from economic science, rather than from a combination of evidence and
value judgments, overstate their expertise. The discipline is more useful as a
tool for clarifying trade-offs than for prescribing outcomes.

## In plain English
Economists can tell you what will happen if you do X — they can't tell you whether
X is what you should want. Policy decisions require value judgments that economics
cannot make; economists who pretend otherwise are doing politics, not science.

## The fallacy it corrects
"The economics profession agrees that we should do X, so policy should follow."
Economists agreeing on an empirical prediction is different from agreeing on a policy
recommendation, which smuggles in value judgments about whose interests matter and
how much. Consensus on effects does not imply consensus on desirability.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell distinguishes throughout *Basic Economics* between
  positive economics (what is) and normative economics (what should be), emphasizing
  that his own analysis is aimed at clarifying how economies actually work — not
  at prescribing outcomes. He is explicit that the same facts can lead different
  people with different values to different policy conclusions.
- **Broader cases:** The debate over income redistribution illustrates the limits of
  economic expertise: economists can analyze who pays, who benefits, and what the
  efficiency costs are; they cannot settle the underlying question of how much
  redistribution is desirable, which depends on value judgments about equity,
  liberty, and social obligation that are not resolvable by empirical analysis.
