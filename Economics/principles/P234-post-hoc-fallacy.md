# P234 — Post Hoc Fallacy in Economic Reasoning

**Category:** Special Issues
**Tags:** post-hoc, causation, correlation, economic-reasoning
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 18 — Special Economic Issues

## Principle
The post hoc ergo propter hoc fallacy ("after this, therefore because of this")
attributes causation to temporal sequence: if B follows A, A caused B. In economic
reasoning this is pervasive: "The economy grew after we enacted policy X, therefore
X caused growth." Economic outcomes have many causes operating simultaneously,
and coincidence in timing does not establish causality. Sound analysis requires
identifying counterfactuals — what would have happened without the policy.

## In plain English
The fact that something good happened after a policy was enacted doesn't mean the
policy caused it. The economy is too complex, and too many things change at once,
to attribute outcomes to whatever policy happened to precede them.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Policy X worked because the economy improved after we enacted it." The economy might
have improved anyway, improved despite the policy, or improved less than it would
have without the policy. Post hoc attribution of credit or blame to policies, without
a counterfactual, is economically uninformative.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell points out that New Deal programs are frequently credited
  with ending the Great Depression — but the U.S. unemployment rate remained near
  15–20% until WWII mobilization, a decade after the New Deal began. Whether the
  New Deal improved on the counterfactual of no policy response is a genuinely
  contested empirical question, not a settled fact.
- **Broader cases:** Minimum wage increases are often followed by periods of rising
  employment (because the general business cycle was rising), leading proponents to
  claim the increases caused the employment gains. Separating minimum wage effects
  from business cycle effects requires careful econometrics — the post hoc pattern
  alone is uninformative.
