# P164 — The Proper Functions of Government

**Category:** National Economy
**Tags:** government, property-rights, rule-of-law, public-goods, defense
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 14 — The Role of Government

## Principle
Market economies require a legal and institutional framework that only government
can provide: enforcement of contracts, protection of property rights, maintenance
of rule of law, national defense, and provision of genuine public goods (non-rival,
non-excludable). Without these, markets cannot function. But government activities
beyond this core often reduce rather than increase total wealth by distorting prices,
displacing private activity, creating perverse incentives, or imposing costs that
exceed the benefits produced.

## In plain English
Government is necessary for markets to work — you need enforceable contracts and
secure property rights. But government doing more doesn't necessarily mean a better
outcome; many interventions cost more than they produce.

## The fallacy it corrects
"More government activity means better outcomes; the question is only how much more."
Government has a necessary and bounded core function. Beyond that core, each
additional intervention must be evaluated on whether its benefits exceed its costs —
including the costs of unintended consequences and the distortion of market signals.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell contrasts the rapid economic development of economies
  with strong property rights and rule of law (post-WWII West Germany, East Asian
  tigers) with the stagnation of economies where property rights were insecure and
  contracts were not reliably enforced.
- **Broader cases:** Hernando de Soto's research on informal economies in Peru and
  other developing countries demonstrated that billions of dollars in assets held
  by the poor were "dead capital" — unable to be leveraged for credit or investment —
  because those assets lacked formal legal title. The absence of the government's
  core property-rights function was itself a cause of poverty.
