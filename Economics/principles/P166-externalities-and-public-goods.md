# P166 — Externalities and Public Goods

**Category:** National Economy
**Tags:** externalities, public-goods, market-failure, government, pollution
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 14 — The Role of Government

## Principle
Externalities are costs or benefits imposed on parties outside a market transaction.
Negative externalities (pollution) cause markets to overproduce the good; positive
externalities (basic research) cause underproduction. Public goods are non-rival
(one person's use doesn't reduce availability) and non-excludable (non-payers cannot
easily be excluded); private markets underprovide them because free-riding is possible.
Both represent genuine cases where market outcomes diverge from socially optimal ones —
but the case for government intervention must still weigh government failures against
market failures.

## In plain English
Markets work well when all costs and benefits fall on the transacting parties. When
a factory's smoke falls on non-consenting neighbors, or when a vaccine protects
people who didn't pay for it, markets under- or over-produce. These are real cases
for government action — but the cure must be compared to the disease.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Any market failure justifies government intervention." Even when market failures
exist, government intervention has its own failure modes (regulatory capture,
unintended consequences, information problems). The choice is between imperfect
markets and imperfect government — not between a flawed market and a perfect
government.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell accepts externalities as a genuine rationale for
  government action while warning against treating every market outcome one dislikes
  as an "externality" justifying intervention. He emphasizes that the concept is
  analytically precise and should not be stretched to cover general dissatisfaction
  with market results.
- **Broader cases:** Carbon taxes are the textbook externality correction: pricing
  the negative externality of CO₂ emissions into market decisions. Cap-and-trade
  systems in the EU and California demonstrate the mechanism in practice. However,
  regulatory approaches to the same problem (fuel economy mandates, appliance
  standards) often impose higher costs for the same environmental benefit, illustrating
  that the choice of instrument matters even when the underlying market failure is real.
