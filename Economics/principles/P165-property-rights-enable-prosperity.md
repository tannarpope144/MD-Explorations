# P165 — Property Rights Enable Prosperity

**Category:** National Economy
**Tags:** property-rights, prosperity, institutions, incentives
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 14 — The Role of Government

## Principle
Secure property rights — legal recognition and protection of individuals' right to
own, use, and transfer assets — are foundational to economic prosperity. They create
the incentive to invest, maintain, and improve assets (since the owner captures the
returns), enable capital markets (since assets can be collateralized), and facilitate
trade (since ownership transfers are enforceable). Societies with weak or insecure
property rights systematically underinvest and grow more slowly.

## In plain English
When you own something securely, you take care of it, invest in it, and are willing
to trade it. Without secure ownership, nobody invests — because the returns might
be taken from them. Property rights are the foundation that makes everything else
in a market economy possible.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Property rights primarily protect the wealthy at the expense of the poor." Strong
property rights are even more important to the poor, who cannot protect assets
through political connections or private security. In weak-property-rights regimes,
the powerful can appropriate assets from the powerless; formal property rights
constrain this.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell contrasts the economic trajectories of North and South
  Korea — starting from similar conditions — where the North's elimination of private
  property led to stagnation and famine, while the South's market-oriented system
  with property rights produced one of history's fastest sustained growth episodes.
- **Broader cases:** Daron Acemoglu and James Robinson's research (*Why Nations Fail*)
  documents systematically that institutional differences — especially property rights
  and rule of law — explain a large fraction of cross-country income differences.
  Countries that transitioned from extractive to inclusive institutions (Botswana,
  South Korea) showed corresponding improvements in prosperity.
