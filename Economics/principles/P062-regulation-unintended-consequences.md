# P062 — Regulation and Its Unintended Consequences

**Category:** Industry & Commerce
**Tags:** regulation, unintended-consequences, regulatory-capture, barriers-to-entry
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 8 — Regulation and Anti-Trust Law

## Principle
Government regulation of industry frequently produces outcomes contrary to its stated
purpose. Regulated industries often capture their regulators (regulatory capture),
using them to restrict entry and reduce competition. Compliance costs fall more heavily
on new entrants than on incumbents, raising barriers to entry and entrenching existing
firms. Regulations that reduce competition can raise consumer prices more than the
harm they were meant to prevent.

## In plain English
Regulations aimed at protecting the public often end up protecting existing businesses
from competition instead — with the public paying higher prices as a result.

## The fallacy it corrects
"More regulation always means better consumer protection." Regulation is an instrument
with side effects. When incumbents control the regulatory process, regulation becomes
a tool of market exclusion rather than consumer protection.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell discusses how the Interstate Commerce Commission,
  originally created to regulate railroads in the public interest, came to be
  dominated by the railroads themselves and used to maintain above-competitive
  rates. He also discusses occupational licensing as a mechanism incumbents use
  to restrict entry into their trades.
- **Broader cases:** The U.S. Civil Aeronautics Board (CAB), before airline
  deregulation in 1978, fixed airfares well above competitive levels and restricted
  route entry. After deregulation, fares fell substantially and passenger volumes rose
  dramatically. Occupational licensing in the United States has expanded from roughly
  5% of the workforce in the 1950s to around 25% by the 2010s, with studies showing
  it raises wages for practitioners while raising prices for consumers and reducing
  mobility.
