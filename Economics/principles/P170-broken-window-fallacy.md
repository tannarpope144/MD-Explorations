# P170 — The Broken-Window Fallacy

**Category:** National Economy
**Tags:** broken-window, opportunity-cost, seen-and-unseen, destruction
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 13 — The National Economy

## Principle
Destruction does not create net wealth. When a window is broken, the glazier's income
rises (the seen benefit), but the shopkeeper cannot spend that money on something else
(the unseen cost). The economy ends up with the same amount of money circulating but
one fewer window — net poorer by the value of the window. Any economic reasoning that
counts the visible spending generated by a disaster as a gain, without counting the
lost alternative uses of those resources, commits this error.

## In plain English
Breaking things to create work is not a path to prosperity — you are consuming
resources to restore what you had, not to create something new. The spending is
real; the gain is an illusion.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Disasters, wars, and destruction stimulate the economy by creating demand." Wars and
natural disasters can trigger visible spending and apparent economic activity, but
they consume resources that would otherwise have been used for something valuable.
The "stimulus" of rebuilding is the replacement of destroyed wealth, not its creation.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell uses Bastiat's broken-window parable (from *Ce qu'on
  voit et ce qu'on ne voit pas*, 1850) to illustrate the seen-versus-unseen principle
  that runs through much of economic reasoning. He applies it to arguments that wars
  ended the Great Depression (wars consumed resources, not just reallocated them).
- **Broader cases:** After Hurricane Katrina (2005) and the Japan tsunami (2011),
  some commentators cited the expected reconstruction spending as an economic
  stimulus. Economists widely rejected this framing: the "stimulus" represented
  replacement of destroyed capital, not net addition to productive capacity.
