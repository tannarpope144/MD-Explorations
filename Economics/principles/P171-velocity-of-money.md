# P171 — Velocity of Money

**Category:** National Economy
**Tags:** velocity, money-supply, inflation, monetary-policy
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 13 — The National Economy

## Principle
The velocity of money is the average number of times each unit of currency changes
hands in a given period. Total nominal spending equals the money supply multiplied
by velocity (MV = PQ). An increase in the money supply does not cause proportional
inflation if velocity simultaneously falls — as it did during the 2008–09 financial
crisis, when massive monetary expansion by the Federal Reserve did not produce
the inflation many feared, because velocity collapsed as banks and households held
more cash.

## In plain English
Printing more money doesn't always cause inflation — if people and banks hold on
to the money rather than spend it, velocity falls and the effect on prices is
dampened. Velocity determines how much economic activity any given amount of money
supports.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Any expansion of the money supply causes proportional inflation." The relationship
between money supply and prices runs through velocity, which is not constant. When
velocity falls (as in a liquidity trap or financial panic), large increases in the
money supply may have limited inflationary effect in the short run — though inflation
can emerge later if velocity recovers.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell explains the quantity theory of money (MV = PQ) and
  why the relationship between money and prices is not mechanical: changes in
  velocity can offset or amplify changes in the money supply.
- **Broader cases:** The Fed's quantitative easing programs (2008–2014) expanded
  the monetary base several-fold without producing corresponding inflation, because
  the excess reserves banks held largely did not enter circulation. When the Fed
  again expanded the money supply aggressively in 2020–21, velocity did not fall
  commensurately and inflation followed in 2021–22.
