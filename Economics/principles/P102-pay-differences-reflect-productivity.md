# P102 — Pay Differences Reflect Skill, Risk, and Productivity

**Category:** Work & Pay
**Tags:** wages, skill, productivity, risk, pay-gap
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 9 — Labor and Pay

## Principle
Observed wage differences between groups (by occupation, education, race, sex, or age)
largely reflect differences in the skills, experience, risk, and productivity those
workers bring to the labor market — not necessarily differences in employer treatment.
Comparing wages of workers in different occupations, with different education levels,
different hours worked, or different industries without controlling for these factors
produces misleading inferences about discrimination or exploitation.

## In plain English
A doctor earns more than a janitor not because one employer is fairer than another
but because medicine requires years of costly training and commands high market value.
Raw wage gaps between groups tell you nothing about fairness until you account for
what each group brings to the market.

## The fallacy it corrects
"A raw wage gap between Group A and Group B proves discrimination." Raw gaps almost
always reflect differences in the measured and unmeasured characteristics of workers,
not just employer behavior. Controlling for relevant factors typically reduces but
does not always eliminate measured gaps — the residual may reflect unmeasured
differences, discrimination, or both.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell shows that the male-female wage gap narrows substantially
  when controlling for hours worked, years of continuous employment, occupation choice,
  and industry. Never-married women without children have historically earned close to
  or above never-married men with similar characteristics, suggesting the raw gap
  reflects life-choice differences as much as discrimination.
- **Broader cases:** Asian-American incomes exceeding White-American median incomes
  in the United States cannot be explained by anti-discrimination law favoring
  Asian-Americans — it reflects differences in education attainment, field of study,
  and geographic concentration in high-wage regions. This demonstrates that group
  outcomes reflect group characteristics as well as societal treatment.
