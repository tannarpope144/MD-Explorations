# P064 — How Profit Allocates Capital Across the Economy

**Category:** Industry & Commerce
**Tags:** profit, capital-allocation, investment, resource-allocation
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 4 — An Overview of Prices

## Principle
In a market economy, capital flows toward its most profitable uses — which, in
competitive markets, correspond to its most valued uses. Industries earning above-
average returns attract new investment; industries earning below-average returns
see capital withdraw. This process continuously reallocates the economy's capital
stock toward activities consumers value most, without central direction.

## In plain English
Money chases profit, and profit follows consumers. Capital ends up where it can best
serve what people want to buy — not because anyone planned it that way, but because
that's where the returns are.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Capital markets are just casinos that serve financiers, not the real economy."
Capital markets are the mechanism by which savings are directed toward the highest-
value productive uses. Disrupting this allocation (through interest rate suppression,
capital controls, or directed lending) redirects capital from high-value to lower-
value uses.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell traces how new industries — automobiles, chemicals,
  electronics — attracted massive capital investment when they proved profitable,
  dramatically expanding output and employment, while older industries that became
  less profitable saw capital leave and shrink.
- **Broader cases:** Venture capital concentration in Silicon Valley from the 1980s
  onward reflected expected profit in software and internet businesses — and indeed
  produced enormous economic value. Conversely, directed lending in China's banking
  system toward state-favored industries regardless of profitability has generated
  large amounts of non-performing loans and misallocated capital, with economic costs
  documented by economists studying China's investment efficiency.
