# P137 — Present Value Caps Natural-Resource Extraction

**Category:** Time & Risk
**Tags:** natural-resources, present-value, reserves, scarcity, environment
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 12 — Investment, Gambling, and Insurance

## Principle
"Known reserves" of natural resources (oil, coal, minerals) are not a physical tally
of what exists but an economic construct: the quantity it is profitable to extract
at current prices and technology. When prices rise or technology improves, "known
reserves" grow — not because more was discovered but because previously uneconomic
deposits become economically viable. The present value of future resource revenues
also limits extraction: owners have incentive to conserve resources whose future
price is expected to rise, rather than extract them all now.

## In plain English
Running-out-of-oil alarms based on "known reserves" confuse an economic measure
with a physical one. Known reserves grow as prices and technology change. And owners
don't want to extract everything now if prices will be higher tomorrow.

## The fallacy it corrects
"At current production rates we will run out of oil/gas/copper in X years." This
mechanically divides current known reserves by current production rates — ignoring
that reserves are a price-sensitive, technology-sensitive concept, not a fixed
inventory. Predictions of imminent exhaustion based on this calculation have been
repeatedly wrong.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell notes that forecasts of imminent oil exhaustion have
  been made repeatedly throughout the 20th century, yet "known reserves" have generally
  grown as exploration, technology, and prices changed what was economically recoverable.
  U.S. natural gas reserves increased substantially after hydraulic fracturing made
  previously uneconomic deposits viable.
- **Broader cases:** The Club of Rome's 1972 *Limits to Growth* projections of resource
  exhaustion within decades proved wrong in virtually every specific prediction because
  the model treated fixed resource inventories rather than price-responsive economic
  reserves. Known copper reserves roughly doubled between 1950 and 2000 despite
  unprecedented consumption over that period.
