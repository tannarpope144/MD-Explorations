# P167 — The National Debt — What It Is and Is Not

**Category:** National Economy
**Tags:** national-debt, government-spending, deficits, fiscal-policy
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 14 — The Role of Government

## Principle
The national debt is the accumulated sum of past government deficits. Unlike household
debt, it is not entirely a burden on "future generations" in a simple sense — when
government borrows from its own citizens, the interest paid is also received by those
same citizens (a transfer within the economy). However, debt does impose real costs:
it crowds out private investment (by absorbing savings), and if ultimately financed
by money creation, generates inflation. Debt owed to foreign creditors does transfer
purchasing power abroad.

## In plain English
The national debt is not exactly like a family's credit card debt, but it is also
not "money we owe ourselves" in a costless sense. It crowds out investment and may
impose inflation costs. The real burden depends on how the debt is financed and
who holds it.

## The fallacy it corrects
"The national debt doesn't matter because we owe it to ourselves." This ignores that
government borrowing competes with private investment for available savings, raises
interest rates, and crowds out capital formation — reducing future productive capacity.
Debt held abroad does transfer real claims on future output to foreigners.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell distinguishes between the gross national debt and debt
  held by the public, and between debt denominated in domestic vs. foreign currency —
  noting that the commonly cited figures often conflate categories with very different
  economic implications.
- **Broader cases:** Japan's extremely high debt-to-GDP ratio (exceeding 200%) is
  held almost entirely domestically and has not produced the exchange-rate crisis
  that similarly high ratios in Greece or Argentina did — illustrating how the
  holder of the debt matters. Greece's debt crisis (2010–12) showed the costs of
  debt owed to foreign creditors in a currency the government could not print.
