# P209 — Multinational Corporations' Role

**Category:** International Economy
**Tags:** multinationals, foreign-investment, development, wages
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 16 — International Transfers of Wealth

## Principle
Multinational corporations are often depicted as exploiters of developing-country
labor and resources. In fact, multinationals typically pay wages above local market
rates, employ more advanced technology, and transfer skills and managerial practices
to host countries. Foreign direct investment by multinationals has been one of the
primary channels through which technology, capital, and managerial knowledge have
transferred from rich to poor countries, accelerating development.

## In plain English
Multinationals paying wages above local averages are usually the most attractive
employers in developing countries — that's why workers seek jobs there. The
"exploitation" narrative ignores that the alternative for workers is lower-paying
local employers, not rich-country wages.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Multinationals exploit developing-country workers by paying low wages." The relevant
comparison is not to rich-country wages but to local alternatives. Multinationals
paying twice the local average wage while providing training and stable employment
are not exploiting workers — they are competing for them.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell documents that multinational manufacturing in developing
  countries consistently pays above local average wages and provides better working
  conditions than local alternatives — making them sought-after employers, not
  exploitative ones.
- **Broader cases:** Nike's factories in Vietnam paid wages several times the local
  average and were among the most desirable employers in their regions. Studies of
  foreign direct investment in developing countries (Aitken & Harrison, 1999;
  subsequent literature) find positive wage and productivity spillovers to local firms
  over time. South Korea and Taiwan's development was heavily dependent on technology
  transfer from multinational investment.
