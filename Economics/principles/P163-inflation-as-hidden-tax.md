# P163 — Inflation: Too Much Money Chasing Too Few Goods; Inflation as a Hidden Tax

**Category:** National Economy
**Tags:** inflation, monetary-policy, hidden-tax, purchasing-power
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 13 — The National Economy

## Principle
Inflation — a sustained rise in the general price level — occurs when the money
supply grows faster than real output, so more money is chasing the same (or fewer)
goods. It effectively taxes holders of money and fixed-income assets by eroding their
purchasing power without any formal legislative act. Governments that print money to
finance spending are levying an inflation tax on the population.

## In plain English
When the government prints more money without producing more goods, each dollar
buys less. That hidden erosion of purchasing power is a tax — it transfers wealth
from holders of money and bonds to whoever spends the new money first (usually
the government).

## The fallacy it corrects
"A little inflation is harmless and stimulates the economy." Even modest inflation
imposes costs: it erodes savings, distorts price signals, creates uncertainty in
long-term contracts, and — when unpredictable — raises the risk premium on investment.
High or hyperinflation is economically devastating.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell details the Weimar Republic hyperinflation of 1921–23,
  where money printing to finance WWI reparations destroyed the currency, wiped out
  the middle class's savings, and contributed to the social instability that aided
  the rise of extremist politics.
- **Broader cases:** Zimbabwe's hyperinflation (2007–09) and Venezuela's hyperinflation
  (2016–present) are modern examples of money-financed deficits destroying currency
  value. In both cases, price controls were imposed alongside money printing — producing
  the shortages and black markets predicted by standard price-control analysis.
