# P099 — Income Brackets Are Not Fixed Groups — Income Mobility Over a Lifetime

**Category:** Work & Pay
**Tags:** income-mobility, income-brackets, statistics, lifetime-earnings
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 11 — Income and Wealth

## Principle
Statistical income quintiles and brackets are filled by different people at different
times. Studies that track the same individuals over time (longitudinal studies) find
substantial income mobility: most people who start in the bottom quintile move up
over their careers; many who are in the top quintile in one year were not there
a decade earlier. Snapshot comparisons of income brackets treat groups of people
as if they were permanent populations when they are in fact statistical constructs
that constantly change in composition.

## In plain English
The "bottom 20%" and "top 20%" are not the same people year after year. Most people
start in lower brackets and rise; the brackets move people through them over a
lifetime. Focusing on brackets as if they were permanent populations misleads about
who benefits and who is harmed by economic policies.

## The fallacy it corrects
"The rich are getting richer and the poor are getting poorer." This claim conflates
the trend in bracket averages with the fortunes of individuals. If the poor (as a
group defined at time t) move into higher brackets, the "poor" bracket at time t+10
may contain different, and newly poor, people — not the same people who were poor before.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell cites U.S. Treasury Department studies tracking
  individual taxpayers over years, finding that most individuals in the bottom income
  quintile in one decade had moved to higher quintiles a decade later, while the
  top quintile had substantial turnover as well.
- **Broader cases:** Research by economists including Raj Chetty and colleagues has
  documented significant intergenerational income mobility in the United States,
  though varying by region. Studies across OECD countries consistently show that
  income measured over a lifetime is substantially more equal than income measured
  in a single year, because people's earnings rise and fall through stages of
  education, career growth, and retirement.
