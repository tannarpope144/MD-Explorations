# P132 — Interest as the Price of Time and Credit

**Category:** Time & Risk
**Tags:** interest, credit, time-preference, price-of-time
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 12 — Investment, Gambling, and Insurance

## Principle
Interest is the price paid for obtaining purchasing power now rather than later. It
compensates lenders for deferring their own consumption and for bearing the risk of
non-repayment. Interest rates coordinate the supply of savings with the demand for
borrowed funds, directing capital to its most valued uses. Suppressing interest rates
below their market-clearing level (through usury laws or central bank policy) creates
a surplus of borrowers over lenders — a credit shortage — and misallocates capital
toward less productive uses.

## In plain English
Interest is the cost of borrowing time. If you want money now that you'd otherwise
have to wait for, you pay for the privilege. Cap that price and you get the same
shortages any other price cap produces.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Low or zero interest rates are good for the economy because they make borrowing
cheap." Artificially suppressed rates reduce the return to saving, discourage capital
formation, misallocate credit toward marginal investments, and can produce financial
instability when the mispricing is large and prolonged.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell discusses usury laws — government caps on interest rates —
  as a historical example of price controls on credit, producing credit rationing and
  driving lending into informal markets. The pattern mirrors other price ceilings.
- **Broader cases:** The Federal Reserve's near-zero interest rate policy (2008–2015
  and again 2020–2022) reduced returns to savers, encouraged risk-taking, and
  contributed to asset price inflation. When rates eventually rose, assets priced
  on the assumption of near-zero rates (long-duration bonds, speculative tech stocks)
  fell sharply — consistent with the distortions created by prices held artificially
  below market-clearing levels.
