# P206 — Outsourcing and Jobs

**Category:** International Economy
**Tags:** outsourcing, jobs, trade, comparative-advantage, labor
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 15 — International Trade

## Principle
When domestic firms outsource production (or service work) to lower-cost foreign
suppliers, they reduce costs and free resources for higher-value activities. Workers
displaced from outsourced activities must transition to other work, which may be
painful for individuals, but the economy-wide result is the same as from any
productivity improvement: more output from the same resources. Preventing outsourcing
to protect specific jobs imposes costs across the economy to benefit a concentrated
group, just as tariffs do.

## In plain English
Outsourcing does to services what imports do to goods: it lets a country buy something
more cheaply elsewhere and redirect its own resources to what it does better. The
job losses are visible and painful; the benefits — lower prices, freed resources for
higher-value work — are diffuse.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Outsourcing exports jobs and must be restricted to preserve employment." Outsourcing
is trade in services. The same logic that shows gains from trade in goods applies
here. Preventing outsourcing raises costs, reducing the competitiveness of domestic
industries and ultimately destroying more jobs than it saves.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell applies the standard comparative advantage and opportunity
  cost logic to service outsourcing, noting that the same rhetoric used against
  manufacturing imports in earlier decades appeared again, directed at service
  outsourcing — with the same flawed logic.
- **Broader cases:** U.S. tech companies outsourcing software development to India
  in the 1990s–2000s reduced costs, enabling them to invest more in product development
  and expand employment overall. Studies of firms that outsourced showed they
  subsequently grew faster and employed more domestic workers in higher-value tasks
  than comparable firms that did not outsource.
