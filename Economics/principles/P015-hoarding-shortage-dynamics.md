# P015 — Hoarding and Shortage Dynamics Under Controls

**Category:** Prices & Markets
**Tags:** hoarding, shortages, price-controls, expectations, behavior
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 3 — Price Controls

## Principle
When price controls create shortages and consumers expect them to persist or worsen,
rational individuals respond by purchasing more than their immediate needs — "hoarding"
— which intensifies and accelerates the very shortage they fear. This self-fulfilling
dynamic does not arise from greed or irrationality; it is a predictable response to
an environment where price cannot signal adequate supply.

## In plain English
If you can't trust the store to have it tomorrow (because price controls prevent
restocking at a profit), it is rational to buy extra today. That rational behavior
by many people empties shelves faster, confirming everyone's fears.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Shortages under price controls are caused by hoarders; the solution is anti-hoarding
laws." Hoarding is a symptom of the shortage, not its cause. Anti-hoarding enforcement
cannot resolve the underlying supply-demand gap created by the price control; it
merely shifts the allocation method.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** During WWII and the 1973–74 oil crisis, American consumers
  stocked up on gasoline and other controlled goods, contributing to lines and visible
  shortages. Ration-book systems were introduced partly to prevent this dynamic.
- **Broader cases:** Venezuela's price controls in the 2010s produced hoarding of
  basic goods alongside empty supermarket shelves. The Soviet Union periodically
  experienced "deficit panics" in which rumors of coming shortages triggered runs
  on controlled goods, clearing shelves and confirming the rumors.
