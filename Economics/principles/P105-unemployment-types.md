# P105 — Unemployment Types (Frictional, Structural)

**Category:** Work & Pay
**Tags:** unemployment, frictional, structural, labor-market, policy
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 9 — Labor and Pay

## Principle
Unemployment is not a single phenomenon. Frictional unemployment is the normal,
temporary unemployment that occurs as workers transition between jobs — searching
for better matches. It is inevitable in a dynamic economy and is not a sign of
economic failure. Structural unemployment occurs when workers' skills do not match
available jobs, often because of technological change or industry decline; it requires
retraining, relocation, or adjustment, not just stimulus. Cyclical unemployment
results from economy-wide downturns. Treating all unemployment as the same
misdiagnoses the problem and prescribes the wrong cure.

## In plain English
Not all unemployment is the same. Some is normal job-switching; some reflects skills
being obsolete; some reflects a recession. A policy designed to fix one kind may do
nothing for — or worsen — another kind.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Any unemployment above zero is a government failure that can be fixed with the
right policy." Frictional unemployment is a healthy feature of a flexible labor market;
structural unemployment requires supply-side adjustment, not demand stimulus; cyclical
unemployment responds to macroeconomic policy. Conflating them leads to policies that
address the wrong type.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell distinguishes the unemployment of someone between jobs
  by choice (frictional) from someone whose occupation has been made obsolete by
  technology (structural), showing that each requires a different response.
- **Broader cases:** The 2008 financial crisis initially created cyclical unemployment;
  however, in certain regions (the Rust Belt, mining communities), structural
  unemployment persisted long after the macroeconomic recovery because the industries
  that had employed those workers did not return. Demand stimulus helped with cyclical
  unemployment but could not resolve structural mismatches, which required workforce
  retraining and migration — supply-side adjustments.
