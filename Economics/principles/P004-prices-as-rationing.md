# P004 — Prices as a Rationing Mechanism

**Category:** Prices & Markets
**Tags:** prices, rationing, allocation, scarcity
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 2 — The Role of Prices

## Principle
Because wants exceed supply, every society must ration scarce goods — decide who gets
how much. Market prices perform this function automatically: only those willing and able
to pay the price receive the good. When prices are suppressed, rationing still occurs
but through other mechanisms: queues, connections, bureaucratic criteria, or force.

## In plain English
Something always rations scarce goods. When price isn't allowed to do it, waiting in
line, knowing the right people, or government paperwork does it instead — just less
efficiently and less visibly.

## The fallacy it corrects
"We can eliminate rationing by removing price controls." Rationing is inescapable wherever
scarcity exists. Price controls don't eliminate rationing; they replace price rationing
with non-price rationing, which typically disadvantages those with less time, less
information, or fewer connections.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Soviet-era bread queues rationed a product whose below-market price
  meant demand exceeded supply. The queue — consuming hours of time — was the real cost.
  Similarly, U.S. gasoline lines in 1973–74 were the visible result of price controls
  preventing the price mechanism from rationing a suddenly scarcer commodity.
- **Broader cases:** In countries with government-controlled healthcare prices, waiting
  lists serve as the rationing device. In Venezuela's price-controlled economy in the
  2010s, consumers spent hours in queues for basic goods, with politically connected
  individuals often obtaining them through other channels.
