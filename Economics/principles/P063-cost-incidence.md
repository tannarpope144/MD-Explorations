# P063 — Cost Incidence — Who Actually Bears a Cost or Tax

**Category:** Industry & Commerce
**Tags:** incidence, taxation, costs, prices, elasticity
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 8 — Regulation and Anti-Trust Law

## Principle
The legal assignment of a cost — who is required by law to pay a tax or fee — does not
determine who actually bears the economic burden. The true incidence depends on the
relative price-sensitivity (elasticity) of supply and demand. If demand is inelastic
relative to supply, consumers bear most of the burden; if supply is more inelastic,
producers do. Mandated costs on employers (payroll taxes, benefits requirements) are
often borne by employees through lower wages rather than by employers.

## In plain English
Whoever the government makes write the check doesn't necessarily end up poorer — they
adjust prices or wages to pass the cost along. Who actually bears the burden depends
on who has fewer alternatives.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Taxing corporations means corporations pay the tax." Corporations cannot bear costs —
only people can. Corporate taxes are ultimately paid by some combination of
shareholders (lower returns), workers (lower wages), and consumers (higher prices),
depending on competitive conditions.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell demonstrates that the employer's share of payroll taxes —
  nominally paid by the employer — tends to come out of wages: total labor compensation
  to the employer (wage + tax) is what the employer responds to, so the tax reduces
  the net wage workers receive. The legal incidence is on the employer; the economic
  incidence falls partly on workers.
- **Broader cases:** Studies of minimum wage increases have found that employers in
  competitive product markets pass some or all of the cost increase to consumers through
  higher prices (fast-food prices, for example). Import tariffs, nominally paid by
  importing companies, are largely passed through to domestic consumer prices.
