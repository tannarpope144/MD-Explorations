# P134 — Insurance as Risk Pooling

**Category:** Time & Risk
**Tags:** insurance, risk-pooling, risk, uncertainty
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 12 — Investment, Gambling, and Insurance

## Principle
Insurance works by pooling the risks of many individuals so that the predictable
(law of large numbers) aggregate loss can be shared among all members of the pool.
Each member pays a premium roughly equal to their expected loss plus administrative
costs; in return, they are insured against a potentially catastrophic individual
loss. The mechanism transforms an uncertain large loss into a certain small payment,
benefiting risk-averse individuals even when the expected value of the premium
equals or slightly exceeds the expected loss.

## In plain English
Insurance pools many people's small, certain payments against the uncertain,
potentially large losses of a few. It doesn't reduce total losses — it redistributes
them so no one faces a devastating individual loss alone.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Insurance is a bad deal because you pay in more than you statistically expect to
receive." For a risk-averse person, the certain small cost of the premium is worth
more than the expected (probability-weighted) benefit, because a catastrophic loss
not covered by insurance could be ruinous. The "negative expected value" calculation
ignores the value of certainty.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell explains why rational people buy insurance even when they
  know the insurer expects to profit — the premium is for certainty of protection
  against ruin, not for a probabilistic expected gain.
- **Broader cases:** The near-collapse of AIG in 2008, which had written enormous
  amounts of credit-default swap "insurance" without adequate reserves, illustrated
  what happens when the risk-pooling mechanism breaks down: the pool was not large
  or diversified enough to absorb a correlated (systemic) shock. Insurance works
  when risks are independent; it fails when risks are all correlated — as housing
  prices in 2008 turned out to be.
