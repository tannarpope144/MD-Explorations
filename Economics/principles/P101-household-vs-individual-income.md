# P101 — Household vs. Individual Income Statistics Mislead

**Category:** Work & Pay
**Tags:** income-statistics, household, individual, inequality, measurement
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 11 — Income and Wealth

## Principle
Household income statistics conflate changes in household size, household composition,
and individual earnings. Rising household income inequality can reflect the increasing
prevalence of single-person and single-parent households (lower income) alongside
dual-earner professional households (higher income) rather than any deterioration
in individual earning opportunities. Comparing household income over time without
controlling for household size and composition is misleading.

## In plain English
A "household" of one and a "household" of two wage-earners are counted the same way
in household income statistics, even though they represent fundamentally different
situations. Rising household inequality partly reflects more people living alone,
not just richer rich and poorer poor.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Household income inequality has risen, therefore individuals are worse off relative
to each other." Household income data cannot separate demographic changes (who lives
together) from genuine individual earnings changes. Individual earnings inequality
and per-capita income tell different stories than household income inequality.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell points out that much of the apparent rise in U.S. household
  income inequality in the late 20th century reflects the breakup of multi-person
  households into smaller units (divorce, delayed marriage, more single-person
  households), which mechanically produces more low-income households without any
  individual becoming poorer.
- **Broader cases:** Demographic data show that average U.S. household size fell from
  around 3.3 persons in 1960 to around 2.5 by the 2010s. This alone shifts the
  household income distribution without any change in individual earnings. Per-capita
  income and per-capita consumption statistics — which adjust for household size —
  show substantially different trends than raw household income comparisons.
