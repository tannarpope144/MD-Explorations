# P090 — Wages Reflect Productivity, Not Employer Generosity or Worker Need

**Category:** Work & Pay
**Tags:** wages, productivity, labor-market, compensation
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 9 — Labor and Pay

## Principle
In competitive labor markets, wages tend toward the value of what workers produce —
their marginal revenue product. Employers who pay less than this lose workers to
competitors; employers who pay more earn losses. Neither employer generosity nor
worker need drives wages in the long run: productivity and competition do. Higher
wages in some industries reflect higher worker productivity, not more virtuous employers.

## In plain English
Workers are paid what they produce, not what they need or what employers feel like
giving. If a worker produces more, competition among employers bids up their wage.
If they produce less than they are paid, the employer loses money and must adjust.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Workers' wages depend on how generous or ethical their employers are." This
misattributes wages to employer character rather than market forces. Employers who
"generously" pay above market rates lose to competitors who pay market rates; those
who pay below market lose workers to competitors who pay market rates.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell notes that Henry Ford's famous $5/day wage in 1914 —
  often cited as generosity — was actually driven by high employee turnover that was
  costing Ford more than the wage increase. Paying above the going rate was a
  productivity and retention decision, not philanthropy.
- **Broader cases:** The wage gap between high-skill and low-skill workers widened
  significantly in the U.S. from the 1970s onward — not because employers became
  less generous to the unskilled, but because technological change increased the
  relative productivity (and thus market wage) of skilled workers. The explanation
  lies in productivity differentials, not in employer attitudes.
