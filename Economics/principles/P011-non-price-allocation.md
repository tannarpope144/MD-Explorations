# P011 — Non-Price Allocation

**Category:** Prices & Markets
**Tags:** allocation, price-controls, discrimination, queuing, rationing
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 3 — Price Controls

## Principle
When prices are suppressed below market-clearing levels, the resulting excess demand
must be resolved by some other allocation mechanism. Alternatives include: first-come-
first-served queues, personal connections and favoritism, bureaucratic criteria, political
influence, or discrimination on non-economic grounds (race, social class, appearance).
Non-price rationing typically disadvantages those with less time, fewer connections,
or less political pull — often the poor and minorities that price controls purport to help.

## In plain English
When price can't allocate, something else does — and that something else is usually
less fair and less visible. Standing in line, knowing someone, or looking the "right way"
replaces ability to pay.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Removing price as an allocation mechanism makes distribution more equitable." Non-price
rationing does not eliminate favoritism — it replaces transparent (if unequal) price
allocation with opaque allocation that rewards those with time, connections, or social
characteristics unrelated to need.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell documents that rent control in New York allowed landlords
  to discriminate among applicants — since the price could not vary, landlords selected
  tenants based on race, appearance, or social class in ways the market would have
  penalized. Soviet goods were allocated partly through political connections
  ("blat") rather than price.
- **Broader cases:** In countries with price-controlled healthcare, access often
  correlates with proximity to urban centers, professional status, or personal
  connections with medical staff rather than need or ability to pay. NHS waiting lists
  historically varied by region and by the social characteristics of patients, not
  just by severity.
