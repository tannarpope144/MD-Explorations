# P202 — Tariffs and Quotas Raise Consumer Costs and "Save" Jobs at High Cost Per Job

**Category:** International Economy
**Tags:** tariffs, quotas, protectionism, consumer-cost, jobs
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 15 — International Trade

## Principle
Tariffs and import quotas protect domestic producers by raising the price of foreign
goods, but they do so by taxing domestic consumers. The cost per job "saved" is
typically many times the annual wage of the worker whose job is protected, making
trade protection an extremely expensive form of employment policy. Moreover, the
protected industry's higher costs ripple through the economy to industries that use
its output as an input.

## In plain English
Every job "saved" by a tariff costs consumers far more than the worker earns —
the protection tax is spread across all buyers of the product. The saved job is
visible; the cost spread across millions of consumers is invisible.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Tariffs protect jobs." Tariffs protect some jobs in the protected industry while
destroying jobs in import-using industries and export industries that face
retaliation, and while imposing a large tax on all domestic consumers. The net
employment effect is at best zero and often negative.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell cites analyses of U.S. trade protection showing the
  per-job cost of protecting industries like steel, sugar, and apparel ranges from
  tens of thousands to hundreds of thousands of dollars per job per year —
  multiples of the average wage in those industries.
- **Broader cases:** The 2002 Bush steel tariffs were estimated to cost roughly
  $400,000–$800,000 per steel job "saved" per year, while destroying more jobs in
  steel-using industries than were saved in steel itself. The 2018–19 U.S.-China
  trade war's tariffs were found in multiple studies to be borne primarily by U.S.
  consumers and importers, not by Chinese exporters.
