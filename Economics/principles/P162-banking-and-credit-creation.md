# P162 — Banking and Credit Creation

**Category:** National Economy
**Tags:** banking, credit, money-supply, fractional-reserve
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 13 — The National Economy

## Principle
Banks do not merely store deposits; they lend out most of what is deposited, keeping
only a fraction in reserve (fractional-reserve banking). This multiplies the money
supply: a new deposit of $100 supports multiple rounds of lending, creating more
money in circulation than the original deposit. The total money supply is thus
far larger than the monetary base, and it contracts sharply when banks fail or
tighten lending — amplifying economic downturns.

## In plain English
Banks create money by lending it out: your deposit becomes someone else's loan,
which becomes someone else's deposit, which becomes another loan. Money multiplies
through the banking system — and can contract just as fast when lending stops.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Banks are simple intermediaries that take in savings and lend them out one-for-one."
Banks actively expand the money supply through the credit-creation process. This makes
the banking system central to monetary conditions and a source of potential amplification
in both booms and busts.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell explains the mechanics of fractional-reserve banking
  and how bank runs, by forcing banks to call in loans or refuse new ones, cause
  the money supply to contract sharply — as happened across the U.S. in 1930–33,
  turning a recession into the Great Depression.
- **Broader cases:** The 2008 financial crisis featured a modern version of a bank
  run in the wholesale funding markets (repo and commercial paper), causing the
  effective money supply to contract as credit creation seized up. The Federal
  Reserve's response (quantitative easing) was partly designed to offset this
  contraction.
