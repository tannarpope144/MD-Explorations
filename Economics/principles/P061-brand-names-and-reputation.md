# P061 — Brand Names and Reputation as Information

**Category:** Industry & Commerce
**Tags:** brand, reputation, information, quality-assurance, consumer
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 7 — The Rise and Fall of Businesses

## Principle
Brand names and corporate reputations are mechanisms for conveying quality information
to consumers who cannot easily inspect products before purchase. By investing in a
reputation (advertising, consistent quality, guarantees), a seller makes a credible
commitment that creates consequences for poor quality — loss of the brand's value.
The brand premium consumers pay is compensation for this information and assurance.

## In plain English
A brand name is a promise backed by money already spent. If the seller breaks the
promise, that investment is lost — which is why brand-name sellers have strong
incentives to maintain quality and why consumers rationally pay more for them.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Brand-name products are identical to generics; consumers are just paying for
advertising." The brand premium also buys consistency, quality assurance, and
accountability that may be worth the price — especially for goods where quality
is hard to observe before purchase or where defects are costly.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell uses the example of fast-food chains: a traveler in an
  unfamiliar city can predict the quality of a McDonald's meal more reliably than
  that of an unknown local diner, which has value when the cost of a bad meal is
  high. The chain's brand is worth protecting, so it enforces standards.
- **Broader cases:** Contamination scandals that destroy brand value — melamine in
  Chinese infant formula (2008), Tylenol tampering (1982) — demonstrate how much
  capital is invested in a brand name and how quickly it is destroyed by quality
  failures. The Tylenol response (Johnson & Johnson's rapid recall) showed the brand
  protection incentive working as the theory predicts.
