# P010 — Quality Deterioration Under Price Controls

**Category:** Prices & Markets
**Tags:** price-controls, quality, unintended-consequences, housing
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 3 — Price Controls

## Principle
When a price ceiling prevents suppliers from recovering costs through price increases,
they adjust along other margins: reducing quality, cutting services, deferring maintenance,
or reducing quantity per unit. The nominal price stays low, but the real value received
by consumers falls as quality erodes — an indirect price increase that price controls
cannot prevent.

## In plain English
Cap the price and producers find other ways to cut costs — fewer features, worse service,
less maintenance. The number on the label stays the same; what you actually get shrinks.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Price controls protect consumers by keeping prices low." Controls fix the number but
not the value. A rent-controlled apartment that is not maintained, repainted, or repaired
is not "affordable housing" in any meaningful sense; it is a decaying asset at a
nominally low price.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell documents rent-controlled buildings in New York City that
  were abandoned or allowed to deteriorate dramatically because rental income was
  insufficient to cover maintenance and upkeep. Landlords in controlled markets reduce
  services (heat, hot water, repairs) rather than raise prices.
- **Broader cases:** Post-WWII rent controls in European cities (including Paris and
  London) were associated with severe deterioration of the rental housing stock and
  reduced new construction. In product markets, Soviet consumer goods became notorious
  for poor quality partly because enterprises could not price to reflect costs and had
  no competitive incentive to improve.
