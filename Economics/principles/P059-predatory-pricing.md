# P059 — Predatory Pricing — Theory vs. Evidence

**Category:** Industry & Commerce
**Tags:** predatory-pricing, monopoly, competition, antitrust
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 6 — The Economics of Big Business

## Principle
Predatory pricing — a firm selling below cost to drive out rivals, then raising prices
once the market is captured — is theoretically possible but economically difficult to
sustain. The predator must absorb losses during the predation period; rivals can
re-enter once prices rise; and the predator must have the financial staying power
to outlast its targets. Empirical evidence for successful long-run predatory pricing
strategies is sparse.

## In plain English
The theory that big firms slash prices to bankrupt rivals, then hike prices once
competition is gone, sounds plausible — but it requires the predator to lose more
money than the prey during the price war, and rivals can always return. In practice,
low prices are usually just... low prices.

## The fallacy it corrects
"When a big company cuts prices aggressively, it must be trying to destroy competition."
Low prices benefit consumers. Labeling competitive price-cutting as predatory and
restricting it "to protect competition" often protects inefficient competitors at
consumers' expense — the opposite of the stated goal.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell reviews cases like Standard Oil, historically accused of
  predatory pricing, and notes that Standard Oil's price cuts typically reflected
  genuine cost reductions from improved refining efficiency — consumers benefited —
  rather than a successful predation strategy that raised prices afterward.
- **Broader cases:** The antitrust case against A&P grocery stores in the 1940s accused
  the chain of selling too cheaply — using buying power to lower prices to consumers.
  Courts have generally become more skeptical of predatory pricing claims when the
  alleged predation benefits consumers, since below-cost pricing is hard to sustain and
  re-entry by rivals is usually feasible.
