# P001 — Scarcity and Trade-offs

**Category:** Prices & Markets
**Tags:** scarcity, trade-offs, opportunity-cost, foundations
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 1 — What is Economics?

## Principle
Resources — land, labor, capital, time — are limited relative to human wants. Every use
of a resource forecloses other uses; therefore every choice involves a trade-off. Economics
is the study of how societies allocate scarce resources among competing uses.

## In plain English
There is never enough of anything to fully satisfy everyone. Choosing one thing always
means giving up something else — that's the inescapable condition economics is built on.

## The fallacy it corrects
"We just need to allocate resources more fairly and there will be enough for everyone."
Scarcity is not a distribution problem to be solved by better intentions; it is the
baseline condition that makes all economic decisions necessary.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell opens *Basic Economics* by noting that the total cost of all
  goods and services Americans might want vastly exceeds what the economy can produce.
  Wartime rationing — where governments must explicitly allocate food, fuel, and materials
  — makes scarcity visible in a way peacetime markets normally obscure.
- **Broader cases:** Every centrally planned economy (Soviet Union, Maoist China, Cuba)
  that tried to abolish market allocation still faced scarcity; the result was queues,
  shortages, and black markets rather than abundance. The problem was not eliminated —
  only the mechanism for revealing it changed.
