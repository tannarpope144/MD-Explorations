# P060 — The Entrepreneur and the Middleman Add Value

**Category:** Industry & Commerce
**Tags:** middleman, entrepreneur, value-added, intermediaries
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 7 — The Rise and Fall of Businesses

## Principle
Middlemen and entrepreneurs are often vilified as parasites who add cost without
adding value. In fact, they add value by performing functions — matching buyers and
sellers, reducing transaction costs, holding inventory, absorbing risk, providing
information, and improving timing and location of goods — that would otherwise have
to be performed at higher cost by producers or consumers themselves. If a middleman
did not add value exceeding his cost, buyers and sellers would bypass him.

## In plain English
If a middleman didn't make the transaction cheaper or better in some way, no one
would use him. His profit is evidence that he is providing something worth paying for.

## The fallacy it corrects
"We could lower prices by cutting out the middleman." Sometimes true; more often the
middleman's margin compensates for services that would otherwise be performed at
higher cost elsewhere. Eliminating middlemen doesn't eliminate their functions —
it just redistributes who performs them.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell discusses how merchants in various historical periods
  were condemned as profiteers when they were actually performing the economically
  essential functions of transportation, storage, and market-making — bringing goods
  from where they were plentiful to where they were scarce.
- **Broader cases:** The rise and subsequent partial decline of car dealerships in the
  U.S. illustrates the middleman's role: they reduce search costs, provide financing,
  and absorb inventory risk. Online car sales platforms like Carvana tried to eliminate
  dealerships, but many consumers still valued the services (test drives, financing,
  trade-ins) that the "middleman" provided.
