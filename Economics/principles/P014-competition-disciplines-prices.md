# P014 — Competition Disciplines Prices

**Category:** Prices & Markets
**Tags:** competition, prices, market-discipline, monopoly
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 2 — The Role of Prices

## Principle
In competitive markets, the threat of losing customers to rivals limits any single
seller's ability to charge above-cost prices. Sellers who price too high lose business
to those who price lower; this competitive pressure continuously drives prices toward
cost and quality toward consumer preferences. Competition does not require many firms —
only the credible threat of entry or rivalry.

## In plain English
Competition is the mechanism that keeps prices honest. As long as customers can go
elsewhere and rivals can enter, no seller can sustainably exploit buyers.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Without regulation, businesses will charge whatever they want." In competitive markets
they cannot sustain prices well above costs because rivals undercut them. Regulation
is most needed where competition is absent — not as a substitute for competition
that could exist.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell shows how the U.S. airline industry, after deregulation
  in 1978, saw dramatically lower fares as competitive entry disciplined incumbent
  carriers. Prior to deregulation, regulated fares were well above competitive levels.
- **Broader cases:** The entry of generic pharmaceuticals after patent expiration
  routinely drives drug prices down by 80–90% as competition replaces monopoly.
  The transformation of long-distance telephone service after AT&T's breakup in 1984
  brought prices down substantially over the following decade.
