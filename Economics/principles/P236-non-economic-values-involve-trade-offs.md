# P236 — "Non-Economic" Values Still Involve Trade-offs

**Category:** Special Issues
**Tags:** non-economic-values, trade-offs, environment, culture, ethics
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 18 — Special Economic Issues

## Principle
Invoking "non-economic" values — environmental protection, cultural preservation,
social justice — does not exempt a decision from trade-off analysis. Even purely
ethical or aesthetic goals consume resources that have alternative uses. Pursuing
cleaner air at a certain cost means forgoing other things of value. Economic analysis
does not determine what values to pursue, but it does insist that all choices — even
those framed in non-economic terms — involve opportunity costs that must be honestly
acknowledged.

## In plain English
Saying "this isn't about economics, it's about values" doesn't make the trade-off
disappear. Resources are still scarce. Spending them on one value means not spending
them on another. Honest value decisions require acknowledging this.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Putting a price on the environment (or culture, or health) is morally wrong; we
should just protect these things regardless of cost." Even protecting something "priceless"
consumes resources with alternative uses. Refusing to quantify the trade-off doesn't
eliminate it — it just makes it easier to commit resources without accountability.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell applies this to environmental regulation: rules requiring
  zero-discharge or best-available-technology standards are implicitly trading large
  resources for additional (often marginal) pollution reduction. The economic question
  is not whether clean air matters but whether the last increment of cleanliness is
  worth what it costs.
- **Broader cases:** The Endangered Species Act has imposed significant costs on
  landowners and development to protect particular species. Cost-benefit analyses of
  specific listings (northern spotted owl, delta smelt) have found per-species costs
  running to hundreds of millions or billions of dollars — costs that are real whether
  or not they are acknowledged by the legislation.
