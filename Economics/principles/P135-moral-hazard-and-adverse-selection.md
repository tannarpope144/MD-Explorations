# P135 — Moral Hazard and Adverse Selection

**Category:** Time & Risk
**Tags:** moral-hazard, adverse-selection, insurance, information, risk
**Source:** Basic Economics (Sowell), Ch. 12 — Investment, Gambling, and Insurance

## Principle
Moral hazard occurs when insurance or guarantees reduce the incentive to avoid the
insured-against loss — because the insured no longer bears the full cost of risky
behavior. Adverse selection occurs when those who know they face higher risk
disproportionately seek insurance, driving up the pool's average risk and costs.
Both phenomena arise from asymmetric information (the insured knows more about
their risk than the insurer) and tend to make insurance markets less effective
if not addressed.

## In plain English
When you're insured against a loss, you take more risks than if you bore the cost
yourself — that's moral hazard. And people who know they are high-risk buy more
insurance, which drives up prices for everyone else — that's adverse selection.
Both undermine insurance unless insurers can adjust.

## The fallacy it corrects
"Government should guarantee all loans / insure all deposits / cover all health costs
to protect people from risk." Government guarantees create moral hazard on a large
scale: the insured party takes on risks they would otherwise avoid because the
downside is borne by taxpayers. The 2008 financial crisis illustrated this at
systemic scale.

## How it played out
- **Sowell's cases:** Sowell discusses how federal deposit insurance, while protecting
  depositors, reduces their incentive to scrutinize the riskiness of their banks —
  and reduces banks' incentive to manage risk conservatively, since depositors won't
  withdraw funds based on risk. This contributed to the S&L crisis of the 1980s,
  in which federally insured thrifts took on excessive risk.
- **Broader cases:** The subprime mortgage crisis of 2007–08 featured both moral
  hazard (originators who sold mortgages and bore no ongoing risk had little incentive
  to underwrite carefully) and adverse selection (borrowers with the highest default
  risk sought the most accommodating terms). Both phenomena were amplified by
  government guarantees of mortgage-backed securities.
