# Event Mapping

**Category:** Basic structured analytic technique
**Source:** ATP 2-33.4, *Intelligence Analysis*

## Definition

Event mapping uses a brainstorming diagram to represent scenarios in hypotheses linked around a central word or short phrase that represents the issue or problem to be analyzed.

## When to use

Use this technique when a nonlinear method is desired to generate, visualize, structure, and delineate the events in a scenario or hypotheses related to an intelligence issue or problem. Colors can represent key players in each scenario (economics, military, opposition groups, science, culture, internal and external political pressures). It is also easy to annotate indicators of change to use when forming collection plans.

The diagram, with connections between events on a radial layout, encourages a brainstorming approach. The large number of associations promotes creativity in generating new ideas and associations not previously considered. Mapping scenario hypotheses in radials around the issue — without the implied prioritization that comes from hierarchy or sequential arrangements — helps mitigate anchoring and other cognitive biases.

## General rules

- Start with a blank paper, or use re-adherable notes on a whiteboard.
- Think in terms of key words, phrases, or symbols that represent ideas and concepts.
- Write down ideas as they occur, wherever they fit.
- Do not judge or hold back.
- Develop in the directions the topics take you — not limited by how you are doing the map.
- As you expand the map, try to become more detailed.
- Use arrows or other visual aids to show the links between events in the scenario.

## Method

1. Place the word or symbol representing the issue or problem in the center of the paper or whiteboard. Take a minute to think about it before continuing.
2. Add symbols or words to represent possible actions and outcomes around the central issue.
3. Link the possible actions and outcomes to the central issue. If desired, use colors to indicate the major influence each link represents (e.g., green for economic, red for opposition groups, purple for military). Colors may also differentiate paths for ease of reference.
4. Continue working outward, building the scenario of events into branches and sub-branches for each hypothesis in greater detail.
5. Use emphasis such as underlining and stars to show importance or level of influence.
6. Do not allow yourself or the group to get stuck on one scenario. If ideas run out, move to another area or hypothesis.
7. When creativity wanes, stop and take a break. After an hour or so, return and review the map, making additions and changes as desired.
8. (Optional) Add a number to links or decision points in each hypothesis. On a separate sheet, write the evidence for each number that would disprove that link or decision being made. Use the lists to develop an integrated collection strategy for the issue.

## Tips

- **Think quickly.** Your brain works best in 5–7 minute bursts; capture that explosion of ideas as rapidly as possible.
- **Keep moving.** If ideas slow down, draw empty lines and watch your brain automatically find ideas to put on them. Stand up and use an easel pad or whiteboard to generate more energy.
- **Include distractions.** If you suddenly remember an errand, note it on the side of the map so you don't become fixated on it.
- **Write on links.** Put key words on lines to provide context to the link.
- **Print words.** Print rather than write in script — it is easier to read and remember. Lowercase is more visually distinctive (and easier to remember) than uppercase.

## Cautions

- Unconstrained event mapping can become overly detailed, lose focus, and include events and scenarios that lack relevance to the issue being studied.
