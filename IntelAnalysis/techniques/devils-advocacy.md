# Devil's Advocacy

**Category:** Emerging analytic technique — contrarian
**Source:** ATP 2-33.4, *Intelligence Analysis* (Appendix A)

## Definition

Devil's advocacy is a process for critiquing a proposed analytic assessment, judgment, plan, or decision — usually by a single analyst who was not previously involved in the deliberations that led to the proposed assessment. It challenges a single strongly held view by building the best possible case for an alternate explanation.

## When to use

Most effective when challenging an analytic consensus or a key assumption regarding a critically important intelligence question. On issues where mistakes cannot be afforded, it provides further confidence that the current analytic line will withstand close scrutiny. An individual analyst can assume the role if they have doubts about a widely held view; alternatively, a leader may designate an analyst to challenge prevailing wisdom to reaffirm the group's confidence.

The technique can highlight weaknesses or reaffirm confidence by:
- Explicitly challenging key assumptions to see if they hold under various circumstances.
- Identifying faulty logic or information that would undermine key analytic judgments.
- Presenting alternative hypotheses that could explain the current body of information.

**Possible outcomes:**
- Confirming that the current analytic line is sound.
- Determining that the argument remains strongest, but further analysis is needed in certain areas.
- Identifying serious flaws in logic or evidence that suggest the analytic line needs to change.

## Method

The devil's advocate builds the strongest possible case against the proposed assessment. There is no prescribed procedure, but at a minimum:

- Outline the main points and key assumptions, and characterize the evidence supporting the current view.
- Select one or more assumptions most susceptible to challenge.
- Review the data for questionable validity, possible deception, and gaps.
- Highlight evidence that supports an alternative hypothesis or contradicts current thinking.
- Present findings that demonstrate flawed assumptions, poor evidence, or possible deception.

If the review uncovers major flaws, consider drafting a separate contrarian paper/assessment. Any product must clearly outline the conventional wisdom and be explicitly identified as a devil's advocate project to avoid confusion with the accepted assessment.

## Tips — questions to keep in mind

- What analytic processes were used to create this assessment?
- What sources of uncertainty exist within it?
- What are the critical assumptions?
- What is the diagnosticity of the evidence provided/cited?
- Does any evidence appear anomalous — where does it deviate from the expected norm?
- Were there changes in the broad environment in which events are happening?
- Was an alternative decision model used? If so, where was it applied? (Perhaps the rationale behind a conclusion is unclear.)
- Availability of cultural expertise.
- Are there indicators of possible deception the assessment failed to address?
- Are there remaining information gaps hindering a decisive conclusion? Do those gaps still exist, or do we now have more relevant data?
