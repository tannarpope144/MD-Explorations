# Quality of Information Check

**Category:** Diagnostic analytic technique
**Source:** ATP 2-33.4, *Intelligence Analysis*

## Definition

The quality of information check evaluates the completeness and soundness of available information, both independently and in conjunction with sources.

## When to use

If a major analytic assessment is planned, analysts should — individually or collectively — review the quality of their information and refresh their understanding of the strengths and weaknesses of past reporting on which their analytic line rests. Without understanding the context and conditions under which critical information was provided, it is difficult to assess the information's validity and establish a confidence level in an assessment.

Conduct periodic reviews after the initial check to prevent assumptions or weak judgments from becoming accepted as fact over time.

Weighing the validity of sources is a key feature of critical thinking; confidence in analytic judgments ultimately rests on the accuracy and reliability of the information base, which analysts must judge continuously.

**Determining the quality of information independently of the source is crucial** so that neither unduly compromises nor supports the other. An excellent source can knowingly pass along third- or fourth-hand information of low quality, so keep the two reviews separate.

This check can:
- Provide the most important basis for determining confidence in assessments and judgments.
- Offer an opportunity to mitigate assimilation or confirmation bias based on the source.
- Help catch errors of interpretation.
- Identify intelligence gaps.
- Highlight areas of concern regarding denial and deception.
- Allow analysts to convey a clearer understanding of their confidence in various aspects of the problem to customers.

## Method

For the review to be fully effective, analysts need as much background on sources as possible. Understanding the circumstances in which reporting was obtained is often critical to assessing validity. At a minimum, analysts should:

- Review all sources of information for accuracy and identify any that are more critical or compelling.
- Determine if there is sufficient and/or strong corroboration between the information sources.
- Reexamine previously dismissed information in light of new facts or circumstances.
- Ensure any circular reporting is identified and properly flagged for other analysts; review analysis based on circular reporting to determine if that reporting was essential to the judgments made.
- Consider whether ambiguous information has been interpreted and qualified properly.
- Indicate a level of confidence they can place in sources likely to figure in future analytic assessments.

Consciously **avoid relating the source to the information until the quality of information check is complete.** If relating the source to the quality of the information alters the perception of the information, ensure you can articulate why. Develop and employ a spreadsheet to track the information and record confidence in its quality as a constant reminder of findings.

## Cautions

- Analysts can become susceptible to circular reporting and source-based bias when reviewing information quality.
- Critical information can occasionally be found in reports from sources judged to have low access or a poor record. Ignoring information based solely on its quality — independent of the source — could lead to unduly dismissing valuable insights.
- When one analyst works the same subject or area for extended periods, they may overlook the significance of incremental changes. The use of [indicators](indicators.md) can help mitigate this.
