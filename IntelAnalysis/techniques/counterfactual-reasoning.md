# Counterfactual Reasoning

**Category:** Emerging analytic technique — contrarian
**Source:** ATP 2-33.4, *Intelligence Analysis* (Appendix A)

## Definition

Counterfactual reasoning is an analytic method useful for discovering the relationships between possible events and their most plausible outcomes. It essentially combines two contrarian techniques:

- **[High impact/low probability analysis](high-impact-low-probability.md)** — discusses what could occur and the resultant consequences.
- **["What if" analysis](what-if-analysis.md)** — reframes the question, assuming the surprise event has occurred, then looks backward to identify key actions that, taken in time, might prevent it.

## When to use — purposes

- **Facilitate causal analysis.** Many scenarios rely on causal events as indicators; understanding the most significant causal forces leads to more relevant analysis.
- **Overcome deterministic biases.** Reduce hindsight bias by proactively determining potential futures as if they had already happened, determining how they might have occurred, and looking for indicators.
- **Incorporate creativity.** Avoid a lack of imagination or openness to other possibilities.
- **Ground strategic assessment.** Many strategies are grounded in counterfactual claims about alternate possibilities, their consequences, and the relationships between them.

## Method — four stages (zero through three)

### Stage Zero — Establish Event Context
Begins with determining the focal question driving the analysis (which contains or alludes to the issue's primary driver). Understand the context for the possible event, including the causal background, within the relationship between cause and effect. **Overriding objective: estimate the ten most critical causal forces influencing the topic at present.**
- Ask, "What affects the question?"
- Determine the focal question to drive analysis.
- Ensure the analyst understands the context for the possible event.
- Think in terms of the relationship between cause and effect.
- Determine approximately ten most critical causal forces.
- Determine causal history — what elements or drivers affect the question?

### Stage One — Establish Antecedent Scenario
Construct the antecedent scenario or "what if" backstory that sets the event in motion, thinking in terms of likely or required precursor events. **Purpose: identify two to three deviations from the original ten causal forces that could combine to bring about the event.**
- Ask, "How can the event occur?"
- Examine causal history; focus on a small number of causal forces and their potential deviations.
- Re-engage current trends and understand what change is required — which trends would have to decrease, continue, or increase.
- Consider alternative ways the event could occur (other antecedent scenarios).

### Stage Two — Determine Event Compatibility
Determine whether there is any ripple effect of the event upon the remaining causal forces (a dramatic shift). Such shifts may serve as a primary indicator or signpost of change dynamics that impact the probability of the event and potential after-event consequences. Think in terms of **intermediate states** (the period between the antecedent event and the possible consequences).
- Select intermediate states.
- Evaluate the secondary, tertiary, and further effects of the event on the causal forces not previously considered.
- Ask what new events/trends may emerge as a result, and their secondary and tertiary effects.

### Stage Three — Drawing Consequent Scenarios
Transition to the ultimate objective: draw potential or likely conclusions from the event by drafting several follow-on scenarios or aftermath events. **Purpose: explore three to five scenarios consistent with the antecedent scenario (and intermediate states), plus outcomes common to multiple scenarios.**
- Generate several scenarios from the possible consequences (recommend developing three possible scenarios or COAs).
- Determine possible consequences of each scenario.
- Determine commonalities between the consequences to identify possible trends.
