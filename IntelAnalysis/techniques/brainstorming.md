# Brainstorming

**Category:** Core Army analytic technique (also used as an imaginative emerging technique)
**Source:** ATP 2-33.4, *Intelligence Analysis*

## Definition

Brainstorming is a widely used technique for stimulating new thinking and can be applied to virtually all other structured analysis techniques as an aid to thinking. Analysts typically brainstorm at the beginning of a project to generate a range of hypotheses about their issue.

## When to use

By definition, brainstorming involves a group of analysts meeting to discuss a common challenge. A modest investment of time at the beginning or critical points of a project can leverage the group's diverse perspectives to help structure a problem and build on initial ideas.

An individual analyst can also brainstorm — producing a wide range of ideas without concern for others' egos, opinions, or objections — but lacks the benefit of others' perspectives and may struggle to overcome personal cognitive biases without a diverse group.

Brainstorming can:
- Maximize creativity, encourage stepping outside normal analytic mindsets, and suspend limited perspectives about the practicality of ideas.
- Spark new ideas, ensure a comprehensive examination of a problem, raise unknowns, and prevent premature consensus around a single hypothesis.
- Encourage more radical or "outside the box" thinking.

Applications include developing threat COAs, anticipating actions of various threats (state and non-state, organizations, groups, individuals) influencing the commander's area of interest, and developing information collection and targeting strategies. (Example: an analytical team in Afghanistan brainstorming observables and indicators for Taliban shadow government activities in Kabul province.)

## Method — two phases, eleven steps

Brainstorming should be a structured process: a **divergent thinking phase** to generate and collect ideas, followed by a **convergent phase** to group and organize ideas around key concepts.

**Simple rules:**
- Never censor an analyst's ideas, however unconventional they sound.
- Explore what prompted each thought — it may contain the seeds of an important connection between the topic and an unstated assumption.
- Allow sufficient time. It usually takes one hour to establish the "rules of the game," make the group comfortable, and exhaust conventional wisdom. Only then do truly creative ideas emerge.
- Involve at least one outsider — someone who does not share the same educational background, culture, technical knowledge, or mindset, but is familiar with the topic.

**Phase 1 — Divergent Thinking Phase**
1. Distribute re-adherable notes and pens/markers to all participants. Typically 10–12 people work best.
2. Pose the problem as a focal question. Display it in one sentence on a large easel or whiteboard.
3. Ask the group to write responses using key words that fit on the small note.
4. Stick all notes on a wall for everyone to see — treat all ideas equally.
5. When a pause follows the initial flow of ideas, the group is nearing the end of conventional thinking and new divergent ideas are likely. End the "collection stage" after two or three pauses.

**Phase 2 — Convergent Thinking Phase**
6. Ask participants to rearrange the notes according to commonalities or similar concepts. Talking is discouraged. Notes may be moved multiple times as they cluster; copying notes to include an idea in more than one group is allowed.
7. Select a word or phrase that characterizes each grouping/cluster once all notes are arranged.
8. Identify notes that do not fit easily — consider them isolated thoughts or the beginning of an idea deserving further attention.
9. Assess what the group accomplished: new ideas/concepts identified, or new areas requiring more work or further brainstorming.
10. Instruct each participant to select one or two areas deserving the most attention. Tabulate the votes.
11. Set the group's priorities based on the voting and decide on the next steps for analysis.

## Common pitfalls to avoid

- **Blocking:** the human inability to develop new ideas while holding old ideas in short-term memory. When people must wait for others to speak, they often judge, edit, or forget their own ideas. The larger the group, the more participants are blocked — yielding fewer ideas than the same number working independently.
- **Evaluation apprehension:** anxiety about being negatively (or not positively) evaluated — concern about how others perceive us.
- **Personality faceoff:** clashes among participants. Overbearing individuals dominate; passive ones hide; stubborn ones over-protect their ideas; fearful ones present only safe ideas.
