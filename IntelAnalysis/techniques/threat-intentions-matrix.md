# Threat Intentions Matrix

**Category:** Basic structured analytic technique
**Source:** ATP 2-33.4, *Intelligence Analysis*

## Definition

The threat intentions matrix is a technique used to efficiently analyze information from the threat's perspective. It is essential for the analyst to understand the motivation, goals, and objectives of the threat making the decision, and to assess the criteria in the matrix from the threat's viewpoint.

## When to use

Use to review the most likely and reasonable alternatives from the threat's point of view. When completed, the matrix helps mitigate bias while providing insight into the effect of each of the threat's decision-making criteria on their various options. It enables the analyst to develop clear indicators for each option under study, facilitating specific collection planning.

With the threat's decision-making criteria already established in the column headings, the analyst only needs to enter the alternatives or options being considered. Normally, the matrix can be completed in less than an hour. During input, new and potentially better options may become apparent, increasing the value of the technique.

## Method

Use the column headings entered on the matrix. Enter the information for **every** decision-making criterion in a column before moving to the next column — this approach mitigates bias.

1. Enter the decision options believed to be reasonable from the threat's viewpoint.
2. Fill in the **objectives** for each option from the threat's perspective.
3. Fill in the **benefits** column from the threat's viewpoint with the benefits of the threat's decision option.
4. Fill in the **risk** column from the threat's viewpoint with the risks associated with the threat's decision option.
5. Fill in the **implications** column, which transitions the analyst from the threat's point of view to the analyst's perspective. Enter the implications from the threat's viewpoint, then add a slash (/) followed by the implications from the analyst's viewpoint.
6. Enter the **indicators** from the analyst's viewpoint into the indications column. This provides a basis for generating collection to determine which option the threat selected, as early as possible.

## Tips

- Use the [weighted ranking](weighted-ranking.md) technique for more detailed insight if time allows.
- Color-code entries: red for those from the adversarial point of view, blue for those from the analyst's perspective.

## Cautions

- The criteria used in the matrix may be less extensive — and quite possibly less relevant — than criteria derived during [weighted ranking](weighted-ranking.md). As a result, the insights gained may be less than those obtained using other techniques.
