# Weighted Ranking

**Category:** Basic structured analytic technique
**Source:** ATP 2-33.4, *Intelligence Analysis*

## Definition

Weighted ranking is a technique used by an individual or group to gain confidence in the assessment of available alternatives by weighting criteria in importance from the decision-maker's point of view.

## When to use

Use whenever the topic is important enough to warrant the investment of time and there is a need for transparency in the reasoning used to derive the assessment. In intelligence analysis, each criterion must be selected and given a weighted importance from both the analytic and the threat decision-maker's perspective. The insight gained on how each criterion affects the final outcome allows for a clear, persuasive presentation and argumentation of the assessment.

Weighted ranking helps mitigate bias and mindset when the analyst faithfully follows the method and treats each step as equally important. It can be used by a group as long as a facilitator keeps the process on track; group discussion can enhance the validity of the criteria weighting by sharing insight into the threat decision-maker's purpose and point of view.

## Method

There are eight steps to accomplish a weighted ranking review of alternative options.

1. Take the alternatives, options, or hypotheses generated from another process and fill in the first column of a matrix under the heading "Options."
2. On a separate sheet, develop a comprehensive list of independent criteria the threat would likely use to determine which option to select. List one criterion per line. Consider the time, place, and objectives of the action being reviewed.
3. **Pair-rank the criteria.** Pair-ranking requires each item to be compared with every other item, selecting one over the other.
   - Compare the first criterion with the second. Place a mark (✓ or ✓✓) next to the more important of the two.
   - Compare the first criterion with the third; mark the more important. Once the first criterion has been ranked against all others, go to the second criterion and compare it with the third, marking the more important.
   - Rank the second criterion against the fourth, and so on, until it has been ranked against all remaining criteria. The second and succeeding criteria are not ranked against criteria above them on the list (that was already done).
   - Count the marks (votes) for each criterion and write the total to the right.
   - Review the totals and determine how many criteria to use in the weighted ranking matrix. Mark these with an asterisk. **Note:** More than five or six criteria rarely provide enough difference to be worth the time and expertise.
   - Continue ranking each criterion with those below it until the list is complete.
4. Divide the number of votes received by each selected criterion by the total number of votes for all selected criteria. (Example: if the total is 15 votes and the first criterion received 5, then 5 ÷ 15 = 33%; if the second received 4, then 4 ÷ 15 = 27% [rounded up from 26.7%]; and so on. Ensure the percentages total exactly 100% by rounding as required.)
5. Enter the criteria in the options matrix as column headings, starting with the second column (the first heading is "Options"). Include each criterion's percentage in its heading. Order is not critical, but entering criteria in descending order of percentage avoids confusion.
6. **Pair-rank the options** based on the first criterion, from the perspective of the threat decision-maker (same pair-ranking procedure as Step 3; the source text says "Step 4," an apparent error in the original).
   - Compare the first option with the second and determine which best meets the criterion.
   - Place a mark (✓ or X) in the box at the intersection for the best option for that criterion.
   - After pair-ranking all options for the first criterion, move to the second criterion (column) and repeat, until all criteria are used to pair-rank the options.
7. Count the marks (votes) in each square under the criteria and write the number in the square. Then multiply that number by the weight of the criterion (the percentage in the column heading). Write the product in the square as well.
8. Once all marked squares have been multiplied by the percentage for that criterion, add the products in each square across each option (row) and place the total in the final column for that row. This number can be larger than 1 (e.g., 2.58). **The row with the largest total is the most likely option.**

End the review by performing a sanity check of the results and reviewing the impact of the weighted criteria on the final result. This review should provide the insight needed to present the results clearly and persuasively.

## Tips

- Use a different color for each criterion and alternative during pair-ranking to make the choices transparent (easy to review or recreate).
- At a minimum, this technique provides the analyst insight into the interaction of the criteria from the threat decision-maker's perspective.

## Cautions

- Weighted ranking takes more time than other basic analytic techniques.
- Many analysts avoid it because it relies on mathematical computations.
