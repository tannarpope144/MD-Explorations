# Analysis of Competing Hypotheses (ACH)

**Category:** Emerging analytic technique — structured
**Source:** ATP 2-33.4, *Intelligence Analysis* (Appendix A)

## Definition

The Central Intelligence Agency developed Analysis of Competing Hypotheses (ACH) in the 1970s to evaluate multiple competing hypotheses and foster unbiased conclusions. The goal is to produce the best possible conclusion when analyzing uncertain data. ACH requires analysts to explicitly identify all reasonable alternatives and evaluate them against each other, rather than evaluating their plausibility one at a time.

It is an **eight-step procedure** grounded in cognitive psychology, decision analysis, and the scientific method. Because of its thoroughness, it is particularly appropriate for controversial issues where analysts want to leave an audit trail showing what they considered and how they arrived at their judgment.

> "Hypothesis" is used in its broadest sense — a potential explanation or conclusion to be tested by collecting and presenting evidence.

## When to use

When working difficult intelligence issues, analysts are choosing among several alternative hypotheses: which of several possible explanations is correct? Which of several possible outcomes is most likely? ACH still serves national-level analysts who must make judgments where there is a high risk of error, and is emerging as a tool effective at operational and tactical levels for complex problems such as those in stability operations.

ACH counters the **"satisficing"** strategy — selecting the first solution that seems satisfactory rather than exploring all possibilities for the best one. Most analysts intuitively pick the answer they suspect is most likely, then look for confirming evidence; if it seems to support that hypothesis, they look no further. The danger: much of that evidence may also be consistent with other, unexplored explanations.

## Method — eight steps

1. **Identify the possible hypotheses to be considered.** Use a group of analysts with different perspectives to brainstorm possibilities. People are generally poor at generating all possibilities — if the correct hypothesis is never generated, the correct answer is never reached.
2. **Make a list of significant evidence and arguments for and against each hypothesis.** Interpret these broadly — include all factors that impact judgments, not just concrete current reporting. Include your own assumptions or logical deductions about others' intentions, goals, or standard procedures, since these often drive the final judgment.
3. **Prepare a matrix** with hypotheses across the top and evidence down the side. Analyze the **diagnosticity** of the evidence — identify which items are most helpful in judging the relative likelihood of alternative hypotheses. *This is the most important step* and differs most from intuitive analysis; it is the step most likely to be overlooked or misunderstood. The matrix provides an overview of all significant components of the problem.
4. **Refine the matrix.** Reconsider the hypotheses and delete evidence/arguments with no diagnostic value. Exact wording of hypotheses is critical. Consider: Are there hypotheses to add or finer distinctions to make? If little/no evidence distinguishes two hypotheses, should they be combined?
5. **Draw tentative conclusions about the relative likelihood of each hypothesis.** Proceed by trying to **disprove** hypotheses rather than prove them. Work down the matrix (each hypothesis as a whole), letting hypotheses compete against each other.
6. **Analyze how sensitive your conclusion is to a few critical items of evidence.** Consider the consequences if that evidence were wrong, misleading, or subject to a different interpretation. Revisit the linchpin assumptions or items that significantly influence the outcome: Are there questionable underlying assumptions? Alternative interpretations? Could the evidence be incomplete and misleading?
7. **Report conclusions.** Discuss the relative likelihood of **all** hypotheses, not just the most likely one — decision-makers may need contingency or fallback plans if a less likely alternative turns out to be true.
8. **Identify milestones for future observation** that may indicate events are taking a different course than expected. Conclusions are always tentative. Specifying in advance what to watch for is useful for consumers following the situation and makes it harder to rationalize away developments that should modify your judgment.

## Three distinguishing elements

ACH differs from conventional intuitive analysis because:
- Analysis starts with a **full set of alternative possibilities**, not a single most-likely alternative seeking confirmation — ensuring fair treatment of all hypotheses.
- Analysis **identifies and emphasizes the few items of evidence or assumptions with the greatest diagnostic value.** In intuitive analysis, such key evidence is rarely considered explicitly and is often ignored.
- Analysis involves **seeking evidence to refute hypotheses.** The most probable hypothesis is usually the one with the *least evidence against it*, not the one with the most evidence for it.

## Example — Indian nuclear weapons testing, 1998

The intelligence community reported: "There was no indication the Indians would test in the near term." Such a conclusion fails to distinguish an *unproven* hypothesis from a *disproved* one — the absence of current testing does not disprove that India may test in the future.

Had ACH been used, one hypothesis would certainly have been that India was planning to test in the near term but would conceal preparations to avoid international pressure. Considering this would have required evaluating India's motive, opportunity, and means for concealment, and assessing whether U.S. intelligence could see through Indian denial and deception — heightening awareness of the possibility of successful deception.

**Principal lesson:** whenever tempted to write "there is no evidence that...," the analyst should ask — *if this hypothesis were true, could I realistically expect to see evidence of it?*
