# Indicators

**Category:** Diagnostic analytic technique
**Source:** ATP 2-33.4, *Intelligence Analysis* (technique from Ch 4; example tables from Appendix B)

## Definition

An **indicator**, in intelligence usage, is an item of information that reflects the intention or capability of an adversary to adopt or reject a course of action (JP 2-0). An indicator serves as positive or negative evidence of threat activity, or any characteristic of the area of operations (AO) that points toward threat vulnerabilities, the adoption or rejection of a particular activity, or that may influence the commander's selection of a COA. Indicators may result from previous actions or from the threat's failure to take action.

Identifying and monitoring indicators are fundamental tasks of intelligence analysis, as they are the principal means of avoiding surprise. They are often described as forward-looking or predictive indicators. Detection and confirmation of indicators enable analysts to answer priority intelligence requirements (PIRs).

## Key principles

- **Never act on a single indicator.** Integration of multiple indicators and other factors is essential before analysts can detect patterns and threat intentions. Reviewing high-value targets, the most likely enemy COA, and the enemy center of gravity leads analysts to true threat intentions and capabilities.
- **Indicators may be deceptive.** If an indicator is a ploy (part of a deception plan), conclusions based on it may be incorrect. A threat may attempt to create false or misleading patterns by providing false indicators. Discover deception by comparing indicators, intelligence, and combat information from all sources.
- An estimate is not certain — it is an opinion based on facts and the analysis of a particular situation.
- Indicators serve as a means of prediction across all levels: strategic, operational, and tactical.
- The most obvious indicators are not necessarily the best depiction of an enemy COA; obvious indicators may be elements of a deception plan. Do not search for indicators of an *expected* COA or expect a certain COA at all. Use indicators as a tip-off that something is occurring, then dig deeper into the why and what.

Analysts should:
- Connect a series of occurrences or alarms to point to a higher indicator.
- Use indicators to evaluate particular events or activities against probable threat COAs.
- Use indicators to determine what events or activities will likely occur for a threat force to follow a particular COA.

## The four steps

1. Develop indicators.
2. Weigh indicators.
3. Analyze indicators.
4. Use indicators.

### Step 1 — Develop Indicators

Start from the event, work backwards, and include as many indicators as possible. Unnecessary indicators can be removed later.

**Each indicator must meet five criteria:**

- **Observable and collectible.** There must be a reasonable expectation that, if present, the indicator will be observed and reported by a reliable source. To monitor change over time, it must be collectible over time.
- **Valid.** Clearly relevant to the end state being predicted or assessed, and inconsistent with all (or at least some) alternative explanations or outcomes. It must accurately measure the concept or phenomenon at issue.
- **Reliable.** Data collection must be consistent when comparable methods are used; observers must observe the same things. Reliability requires precise definition of indicators.
- **Stable.** Useful over time to allow comparisons and track events. Ideally observable early in the evolution of development so analysts and decision-makers have time to react.
- **Unique.** Measures only one thing and, in combination with others, points only to the phenomenon being studied. Valuable indicators are consistent with a specified scenario or hypothesis but inconsistent with all alternatives.

Test questions: Are the indicators mutually exclusive and comprehensive? Have a sufficient number of high-quality indicators been generated for each scenario? Can they help detect a planned attack or deter a possible enemy COA?

Indicator development for a PIR may include:
- **Threat characteristics** — particularly composition, disposition, tactics, training, sustainment, and combat effectiveness.
- **Electronic characteristics** — threat digital and analog communications links, systems, and associated units.
- **Personalities.**
- **The location** in the AO to expect a particular threat activity.

> **Note.** In stability tasks or DSCA missions, it may be more applicable to use queries such as who, what, when, where, why, how, and in what strength.

### Step 2 — Weigh Indicators

Analysts weigh indicators to help resolve uncertainty. Conflicting indicators result from:

- Deliberate deception.
- Poor mission execution.
- Temporary indecision (hesitation in making a decision).
- Transition between missions.
- Random activity.
- Incomplete or inaccurate information.
- Uncertainty or doubt of the indicator itself.

The G-2 staff develops a list of indicators and places a priority on each, establishing the relative weight of one indicator versus another. Despite weighting, it is dangerous to draw conclusions from a single indicator. Integrate each indicator with others and factors, then define patterns and establish threat intentions.

In combat, the analyst is usually confronted with conflicting indicators — an enemy will go to great efforts to deceive by portraying indications pointing to a COA they do not intend to adopt, and may use patterns associated with attack, defense, and delay simultaneously.

A thorough knowledge of the threat and of the operational environment is required: enemy organization, equipment, tactical doctrine, logistical methods, the enemy's probable knowledge of the friendly-controlled area, the personalities of enemy commanders, and the past performance of opposing units. One technique for determining enemy intent is to consider the **origin or source** of the indicator — why the enemy presents a certain pattern. Indicators stem from military logic, doctrinal training, organizational constraints, bureaucratic constraints, or the personality of the enemy commander.

The event matrix and information collection plan are important tools for documenting developing events. **Caution:** there can be a tendency to over-rely on them, and indicators can discourage analytical thinking because a broad view of the event seems readily apparent.

While focusing on indicators of military activity, do not ignore specific indicators that might not fit a military category. At the operational and strategic levels, consider the availability of resources such as funding, fuel, and a country's ability to sustain its military:

- **Fiscal resources** are one of the best indicators of an organization's or country's ability to support a limited or protracted military mission, or its willingness to overtly/covertly support another country's military or terrorist action. A significant commitment of fiscal resources strongly indicates serious intent, capability, and commitment. Fiscal independence also adds credibility to verbal threats and political/economic influence.
- **Other resources.** Committing a scarce resource may indicate intent — or it may project a false commitment to force another country toward a peaceful resolution involving concessions. Countries with scarce resources may employ a resource they have in abundance (personnel, training areas, raw minerals) to acquire scarce ones (technology, equipment, experts). If a threat makes a preparation that cannot be reversed by inexpensive, efficient means, it may unintentionally signal intent (e.g., an artillery brigade dumping more ammunition at its gun locations than it can transport in one lift suggests it plans to fire it or leave it in place).

### Step 3 — Analyze Indicators

All-source intelligence analysts:
- Examine collected information and intelligence.
- Ensure any information pertaining to indicators includes footnotes with event details.
- Look for the development of patterns, noting when one set of indicators is satisfied and identifying if another is not.
- Watch closely to detect deception operations.
- Recheck the validity of the indicators and verify that the staff did not overlook relevant information. If valid, report findings. The commander needs to know whether the threat is conducting an actual combat operation or a deception operation.
- Look for indicators based on the principle of **mass.** Deception operations are typically carried out as inexpensively as possible; indicators based on a major confirmed commitment of forces are most likely to reflect the true situation.

### Step 4 — Use Indicators

Indicators provide an objective baseline for tracking events, instilling rigor in the analytic process, and enhancing credibility of the final product. They:

- Help assess whether there are sufficient grounds to believe a specific action is taking place (descriptive indicators).
- Provide a systematic way to validate a hypothesis or substantiate an emerging viewpoint.
- Give early warning (a classic application is seeking early warning of an enemy attack or a foreign nuclear test).
- Are often paired with scenarios to identify which of several possible scenarios is developing.
- Can measure changes such as political instability or humanitarian crises.

Defining explicit criteria for tracking and judging events makes the analytic process more visible and available for scrutiny, enhancing credibility. Include an indicators list in the finished product to help decision-makers track future developments and build a more concrete case for the conclusions. The indicator list becomes the basis for directing collection and routing relevant information to all interested parties, and can serve as the foundation for the analyst's filing system.

## Appendix B — Indicator example tables

These tables are exemplary, **not all-inclusive**. They identify different types of indicators and applicable activities, designed as a starting point for more in-depth analysis. An indicator is an activity (or lack of activity) that confirms or denies the action or event specified in an intelligence requirement.

### Table B-1. Offensive Indicators

| Activity | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Massing of maneuver elements, armor, artillery, and logistic support. | May indicate the main effort by weakening areas of secondary importance. |
| Deployment of combat elements on a relatively narrow frontage (not forced by terrain). | May provide maximum combat power at the point of attack by reducing frontages. Likely threat decisive effort. |
| Massing of indirect fire support assets. | May indicate initiation of main effort. |
| Extensive artillery preparation of up to 50 minutes or longer. | Initiates preparation preceding an attack. |
| Dispersal of tanks and self-propelled artillery to forward units. | Can indicate formation of combined arms assault formations with tanks accompanying leading maneuver elements and artillery following in bounds. |
| Surface-to-surface missile units located forward. | Provides depth to offensive tasks; places friendly support/unassigned areas in range. Employed alone, may indicate harassing or special weapons (chemical) delivery. |
| Antiaircraft artillery and mobile surface-to-surface missiles well forward with maneuver elements. | Provides increased protection to massed forces before attack; extends air defense umbrella forward as units advance. |
| Demonstrations and feints. | May precede an attack; may deceive actual point of attack. |
| Establishment and strengthening of counterreconnaissance screen. | Protects assembly areas and forces preparing for attack; may prevent friendly forces from seeing attack preparations. |
| Concentration of mass toward one or both flanks within the forward area. | May indicate intent for single or double envelopment, particularly if massing units are armor heavy. |
| Increased patrolling or ground reconnaissance. | May indicate efforts to gather detailed intelligence on friendly dispositions prior to attack. |
| Command posts located well forward; mobile command posts identified. | Indicates preparation to command an offensive task from as far forward as possible. |
| Movement of noncombatants from the AO. | Indicates preparation for rapid forward advance of troops and follow-on forces. |
| Extensive drills and rehearsals in unassigned areas. | Often indicates major attacks, particularly against fortified positions or strongly defended barriers requiring rehearsal of specialized tactics. |
| Cessation of drills and rehearsals. | Rehearsals complete; the unit is preparing for offensive tasks. |
| Increased activity in supply, maintenance, and motor transport areas. | May indicate movement of additional forces to the front; stocking of sustainment items (ammunition, medical) before an attack. |
| Increased aerial reconnaissance (including unmanned aircraft systems). | Threat effort to collect intelligence on friendly dispositions or defensive positions. |
| Establishment of forward arming/refueling points, auxiliary airfields, or activation of inactive airfields. | Preparation for increased sorties and faster turnaround; supports offensive tasks with aircraft as far forward as possible. |
| Clearing lanes through own obstacles. | Facilitates forward movement and grouping of assault units, particularly at night; usually immediately precedes an attack. |
| Reconnaissance, marking, and destruction of defending force's obstacles. | Indicates where assaults will occur. |
| Gap-crossing equipment (swimming vehicles, bridging, ferries, assault boats) in forward areas (given a large water obstacle/gap). | Expect a substantial effort to cross a water obstacle during a main attack. |
| Staging of airborne, air assault, or special forces with transport aircraft/helicopters. | Likely efforts to attack friendly command, communications, or sustainment nodes; may indicate a main effort linking airborne with ground maneuver forces. |
| Increased signals traffic or radio silence. | May indicate intent to conduct offensive tasks; increased traffic may be deception. Radio silence denies SIGINT-derived information. |
| Signals intelligence and electronic warfare assets located forward. | Provides electronic attack and surveillance support for the attack. |

### Table B-2. Defensive Indicators

| Activity | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Preparation of battalion/company defensive areas with company and platoon strong points. | Intent for holding terrain with defense in-depth, normally supported by armored counterattack forces. |
| Extensive preparation of field fortifications, obstacles, and minefields. | Indicates strong positional defense. |
| Attachment of additional antitank assets to frontline positions. | Intent to contest friendly armor in forward positions; attrite and channel friendly armor into engagement areas for armor counterattack forces. |
| Formation of antitank strong points in depth along avenues of approach. | May allow penetration of friendly armor into engagement areas; engages armor in depth. |
| Preparation of alternate artillery positions. | Increases artillery survivability; indicates great effort to support the main defensive area — no withdrawal of maneuver forces unless defeated. |
| Concentration of armor units in assembly areas in the rear of the main defensive area. | Holding armor in reserve for possible counterattack or counteroffensive tasks. |
| Presence of concentrated antitank reserves. | Quick reaction capability against armor penetrations. |
| Displacement of sustainment and medical units toward the rear area. | Facilitates defensive repositioning, maneuver, and counterattacks. |
| Pre-stocking of ammunition, supplies, engineer/pioneer equipment in forward positions. | Reduces sustainment burden during battle; reduces interdiction vulnerability; ensures strong points survive if bypassed or cut off. |
| Increased depth (from forward line of troops) of artillery and surface-to-surface missile units. | Allows continued artillery employment during maneuver defense without significant rearward displacement. |
| Increased use of land-line communications, often with decreased radio traffic. | Implies intent to remain in position — landlines are less vulnerable to electronic warfare and more secure. |
| Presence of dummy positions, command posts, and weapons. | Complicates friendly targeting/analysis; deceives the attacking force regarding actual defensive positions and strength. |
| Air defense more concentrated in one particular area. | Indicates location of numerous high-value targets (armor, sustainment, artillery, command posts). |

### Table B-3. Delaying Indicators

| Activity | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Withdrawal from defensive positions before becoming heavily engaged. | Delaying action to avoid decisive engagements. |
| Numerous local counterattacks with limited objectives; broken off before position is restored. | Assists disengaging units in contact rather than an attack to restore position. |
| Units bounding rearward to new positions while another force continues to engage. | Local withdrawals to new positions; preserves the force and trades space for time. |
| Maximum firepower forward, firing initiated at long ranges. | Inflict casualties to slow the advance and provide volume of fire to avoid decisive engagements; allows time to disengage and reposition. |
| Extremely large unit frontages compared to usual defensive positions. | Delaying action to economize force, allowing larger formations to withdraw. |
| Chemical/biological weapons in forward areas; reports of threat in chemical protective clothing handling munitions. | Possible chemical munitions use; contaminated areas cause significant delays to attacking forces. |
| Identification of dummy positions and minefields. | Economy of force; causes the advancing force to determine if mines are live or inert. |

### Table B-4. Withdrawal Indicators

| Activity | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Systematic destruction of bridges, communication facilities, and other assets. | Denies the advancing force the use of infrastructure and installations in withdrawal areas. |
| Establishment of a covering force or rear guard. | Covers withdrawal of the main body; usually only the rear guard engages attacking forces. |
| Increased rearward movement at night, particularly during inclement weather. | Avoid contact to preserve the force and combat power. |
| Minimal presence of sustainment and medical units. | Withdrawal of nonessential assets; may also indicate inability to move depots and dumps. |
| Establishing and marking withdrawal routes and traffic control points. | Facilitates rapid rearward movement; organized and rapid withdrawal to preserve the force. |
| Preparation of new defensive positions beyond supporting range of present positions. | Establishing new positions along suitable terrain before deliberately withdrawn forces arrive. |
| Increased engineer activity and stockpiling of explosives near bridges, tunnels, or built-up areas in the rear. | Mobility operations facilitate withdrawal; demolition preparation indicates likely destruction of infrastructure in front of the attacking force. |
| Rearward movement of long-range artillery. | Positions long-range artillery in subsequent defensive positions to support withdrawal with indirect fire. |
| Activation of command posts well removed from the present battle area; command posts along the withdrawal route. | Establishes command nodes in the new position and along the march route to control movement and arrival of forces. |

### Table B-5. Population Indicators

**Indicators of Aggressive Behavior Within the Population:** Identification of agitators, insurgents, militias, or criminal organizations and their supporters/sympathizers who suddenly appear or move. New faces in rural communities. Unusual gatherings. Disruption of normal social patterns. Mass migration (urban↔rural). Massing of combatants of competing power groups. Increases in embassy/consulate staffs from countries supporting disaffected groups, especially those hostile to the U.S. Lack of children playing outside. Increased travel by suspected subversives/leaders to hostile countries. Influx of opposition, resident, and expatriate leaders into the AO. Reports of disaffected population receiving military training abroad. Increase of visitors (tourists, technicians, business persons, religious leaders, officials) from hostile groups/countries. Close connections between diplomatic personnel of hostile countries and local opposition. Communications between opposition groups and external supporters. Increase in disaffected youth gatherings (student protests/demonstrations). Establishment of organizations of unexplained origin with nebulous aims, or new organizations replacing existing structures with identical aims. Many new members in existing organizations (e.g., labor unions). Infiltration of student organizations by known agitators. New organizations stressing grievances of repressed/minority groups. Reports of large donations to new/revamped organizations. Reports of payment to locals for subversive/hostile activities. Reports of opposition paramilitary/militia formation. Reports of target lists for planned attacks. "Professional" agitators at gatherings that turn violent. Paid/armed demonstrators in riots. Significant increase in thefts, armed robberies, violent crime (rural); bank robberies (urban).

**Opposition-Directed Aggressive Behavior Within the Population:** Refusal or difficulty in collecting rent, taxes, or loan payments. Trends of hostility toward government or mission forces. Unexplained disappearance of the population or avoidance of certain areas. Unexplained disappearance/relocation of children and adolescents. Reported attempted recruitment into new movements or underground organizations. Criminals and disaffected youth acting with/for the opposition. Reports of extortion/coercion for financial support. Fear tactics to coerce, control, or influence the population. Surveillance of host-nation government or mission force facilities and personnel.

**Activities Directed Against the Government or Mission Force:** Failure of police/informer nets to report accurately (sources actively supporting opposition or intimidated). Decreasing success of infiltration of opposition organizations. Assassination/disappearance of government intelligence sources. Reports of attempts to bribe/blackmail officials, law enforcement, or mission personnel. Classified information leaked to media. Sudden affluence of certain government/law enforcement personnel. Recurring failure of raids on suspected opposition apparently due to forewarning. Increased hostile/illegal activity against the government, its forces, foreigners, or competing groups. Demonstrations designed to instigate violent confrontations. Strikes or walkouts in critical industries/areas to cast doubt on the government's ability to maintain order. Unexplained loss, destruction, or forgery of identification cards and passports. Recurring unexplained disruption of public utilities. Reports of terrorist acts or extortion against local leaders and business persons. Murder or kidnapping of officials or mission personnel. Closing of schools. Attempts to obtain classified information from officials, offices, or mission personnel.

### Table B-6. Propaganda Indicators

**General Indicators of Negative Propaganda:** Dissident propaganda from unidentified sources. Increase in entertainers with a political message. Increase of political themes in religious services. Appeals to intensify ethnic/religious unrest. Agitation on issues with no identified movement/organization. Renewed activity by groups thought defunct. Circulation of petitions advocating opposition demands. Opposition slogans by word-of-mouth, graffiti, posters, leaflets. Propaganda linking local ethnic groups with those in neighboring countries/regions. Clandestine radio broadcasts appealing to those with grievances or underprivileged ethnic groups. Bullhorns/loudspeakers/public address equipment in "spontaneous" demonstrations. Non-media photographers among demonstrators.

**Propaganda Activities Directed Against the Established Government:** Attempts to discredit or ridicule national/public officials. Attempts to discredit the judicial and law enforcement system. Characterization of government projects and plans. Radio/Internet propaganda from foreign countries aimed at the target population, accusing the government of failing to meet the people's needs.

**Propaganda Activities Directed Against the Mission Force and Host-Nation Military/Law Enforcement:** Accusations that host-nation military and police are corrupt and out of touch with the people. Accusations that mission force personnel will introduce customs/attitudes opposed to local cultural or religious beliefs. Character assassination of mission, military, and law enforcement personnel.
