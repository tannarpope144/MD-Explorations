# Pattern Analysis

**Category:** Core Army analytic technique — conducting pattern analysis
**Source:** ATP 2-33.4, *Intelligence Analysis*

## Definition

Conducting pattern analysis is an analytical technique that aids intelligence personnel in determining possible future enemy actions when there is little or no near-real-time information about enemy location, disposition, movement, or objectives. When conventional threats mask themselves from collection, or unconventional forces are largely invisible to it, analysts study how the enemy operated in the past to predict how they may operate in the future.

Pattern analysis helps determine **when, where, and what type** of enemy activity may occur in the future by analyzing when, where, and what type occurred in the past. It is useful for both conventional military forces and unconventional, complex, adaptive threat networks. Through it, analysts may anticipate future attacks, identify high-payoff targets, and refine threat characteristics.

## Three basic activities

- Determining what is known about the enemy.
- Conducting a pattern analysis of the enemy's recent activity.
- Determining possible enemy actions.

## Four tools (used concurrently or separately)

1. **Chronologies** — timelines and time event charts.
2. **Pattern analysis plot sheet.**
3. **Incident overlay.**
4. **Pattern of life analysis.**

All four are effective for visualizing patterns in time, space, and activity.

## Chronologies

A **chronology** is a list that places events or actions in the order they occurred; a **timeline** is a graphic depiction of those events. Both identify trends or relationships between events/actions (and, for a timeline, between events and other events in the context of the overarching problem). Events may or may not have a cause-and-effect relationship. Use whenever it is important to understand the timing and sequence of relevant events and to identify key events and gaps. Two types: timelines and time event charts.

### Timelines

Aid in identifying patterns and correlations between events, relating seemingly random events to the big picture and highlighting significant changes, trends, developing issues, or anomalies.

- Linear; relate to a single situation or COA. Multiple-level timelines track concurrent COAs that may impact each other.
- Useful at the onset of a task (to establish context) and in postmortem studies (to find causes of intelligence failures after a surprise).
- Activities can lead the analyst to hypothesize that particular events occurred between known events for the flow to make sense — then watch for indicators to find and chart the missing events.
- Organize information for easy understanding in a briefing; support other methods (event trees, complex-network techniques).

**Caution:** do not assume events following earlier events were *caused* by them — there may be no causal relationship. Value drops if the analyst lacks creativity in finding contextual events. Consider factors that may influence timing (e.g., attack times varying by hours but driven by the lunar cycle/moonset, religious events, or friendly patrol patterns).

**Method (chronology or timeline):**
1. As you research, list the relevant information with the date or order in which it occurred. Properly reference the data.
2. Review by asking:
   - What are the temporal distances between key events? If lengthy, what caused the delay? Are there missing pieces of data to collect?
   - Did the analyst overlook intelligence that may have impacted the events?
   - If events seem to happen faster than expected, is the analyst conflating multiple event timelines?
   - Are all critical events necessary and shown for the outcome to occur?
   - What are the intelligence gaps?
   - What are the vulnerabilities in the timeline for collection activities?
   - What events outside this timeline could have influenced the activities?
3. If preparing a timeline, summarize the data along a line (horizontal or vertical). Use the sides of the line to distinguish data types; use multiple lines to show how and where multiple actors converge.

*Tips:* chronologies/timelines are an effective, simple way to order incoming daily message traffic; use tools such as Excel (drawing function) or Analyst Notebook to draw the timeline.

### Time event chart

A method for visualizing individual or group actions chronologically, designed to store and display large amounts of information in a small space. Helps analyze larger-scale patterns of activities and relationships. Uses symbols to represent events, dates, and the flow of time.

- **Triangles** depict the beginning or end of the chart.
- **Rectangles** store administrative data and indicate events/activities; each contains a sequence number and date, with an incident description below.
- An **"X"** through an event highlights a significant event/activity.
- Flow is left to right for each row; the date is always on the symbol.

**Example/inference:** two months after organizing, members received demolitions training (funded by drug smugglers); three weeks later, three members (narcotics traffickers) drove to Kabul, and 4–5 days after arrival there was a bombing at Kabul Airport they claimed; months later, four members drove to Kandahar, and four days later a bombing occurred at police headquarters. From this, analysts inferred: the group operates in teams of 3–4; seven members received demolitions training in Pakistan (capability for multiple attacks); attacks occur 4–5 days after a team arrives (time to reconnoiter); and the pattern indicated an attack in Herat on 21 or 22 January 2015.

## Pattern analysis plot sheet

Depicts patterns in **time and activity** and helps identify when the threat tends to conduct specific types of activities. It is a circular matrix and a calendar: the matrix is divided by time (generally by hour) and subdivided into concentric rings identifying days.

**Example:** six enemy ambushes occurred early in the month between 1800 and 1900; later, two occurred between 1900 and 2000 — possibly indicating a shift in threat tactics, techniques, and procedures (or a change in friendly operations). Further analysis is needed. The plot sheet is effective for visualizing timeframes and types of activity but must be used with other intelligence to support logical conclusions.

**Method:**
- Use a different symbol for each type of incident; track all symbols in a legend.
- Mark all incidents on both the time-wheel matrix and the calendar.
- Annotate noteworthy events with footnotes (the symbol must be marked on both the matrix and the calendar; the footnote is described separately below or near the plot sheet).

## Incident overlay

Similar to a situation map; displays activities **spatially** and is useful for visualizing spatial patterns, but does **not** show temporal patterns. Like a situation map, it cannot stand alone — it only illustrates what and where an event took place. Always include a legend and the time period covered.

Constructed by plotting specific activities on the map using appropriate symbols. Counterinsurgency and stability environments may require many unfamiliar symbols, so a legend is critical; use ADRP 1-02 symbols as much as possible. Maps may be imagery or standard military maps. Incident overlays are excellent briefing tools for updating the enemy situation within a unit's AO.

**Method:**
1. **Select the type of incidents to display** (a particular type, incidents by a particular cell, or analyst's discretion). List relevant information with date/order; properly reference the data.
2. **Plot the incidents** to show activity spatially. Examine by asking:
   - What are the spatial distances between events? If close, what terrain/circumstances caused the grouping? Missing data to collect?
   - Did the analyst overlook intelligence that affected the events?
   - If events are spatially distant, why? Is the analyst conflating multiple groups/cells?
   - What are the intelligence gaps? Are spatial changes in TTPs apparent?
   - What are the collection vulnerabilities in the overlay?
   - What outside events could have influenced the activities?
3. **Display multiple incident overlays,** color-coding specific events or cells. Analyze for patterns (same questions) and summarize the data.

## Pattern of life analysis

A focused analysis of **where and when** a target has been, intended to predict **where the target will be.** Directly aids operational planning. Uses network diagrams and timelines to display a target's historical movement patterns in time and space.

Should be as in-depth as possible — including potential refuge locations, work locations, travel patterns, known vehicles, and social activities. Develop pattern-of-life factors from as many sources as possible: routine contact with local operational management teams, human intelligence collection teams, and the supporting SIGINT section; higher-echelon collection corroborates the patterns.

Used with [network analysis](network-analysis.md) and/or [link analysis](link-analysis.md), pattern of life supports targeting by determining **where** the HVI will be and **when.** (Example: if the analyst identifies that an HVI visits a particular restaurant, that location can be monitored as an anticipated future location for time-sensitive targeting.)

**Method (a combination of multiple techniques):**
1. Select the pattern analysis tools to be used and focus on the target as you construct it.
2. Construct a pattern of life network diagram, visually linking events, people, objects, and places in time and space to the target (flow left to right).
3. Review the diagram and tools; analyze the target for patterns that may predict future events or locations. Ask:
   - What are the temporal distances between key events? If lengthy, what caused the delay? Missing data to collect?
   - Did the analyst overlook intelligence that impacted the events?
   - If events happen faster than expected, is the analyst conflating multiple targets?
   - Are certain critical events necessary for the predicted location to occur?
   - What are the intelligence gaps? What collection may fill them?
   - What are the vulnerabilities in the target's timeline for collection activities?
   - What events outside this timeline could have influenced the activities?
