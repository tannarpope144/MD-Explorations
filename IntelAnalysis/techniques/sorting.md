# Sorting

**Category:** Basic structured analytic technique
**Source:** ATP 2-33.4, *Intelligence Analysis*

## Definition

Sorting is a basic structuring technique for grouping information to develop insights that facilitate analysis.

## When to use

Sorting is effective when information elements can be categorized or subcategorized for comparison, typically using an automated tool such as a spreadsheet. It is particularly useful for:

- Reviewing large data stores related to an intelligence challenge.
- Reviewing multiple categories of information, which — when broken into components — can reveal trends, similarities, differences, or other insights not readily identifiable.
- Initial data gathering and hypothesis generation (it can be used at any stage).
- Sorting transactional data before analysis, such as communications intelligence or transfers of goods.

Sorting large amounts of data can surface trends or abnormalities that would otherwise go unnoticed, and can highlight new analytic insights within an old or new intelligence problem.

## Method

1. **Review the categories** into which the information is broken down to determine which categories or combinations might reveal trends or abnormalities. Place data into a spreadsheet, database, or wheel, using as many fields (columns) as necessary to differentiate among data types (dates, times, locations, people, activities, amounts). List each fact, piece of information, or hypothesis you may want to use in the sorting schema.
2. **Review the listed items** to identify key fields that may uncover patterns or groupings. These become header categories. (Example: if most attacks occur in hotels and restaurants but the times vary, "location" is the main category while date and time are secondary.)
3. **Group items** according to the schema defined in Step 1.
4. **Choose a category and sort** the data within it. Look for insights, trends, or oddities.
5. **Review and re-review** the sorted items for alternative ways to sort them. List alternative sorting schemas. One of the most useful applications is to sort according to multiple schemas and examine the results for correlations between data and categories (e.g., most terrorist attacks in hotels also happen in June).

## Examples

- **Tribal leader attitudes:** Are local tribal leaders pro-U.S., anti-U.S., or neutral toward U.S. policy in the Middle East? Sort leaders by factors that provide insight — birthplace, ethnicity, religion and religious sect, level of professional education, foreign military/civilian/university exchange training (where/when), political influences in life, political decisions made, and whether U.S. forces have negatively or positively affected their tribe. Then review for parallels between categories.
- **Cell phone communications:** Data from cell phone communications among five conspirators is reviewed to determine the frequency of calls, patterns in calls to discover the key communicator, any changes in call frequency prior to a planned activity, and the dates and times of those calls.

## Tips

- Get others to review the sorted information to increase brainstorming opportunities and discover new ways of sorting the data for greater insight.
- Remember that correlation is not the same as causation.
- Return to sorting at any point during analysis when new insights are gained — it can either support or negate conclusions.

## Cautions

- Improper sorting can obscure valuable insights as easily as it can illuminate them.
- Standardizing the data being sorted is imperative. Collaborating with an analyst experienced in sorting can help avoid this pitfall.
