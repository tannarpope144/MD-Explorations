# Delphi Technique

**Category:** Emerging analytic technique — structured
**Source:** ATP 2-33.4, *Intelligence Analysis* (Appendix A)

## Definition

The Delphi technique is a systematic, interactive forecasting method that relies on a panel of independent analysts. The analysts answer questionnaires in two or more rounds. After each round, a facilitator provides an anonymous summary of the analysts' conclusions from the previous round, along with the reasons for those conclusions. Analysts are encouraged to revise their earlier answers based on the replies of others. The goal is to reduce division and converge toward a commonly agreed-upon answer.

The process concludes after a predefined stop criterion is met (e.g., number of rounds, achievement of consensus, or stability of results). The mean or median scores of the final rounds determine the results.

## Why anonymity matters

Participants usually maintain anonymity, and their identities are not revealed even after the final report. This:
- Prevents individuals from dominating others through authority or personality.
- Frees individuals (to some extent) from personal biases.
- Minimizes the "bandwagon effect" or "halo effect."
- Allows individuals to express opinions freely and encourages open critique and admission of errors to revise earlier judgments.

## Method

1. An analyst, serving as the **moderator**, sends a questionnaire to a panel of experts who may be in different locations.
2. The experts respond, typically explaining their responses briefly.
3. The moderator collates the results, identifying common and conflicting viewpoints.
4. The moderator sends the collated responses back to all panel members, requesting them to reconsider their responses based on what they learn from others' responses and explanations. Members may also be asked to answer another set of questions (which may or may not be based on the collated answers).
5. If consensus is not reached, the process continues through thesis and antithesis, gradually working toward synthesis and building consensus.

## When to use

Like [ACH](analysis-of-competing-hypotheses.md), Delphi is effective for complex problems such as those found in counterinsurgency. (Example: given a list of Afghanistan provinces with a generalized indicator list for Taliban use of criminal enterprises, the analyst can determine which provinces — in prioritized order with rationale — are most dangerous to the Afghan government.)
