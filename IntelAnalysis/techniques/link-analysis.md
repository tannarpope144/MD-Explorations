# Link Analysis

**Category:** Core Army analytic technique — analyzing complex networks and associations
**Source:** ATP 2-33.4, *Intelligence Analysis*

## Definition

Link analysis is a technique used to evaluate relationships (connections) between various types of objects — including organizations and individuals — using visualization tools to organize and display data. It identifies, analyzes, and visualizes patterns in data through collection, processing, visualization, and analysis.

Link analysis provides a **quicker, more superficial** look at connections and associations between individuals, organizations, events, and activities. It is the technique most often employed at the tactical level, especially at brigade and battalion analytic elements. (Contrast with [network analysis](network-analysis.md), which goes deeper and is used at operational/strategic levels. The two names are often used interchangeably and analysts often blend them with [social network analysis](social-network-analysis.md).)

Though it can be done manually, link analysis in the U.S. military is largely automated (link analysis software is standard on Army intelligence processors from theater to company level). Originally proven in law enforcement analysis of criminal organizations, it is now used to analyze terrorist and other complex networks.

## Three visualization tools

Link analysis generally follows this order (Steps 1 and 2 are often interchanged or done concurrently):

1. Construct an **association matrix.**
2. Construct an **activities matrix.**
3. Construct a **link diagram.**

### Without automation

An alternative uses small sticky notes on a whiteboard: label notes, place them, and draw connections with markers. Benefits: a potentially larger picture than a computer monitor; easy movement of entities, quick redrawing, and color coding; and collaborative team work.

## Association matrix

Identifies the **existence and type of relationships** between individuals as determined by direct contact (face-to-face meetings, telephonic conversations, membership in a group or organization).

- A **known** association is depicted by a dot or filled-in circle; a **suspected** association by an open circle. Depicting suspected associations approaches an objective solution while staying close to confirmed facts and can focus collection requests to confirm them.
- Suspected associations are considered possible or probable but unconfirmed (e.g., a known party calling a known number where it cannot be determined who answered; a meeting where one party is identified and the other only tentatively).
- Constructed as a right triangle with the same number of rows and columns.

> **Note.** Without modification, the association matrix shows only the *existence* of relationships — not the nature, degree, or duration.

**Method:**
1. Construct a triangular matrix with multiple columns and rows (software tool, spreadsheet, or by hand).
2. Extract entities and relationship information from imagery, SIGINT intercepts, message traffic, or any valid, pertinent source.
3. Place names of individuals and organizations along the angled side; one entity per row and column. **Personalities must be listed in the same order in the association matrix and the activities matrix** so all possible associations are correct.
4. Analyze entities and associations: place a solid circle where columns and rows meet for a known association; an unfilled circle for a suspected one.

> **Note.** Add symbols/colors to clarify relationships — e.g., half-colored circles for greater probability of association (using [subjective probability](subjective-probability.md)), green circles where the association involves money.

## Activities matrix

Determines connections between an **individual and organizations, events, locations, or activities** (excluding other individuals). It is a rectangular array of personalities compared against activities, locations, events, or other relevant information. The number and content of rows/columns are tailored to the problem. Critical for studying a group's internal and external activities, external ties and linkages, and modus operandi.

**Method:**
1. Construct a rectangular matrix with multiple columns and rows (software, spreadsheet, or by hand).
2. Extract entities and relationship information from any valid, pertinent source.
3. Place names of individuals/organizations along one side (one per row), in the **same order** as the association matrix. Place activities, locations, buildings, and facilities along the top or bottom (one per column).
4. Analyze: place a solid circle for known associations, unfilled for suspected.

## Link diagram

Graphically displays connections between individuals, organizations, and activities. Created from a unit's historical files and current reporting. Use whenever individuals, groups, group activities, or process networks are reviewed for insight; the need increases with the complexity of data and networks.

- A link diagram is a **living document that is never finished.** Link analysis provides a snapshot and seldom conveys change over time unless paired with a timeline or other multidimensional approach.
- Can be created manually or with software (e.g., DCGS-A has Analyst Notebook).
- Clarifies what is known and what may be missing; key nodes and hubs can be identified for social, organizational, and infrastructure networks.
- **Caution:** analysts might assume a central figure with the most connections is the leader, but link diagrams do not depict time relationships.

**Symbols:** circles = individuals or organizations; squares = activities; triangles/rectangles = buildings and facilities. Solid lines = associations; dashed lines = possible associations. Colored/varied lines can show different activities (e.g., green solid = money transfer, blue dotted = communications, solid black = activity).

**Method:**
1. Extract entities and relationship information from association and activities matrices, imagery, SIGINT intercepts, and message traffic.
2. Place entity associations into a link diagram (software tool, spreadsheet, or by hand).
3. Analyze the entities and links.
4. Review for gaps, significant relationships, and meaning, asking critical questions:
   - Which entity is central or key to the network?
   - Who or what initiates interactions?
   - What role does each entity play?
   - Who or what forms a bridge or liaison between groups/subgroups?
   - How have the interactions changed over time?
   - Which nodes should be targeted for collection or defeated?
5. Summarize what is seen and draw interim hypotheses regarding the relationships.

> **Note (clutter).** Charts may become cluttered with too much data; set aside peripheral data, use simple charts, break large charts into smaller ones, and eliminate crossing lines for clarity. Software such as Analyst Notebook allows instant redrawing as new information arrives.
