# Futures Wheel

**Category:** Emerging analytic technique — structured
**Source:** ATP 2-33.4, *Intelligence Analysis* (Appendix A)

## Definition

A Futures Wheel is a method used by analysts to identify **second- and third-order effects** of a potential course of action (COA). Originally developed in the 1970s to identify the potential consequences of trends and events, it is also useful for decision-making and analyzing the impact of operations.

## When to use

Analysts are regularly tasked with exploring the impact of a proposed operation or COA. To avoid compiling a shallow, incomplete list, the Futures Wheel provides a structured method and visual tool. It uses structured brainstorming to move from the COA to secondary and tertiary effects, and potentially beyond.

Examples of COAs placed at the center: funding construction operations, conducting a raid on a mosque, or selling arms to a foreign nation.

## Method

1. **First-order effects.** Once the COA is identified (placed at the center of the workspace), brainstorm the possible direct consequences of that COA. These should be realistic and within the capabilities of the entities considered.
2. **Second-order effects.** Use each first-order effect as a starting point for further brainstorming. These indirect effects should be realistic but may not be directly traceable to the original COA. Repeat to determine third, fourth, and further orders of effects.
3. **Mitigate.** Once all desired levels of effects are complete, the wheel presents a clear picture of the possible direct and indirect consequences. The analyst then considers ways to mitigate and manage negative consequences and provides them as alternatives for the decision-maker.
