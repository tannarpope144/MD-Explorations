# Network Analysis

**Category:** Core Army analytic technique — analyzing complex networks and associations
**Source:** ATP 2-33.4, *Intelligence Analysis*

## Definition

Network analysis examines dynamic, multi-link human networks characterized by varying degrees of uncertainty — such as terrorist and other irregular threat organizations typically encountered in counterinsurgency. Unlike conventional hierarchical military organizations, these are cellular and distributed. Network analysis involves knowledge management, research, and modeling to extrapolate the structure, locations, members, goals and objectives, activities, and the defeat mechanism for these organizations.

## Link analysis vs. network analysis

The two are very similar and the names are often used interchangeably. Both use visualization tools to depict enemy networks. The difference:

- **[Link analysis](link-analysis.md):** a quicker, more superficial look at connections; most often employed at the tactical level (brigade/battalion).
- **Network analysis:** takes the effort to the next level — examines the structure and types of associations, the centrality of entities, and the organization of the network. More time- and resource-intensive; employed at operational and strategic levels.

Either can be used at any level; analysts choose the best technique(s) to meet the commander's requirements.

## Method — eight steps

1. **Construct a network chart or diagram.** Extract entities and relationship information from association and activities matrices, imagery, SIGINT intercepts, and message traffic. Place associations into a diagram (software or spreadsheet). Each element is an icon (person, building, etc.) — the more accurate the icon, the more informative. Draw connections.
2. **Identify, combine, or separate nodal components.** List each node in a database/program. Delineate each node and interaction by meaningful criteria (frequency of contact, type of contact, type of activity, source of information). Re-color connections to present each criterion in a different color/style.
3. **Identify the functions of each node, as possible.** Determine centrality; examine network density and distance:
   - What is the centrality of each node?
   - What role is each entity involved in?
   - Who/what forms a bridge or liaison between groups/subgroups?
   - How have interactions changed over time?
   - Which nodes should be targeted for collection or neutralization?
4. **Cluster the nodes.** Look for dense areas; draw shapes around them using varied shapes/colors/line styles to denote types of clusters, confidence, or other criteria.
5. **Review the clusters.** Identify gaps, significant relationships, meanings, network structure, centrality, and density/distance. Determine the function of clusters in the larger network.
6. **Chart the flow of activities between nodes and clusters.** Analyze flow to assess resiliency:
   - Which node initiates interactions?
   - Does it go one direction or multiple?
   - Are the same or different elements/nodes involved?
   - What are the pathways?
   - If one node or pathway were removed, are there built-in alternatives?
7. **Group the clusters into larger clusters** to identify larger organizational networks. Identify network structure, centrality, and density/distance of nodes within the larger cluster.
8. **Summarize what is depicted** and draw hypotheses regarding the network. Continually update and revise as nodes or links change.

## Fundamental concepts

A **network** is an interconnected or interrelated group/chain — a functionally, physically, and/or behaviorally related group of regularly interacting or interdependent elements forming a unified whole.

A system consists of interconnected **nodes** and **links:**
- **Nodes** are tangible elements that can be targeted for action — people, places, or things (materiel, facilities). An individual in a network is a node; a node may also be a non-person point where subsidiary parts originate/center (e.g., the intelligence node or logistics node of a terrorist cell).
- **Links** are the behavioral or functional relationships between nodes — command/supervisory arrangements, a vehicle's relationship to a fuel source, the ideology connecting a propagandist to terrorists. Links establish the interconnectivity that lets nodes work together as a system.

A **critical node** is an element, position, or command-and-control entity whose disruption or destruction immediately degrades the ability of a force to command, control, or effectively conduct operations.

Networks interact with each other, supporters in the population, and political/security/economic/key leaders. They are **resistant to the loss of any one or several nodes,** so targeting focuses not just on who/what to target but on what loss will cause the most damage. The ultimate success is to remove sufficient critical nodes simultaneously (or nearly so) that the network cannot automatically re-route linkages and suffers catastrophic failure. (Example: destroying air defense radars eliminates the link between radars and missiles, degrading the whole air defense system without attacking every node.)

Network analysis can be conducted at — and across — the strategic, operational, and tactical levels.

## Organizational structure

The **cell** is the smallest element of organizations (usually 3–10 people, sometimes a lone member). Cellular configuration provides security — compromise of one cell should not compromise others. Compartmentalizing functions makes the organization difficult to penetrate; personnel in one cell are often unaware of other cells. Cells may be based on family, employment, geography, or function (e.g., direct action, intelligence), or be multifunctional. The cell leader is normally the only person who communicates with higher levels and other cells.

Two basic structures (a group may use either, both, or a mix that changes at different levels):

- **Hierarchical structure:** a well-defined vertical chain of command, control, and responsibility. Data flows up and down vertical channels but may not move horizontally. Features specialization in subordinate cells (support, operations, intelligence); usually only the cell leader knows other cells/contacts, and only senior leaders have visibility of the whole organization. (Military order of battle is a hierarchical network where cell leaders are commanders — but, unlike insurgent/terrorist organizations, military organization and leadership are generally known by all members.)
- **Networked structure:** broader systems used by more experienced groups. Groups based on religious or single-issue motives may lack a specific political/nationalistic agenda and need less hierarchy — they thrive through loose affiliations across various locations. General goals/targets are announced; individuals or cells use flexibility and initiative. Effectiveness depends on a unifying ideology, common goals, or mutual interests; without one, cells may pursue counterproductive objectives. Networks distribute responsibility and plan redundancies; cells avoid unnecessary coordination, enabling leaders to deny responsibility and enhance operational security. Networks are not necessarily dependent on modern IT — low-tech means (couriers, paper messages, landlines) can avoid detection.

### Common network structural options

- **Chain:** each cell links to the next in sequence; communication passes along the line. Common among networks smuggling goods/people or laundering money. Severing one node disrupts the chain until a link is reestablished.
- **Hub-and-wheel:** cells communicate with one central element (which need not be the leader/decision-maker). In the *wheel* variation, outer nodes also communicate with one or two other outer cells. Common in financial/economic networks. Target the central node, or isolate a particular cell, depending on purpose.
- **All-channel:** all nodes are connected to each other; organizationally flat with no hierarchical command above it; command and control are distributed. Communication-intensive and a security risk if linkages can be identified.

These three are encountered together in networked organizations (e.g., a transnational terrorist organization using chain networks for money-laundering, tied to a wheel network for finance, connected to an all-channel leadership network, directing a hub network conducting targeting surveillance/reconnaissance). Selection of critical nodes/linkages depends on the required outcome (surveillance, disruption, destruction, or insertion of misinformation/corrupted technology).

- **Affiliate associate:** a loosely affiliated method depending on ideological intent rather than formalized command/control or support. Semi-independent/independent cells plan and act within their own means to promote a common ideological position; may form from a general inspiration (e.g., Al Qaeda or similar announcements). Connections outside the center are often unconfirmed.
- **Independent confederate:** an individual with some direct contact with a cell who aligns with an extremist agenda, or one who develops extremist views with no apparent external support and acts alone. Another type is a delusional person with psychological/physical ailments whose actions could be mistaken for deliberate terrorism — these conditions are unrelated to functional structures/organizations.
