# "What If" Analysis

**Category:** Emerging analytic technique — contrarian
**Source:** ATP 2-33.4, *Intelligence Analysis* (Appendix A)

## Definition

"What if" analysis imagines that an unexpected event has occurred with potential major impact. Then, with the benefit of hindsight, the analyst figures out how the event could have come about and what the consequences might be.

## When to use

Similar to [high impact/low probability analysis](high-impact-low-probability.md), but it does not dwell on the consequences as much — it accepts the significance and moves directly to explaining how the event might come about. It creates an awareness that prepares the analyst to recognize early signs of a significant change.

Important when a judgment rests on limited information or unproven assumptions. It shifts focus from asking *whether* an event will occur to working from the premise that it *has* occurred, allowing the analyst to determine how it might have happened. This opens the mind to think in different ways and enables development of indicators to monitor.

## Method

Begin by stating the conventional analytic line, then step back to consider what alternative outcomes are too important to dismiss, no matter how unlikely. Once the event itself is established:

1. **Assume the event has happened or is already happening.**
2. **Select triggering events** that permitted the scenario to unfold, to make the "what if" more plausible (e.g., the death of a leader, a natural disaster, an economic event that starts a chain of others).
3. **Develop a chain of reasoning** based on as much logic as evidence to explain how this outcome could have come about.
4. **Think backwards from the event** in concrete ways, specifying what must actually occur at each stage of the scenario.
5. **Identify one or more plausible pathways** to the event (more than one will likely appear possible).
6. **Generate a list of indicators or signposts** to detect the beginnings of the event.
7. **Consider the scope of positive and negative consequences** and their relative impact.
8. **Monitor the indicators** on a periodic basis.
