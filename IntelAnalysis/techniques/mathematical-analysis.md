# Mathematical Analysis

**Category:** Core Army analytic technique
**Source:** ATP 2-33.4, *Intelligence Analysis*

## Definition

Mathematical analysis is effective in determining the capabilities and limitations of an organization based on an evaluation of tangibles and intangibles. It aids the analyst in calculating the overall combat effectiveness of an organization, employing simple mathematical ratios to answer intelligence questions — typically involving the comparison of gross numbers.

## Ratios

A ratio is a comparison of two numbers, separated by a colon (:) or slash (/). A ratio of 8 to 13 may be written 8:13 or 8/13. **Order matters** — a ratio of 1:7 is not the same as 7:1. (Example: a convoy of 15 supply trucks, 2 Strykers, and 4 MRAP vehicles has an MRAP-to-truck ratio of 4:15.)

Mathematical analysis uses both:
- **Tangible facts** — items that can be physically counted (personnel, equipment).
- **Intangible facts** — harder to quantify (leadership, combat experience, training, area knowledge).

## Method

1. **Evaluate the enemy situation:** number of personnel, types of equipment, tactics, combat experience, training, leadership, and area knowledge.
2. **Evaluate the friendly situation:** number of personnel, types of equipment, tactics, combat experience, training, leadership, area knowledge, and the mission.
3. **Write a short paragraph** explaining the intangible facts (tactics, combat experience, training, leadership, area knowledge).
4. **List the tangible facts** below the paragraph for each force (number of personnel, types of equipment).
5. **Evaluate both intangible and tangible facts,** comparing them using mathematical ratios to show the strengths and weaknesses of enemy and friendly forces.
6. **Draw conclusions** based on the mathematical ratios in combination with the intangible facts.

## Worked example — foot reconnaissance near Tuc, Afghanistan

**Enemy situation:** An insurgent cell of ~11 seasoned fighters operates in and around Tuc. The cell leader is known locally, grew up in the area, and knows the terrain. The cell has conducted several successful attacks against Afghan forces in 12 months; their most common method is an "L"-shaped ambush in complex terrain. Typical equipment:
- 11 × AK-47s
- 2 × 9-mm pistols
- 1 × RPD light machinegun
- 3 × RPG-7V
- 8 × fragmentation grenades
- 4 × ICOM radios
- 11 × personal cell phones
- 6 × command-detonated IEDs

**Friendly situation:** Mission is a foot reconnaissance of a route to a commerce village. The patrol leader has been to the village three times (once by helicopter, twice as a patrol member) — this is his first time as patrol leader. The patrol was briefed that the area had no indicators of enemy presence or activities. Patrol of 16 personnel:
- 12 × U.S. Soldiers (incl. a 2-person public affairs team)
- 2 × Interpreters/translators
- 2 × Afghan soldiers (both speak basic English)

Equipped with:
- 10 × M4s (2 with grenade launchers)
- 2 × surface acoustic waves
- 5 × AK-47s
- 4 × 9-mm pistols
- 2 × antitank weapons
- 40 × fragmentation grenades
- 2 × man-packed satellite communications radios
- 15 × personal cell phones

**Analysis:**
- Total numbers: force ratio favors friendly by 1.4:1. Combat troops only: ~1:1.
- Intangibles: combat experience, leadership, and area knowledge favor the enemy.
- Firepower:
  - Crew-served weapons: 2:1 favoring friendly.
  - Assault rifles: 5:4 favoring friendly.
  - Pistols: 2:1 favoring friendly.
  - Grenade launchers: 1:5 favoring friendly.
  - Antitank weapons: 2:0 favoring enemy.
  - Grenades: 5:1 favoring friendly.
  - Demolitions: 6:0 favoring enemy.
- Other intangibles: type and amount of ammunition are even; fields of fire and cover/concealment favor the enemy.

**Conclusion:** Based on aggregate force ratios, U.S. forces would most likely be defeated by an undetected enemy ambush. Recommend conducting additional reconnaissance to mitigate enemy advantages and ensure the security of the patrol.
