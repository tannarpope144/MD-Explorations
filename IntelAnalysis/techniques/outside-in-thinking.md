# Outside-In Thinking

**Category:** Emerging analytic technique — imaginative
**Source:** ATP 2-33.4, *Intelligence Analysis* (Appendix A)

## Definition

Most analysts spend their time on familiar factors within their field — thinking from the *inside* (what they control) *out* to the broader world. Outside-in thinking reverses this: it begins by considering the external changes that might, over time, profoundly affect the analyst's own field or issue.

## When to use

Most useful at the conceptualization of an analytic project, when the goal is to identify all the critical external factors that could influence how a situation will develop. It works well for a group of analysts responsible for a range of functional and/or regional issues.

When assembling a large database that must identify a number of information categories or fields, it helps visualize the entire set of categories needed — reducing the risk (with modest effort) of realizing too late that additional categories are needed and having to recode all previous data.

By recasting the problem in much broader and more basic terms, analysts are more likely to uncover additional factors, an important dynamic, or a relevant alternative hypothesis.

## Method

Begin by developing a generic description of the problem or phenomenon under study. Then:

- List all the key forces — social, technological, economic, environmental, political — that could impact the topic but over which one can exert little influence (e.g., globalization, social stress, the Internet, the global economy).
- Focus next on key factors over which an individual or policymaker can exert some influence (in business: market size, customers, competition, suppliers, partners; in government: policy actions, behavior of allies or adversaries).
- Assess how each of these forces could affect the analytic problem.
- Determine whether these forces actually do have an impact, based on the available evidence.
