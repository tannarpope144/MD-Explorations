# Key Assumptions Check

**Category:** Diagnostic analytic technique
**Source:** ATP 2-33.4, *Intelligence Analysis*

## Definition

A key assumption is any hypothesis that analysts have accepted as true, forming the basis of the assessment. (Example: military analysis may focus exclusively on key technical and military variables and assume forces will operate in a specific environment — desert, open plains, arctic conditions.)

## When to use

A key assumptions check is most useful at the beginning of an analytic project. Rechecking assumptions can also be valuable at any time prior to finalizing judgments. Identifying hidden assumptions can be challenging because they are often held subconsciously and seldom examined or challenged.

Explicitly identifying working assumptions helps analysts understand the key factors shaping the issue and stimulates critical thinking. It also aids in explaining a logical argument while exposing potentially flawed reasoning. **Key assumption checks should be collaborative, as self-checking is often ineffective.**

Postulating other conditions or assumptions could dramatically impact an assessment. The goal is not to undermine or abandon key assumptions but to make them explicit and identify what information or developments would necessitate reconsidering them.

Examples of consequential hidden assumptions:
- U.S. analysis of Soviet-Warsaw Pact operations against NATO had to "assume" a level of non-Soviet-Warsaw Pact reliability (whether those forces would actually engage in combat). Depending on the level of reliability assumed, analysts could reach very different conclusions about a potential Soviet offensive.
- Economists assessing prospects for foreign economic reforms may consciously or unconsciously assume a degree of political stability that may not exist in the future.
- Political analysts reviewing a developing country's domestic stability might unconsciously assume stable oil prices, which could fluctuate and significantly affect economic performance and social peace.

## Method — four-step process

1. **Review the current analytic line of thinking** on the issue. What do you believe you know? What key details support the assumption's truth? Document this for analytic review.
2. **Articulate all premises** — both stated and implied in finished intelligence — that are accepted as true.
3. **Challenge the assumptions** by asking why they must be true and whether they are valid under all conditions. What is the degree of confidence in those initial answers?
4. **Refine the list of key assumptions** to include only those that "must be true" to sustain your analytic line. Consider under what conditions, or in the face of what information, these assumptions might not hold.

## Questions to ask during the process

- How much confidence exists that this assumption is correct?
- What explains the degree of confidence in the assumption?
- What circumstances or information might undermine this assumption?
- Is a key assumption more likely a key uncertainty or a key factor?
- If the assumption proves wrong, how would it significantly alter the analytic line?
