# Red Team Analysis

**Category:** Emerging analytic technique — imaginative
**Source:** ATP 2-33.4, *Intelligence Analysis* (Appendix A)

## Definition

Red Team analysis consciously places analysts in the same cultural, organizational, and personal setting ("putting them in their shoes") in which the target individual or group operates. While analysts typically work from the perspective of *blue* (friendly forces), a Red Team attempts to operate within the environment of the hostile forces.

## When to use

Used to forecast how a foreign leader or decision-making group may behave when there is a risk of falling into the "mirror-image" problem — assuming a foreign leader has the same motives, values, or understanding as the analyst. History shows foreign leaders often respond differently due to different cultural, organizational, or personal experiences.

Like [devil's advocacy](devils-advocacy.md) and [Team A/Team B](team-a-team-b.md), Red Team analysis frees the analyst from the constraints of a well-developed mindset — their own sense of rationality, cultural norms, and personal values. It transforms the analyst from an observer of a foreign threat into an adversary operating within the threat's culture and political context. Particularly useful for replicating the mindset of authoritarian leaders, terrorist cells, or other non-Western groups operating under vastly different codes of behavior or motivations. It can introduce new stimuli not considered in traditional analysis (familial ties; international political, economic, and military pressures) and incorporate how personal power and status might influence behavior.

## Method

Conducting Red Team analysis is challenging and requires significant time to develop a team of qualified experts who can think like the threat. The team must distance itself from conventional analysis and operate as if living in the target's world. Without a sophisticated understanding of the culture, environment, and personal histories, analysts will struggle to behave or think like the enemy.

Assemble a team with in-depth knowledge of the operational environment, the target's personality, and the relevant style of thinking — individuals who have experienced the culture, share the ethnic background, or worked in similar environments (language understanding desirable, not strictly necessary). Once established and separated from traditional analysis, team members should:

- Put themselves in the threat's circumstances and react to foreign stimuli as the target would.
- Develop first-person questions the threat would ask: "How would I perceive incoming information? What would be my personal concerns? To whom would I look for an opinion?"
- Draft analytic papers in which the leader or group makes specific decisions, proposes recommendations, or outlines COAs. The more these reflect the cultural and personal norms of the target, the more they provide a different perspective.
- Avoid using qualifying statements; assume the recipient understands the paper is intended to provoke thought or challenge conventional understanding of how a threat thinks.

> For more detailed information, see the *Red Team Handbook*, published by the University of Foreign Military and Cultural Studies, Fort Leavenworth, Kansas. Analysts may also consider attending one of the Red Team courses offered there.

## Red Hat vs. Red Team

- **[Red Hat analysis](red-hat-analysis.md)** can be conducted by *any* analyst who needs to understand or forecast foreign behavior and can gain access to the required cultural experience. Goal: develop the best possible analysis of a threat's intent.
- **Red Team analysis** is typically conducted by a *permanent organizational unit or a temporary, specially staffed group.* Goal: challenge organizational biases and provide alternative threat COAs.
