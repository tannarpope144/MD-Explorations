# Situational Logic

**Category:** Core Army analytic technique
**Source:** ATP 2-33.4, *Intelligence Analysis*

## Definition

Situational logic is an analytical methodology used when a situation is regarded as unique, and analysis will not benefit from broad or specific comparisons with other events. It is typically used to trace cause-effect relationships or — when dealing with purposeful behavior — means-end relationships. (Example: an analyst identifies the goals being pursued and explains why an enemy believes certain means will achieve them.)

Situational logic is the **most common** method of intelligence analysis, sometimes called the area studies approach. It involves generating different hypotheses based on concrete elements of the current situation while avoiding broad, global generalizations. Even though most analysts know this to be untrue, every situation is treated as unique, to be understood in terms of its own logic.

It is cause-and-effect logic based on the assumption of rational, purposive behavior: the analyst identifies the goals being pursued by the enemy and explains why the enemy believes certain means will achieve those goals.

## Method — three-step process

1. A single entity (country, province, organization, military, or political group) is examined across multiple interrelated issues. The analyst conducts in-depth research and builds a knowledge base on the target entity.
2. The analyst identifies the logical antecedents of the situation — called **building a scenario.** The knowledge base from Step 1 is used to gain a deeper understanding of the situation and how it came about.
3. Using the knowledge base and understanding of the situation, the analyst works backwards to explain the origins of the current situation and identifies logical consequences to estimate the future outcome.

## Cautions — two weaknesses

- It is difficult for an analyst to see problems as an enemy may see them. A major risk is **projecting personal values onto an enemy.**
- The process does not incorporate theoretical data from other areas or events that may assist in the analysis.
