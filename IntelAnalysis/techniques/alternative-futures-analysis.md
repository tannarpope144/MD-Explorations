# Alternative Futures Analysis

**Category:** Emerging analytic technique — imaginative
**Source:** ATP 2-33.4, *Intelligence Analysis* (Appendix A)

## Definition

Alternative Futures Analysis (often referred to as **scenarios**) is a divergent thinking technique that uses the complexity and uncertainty of a situation to describe multiple outcomes or futures that analysts and policymakers should consider — rather than predicting a single outcome. It sharply contrasts with [contrarian techniques](devils-advocacy.md), which aim to challenge analysts' high confidence and relative certainty about an event or trend.

## When to use

Most useful when a situation is viewed as too complex, or outcomes too uncertain, to trust a single-outcome assessment. Use when:

- Analysts recognize high uncertainty regarding the topic.
- Analysts and customers acknowledge the need to consider a wide range of factors.
- Analysts are prepared to explore a variety of outcomes and are not committed to any preconceived result.

Extremely useful in highly ambiguous situations with both known unknowns and unknown unknowns. Because outcomes are not known beforehand, analysts must be prepared for the unexpected and engage in a more open exchange of views than usual. Given the time and resources involved, scenario analysis is best reserved for situations that could pose grave threats or significant consequences.

Benefits:
- Provides an effective means of weighing multiple unknown/unknowable factors and presenting a set of plausible outcomes.
- Helps bind a problem by identifying plausible combinations of uncertain factors.
- Offers a broader framework for calculating costs, risks, and opportunities of different outcomes.
- Helps anticipate surprising developments by forcing challenges to assumptions and consideration of wild cards or discontinuous events.
- Generates indicators to monitor for signs that a particular future is becoming more or less likely, allowing policy reassessment.

Involving decision-makers in the exercise is the most effective way to communicate results and sensitize them to key uncertainties; most participants find developing the scenarios as useful as any finished product.

**Caution:** depending on complexity, the effort can require a significant investment of time, analytic resources, and money — from several hours/days to a multi-day workshop with outside experts, often requiring trained scenario-development facilitators and conferencing facilities.

## Method

The most common approach (public and private sectors):

1. Develop the focal issue by systematically interviewing experts and officials examining the general topic.
2. Convene a group of experts (internal and external) to brainstorm the forces and factors that could affect the focal issue.
3. Select by consensus the two most critical and uncertain forces and convert them into axes or continuums.
4. Establish the most relevant endpoints for each factor (e.g., if economic growth is the critical uncertain force, endpoints could be fast/slow or transformative/stabilizing).
5. Form a futures matrix by crossing the two axes. The four resulting quadrants characterize alternative future worlds.
6. Generate colorful stories describing these futures and how they could plausibly come about. Develop indicators.

Participants (especially decision-makers) then consider how current decisions or strategies would fare in each of the four worlds and identify alternative policies that might work better across all futures or in specific ones — improving the chance of devising flexible, agile strategies.
