# Deception Detection

**Category:** Diagnostic analytic technique
**Source:** ATP 2-33.4, *Intelligence Analysis*

## Definition

Deception is a threat action intended to influence the perceptions, decisions, or actions of another to the advantage of the threat. Deception detection consists of a set of checklists analysts use to determine when to look for deception, discover whether deception is present, and figure out how to avoid being deceived.

## When to use

Although deception detection is time-consuming, analysts should be concerned about deception when the threat has much to gain by denying or manipulating information collection systems and analysts. It is an effective tool for validating collected information and for supporting conclusions and assessments drawn using other techniques.

When evaluating information used to derive intentions or courses of action (COAs), analysts must consider whether it is genuine or part of a deception campaign intended to redirect collection efforts or lead to other conclusions. Attempting deception detection can strengthen analysis and enhance the effectiveness of other techniques — analysts may otherwise place too much confidence in techniques if they have not considered the possibility of deception.

Historically, intelligence analysis has been vulnerable to deception. Analysts seldom check for it, even when there is a well-known history of its use. The possibility cannot be dismissed simply because there is no evidence of deception; if done well, the analyst should not expect to see any evidence on first examination.

## Method — four sets of assessment criteria

Assess key reporting against the following:

**1. Does the threat have the motive, opportunity, and means?**
- Motive: What are the threat's goals?
- Channels: What means are available?
- Risks: What are the risks of discovery?
- Costs: Can deception be accomplished?
- Feedback: Can the threat monitor its use?

**2. Would this potential deception be consistent with past opposition practices?**
- Does the threat have a history of deception?
- Does this deception fit past patterns?
- If not, are there other historical precedents?
- If not, are there changed circumstances that would explain this form of deception?

**3. Do we have cause for concern regarding the susceptibility to manipulation of the threat?**
- Is the source reliable?
- Does the source have access?
- Is the source vulnerable to control or manipulation by the threat?

**4. What can be learned from the evaluation of evidence?**
- How accurate is the source's reporting?
- Is the whole chain of evidence available?
- Does critical evidence check out?
- Does evidence from one source conflict with others?
- Do other sources provide corroborating evidence?
- Is the absence of evidence unusual?

## Rules for dealing with deception

- Avoid over-reliance on a single source of information.
- Seek and heed the opinions of those closest to the reporting.
- Be suspicious of human sources or sub-sources who have not been personally met or whose information sources are unclear.
- Be cautious of information that appears too easy to collect and presents too perfect a picture.
- Always seek material evidence (documents, reports, imagery) rather than relying exclusively on verbal accounts.
- Look for patterns where a source's information initially seemed correct but later proved false.
- Generate and evaluate a full set of hypotheses at the outset of a task.
- Understand the limitations and capabilities of collection assets, sources, and potential deceivers.
