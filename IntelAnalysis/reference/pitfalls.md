# Analytical Pitfalls, Fallacies & Biases

**Category:** Reference — analytic skills (Chapter 2)
**Source:** ATP 2-33.4, *Intelligence Analysis*

Analytical pitfalls are the influences that shape and mold an analyst's view of the world and ability to reason. The elements of thought, intellectual standards, and intellectual traits help analysts recognize these pitfalls in their own analysis and in the analysis of others. The two general categories of pitfalls are **logic fallacies** and **biases.**

This file also captures the critical-thinking framework (elements of thought, intellectual standards, intellectual traits) used to detect and counter the pitfalls, plus Occam's Razor.

---

## Foundations: basic thinking abilities

Three basic thinking abilities are required for intelligence analysis:

- **Information ordering** — following defined rules to arrange data in a meaningful order so it can be analyzed and synthesized. *Danger:* such ordering is inherently limiting — the analyst may not look for alternative explanations because the known rules lead to an easy conclusion.
- **Pattern recognition** — detecting or imposing patterns to identify relationships and infer future outcomes; separating the important from the trivial. *Danger:* analysts may impose culturally defined patterns on random aggregates rather than recognize inherent patterns, misinterpreting events.
- **Reasoning** — processing information and formulating explanations. Four types:
  - **Inductive** — conclusion based on observed facts; a process of discovery establishing a relationship between events. Normally precedes deduction and is the type used most frequently. Depends on accurate observation and statistics; tainted data harms it. Cannot produce absolute truth, only high probabilities. (Example: patrols ambushed on route blue 0500–1800, four to six days a week → a 57–85% chance daily patrols would be attacked.)
  - **Analogical** — compares similarities between new and understood concepts to gain understanding of the new. (Example: two incidents where forced diversions led to ambush → when obstacles force an unplanned route, beware ambush from elevation or concealment.)
  - **Deductive** — applies general rules to specific problems. A deductive argument is *sound* if its premises are true, but sound reasoning does not guarantee a true conclusion (and is not always effective in forecasting human behavior).
  - **Abductive** — the thought process accompanying insight or intuition; when information doesn't match expectations, the analyst generates a new hypothesis explaining why. (Example: a group that aided U.S. operations stops and is now reported supporting insurgents → ask why the dynamic changed, develop and test explanations.)

## Critical-thinking framework

### Elements of thought (eight)

All thinking has these parts: we think for a **purpose**, within a **point of view**, based on **assumptions**, leading to **implications and consequences**; we use **concepts** to interpret **information** in order to answer a **question at issue** through **interpretation and inference**.

1. **Purpose** — all thinking has a purpose; state it clearly, distinguish it from related purposes, and check you're on target.
2. **Question at issue** — state questions clearly and precisely; express them several ways; break into sub-questions.
3. **Information** — support conclusions with relevant information; actively search for information that contradicts as well as supports a position; ensure accuracy, clarity, relevance.
4. **Interpretation and inference** — infer only what the evidence implies; crosscheck inferences; identify the assumptions/concepts behind them; consider alternatives. *This is the most important part of what analysts provide.*
5. **Concepts** — a generalized idea of a thing; people do not always share the same concept. Identify the meaning ascribed to key concepts and check whether others ascribe different meanings.
6. **Assumptions** — a proposition accepted as true without supporting fact. The availability of fact determines how much assumption is needed. Identify assumptions and determine whether they are justifiable.
7. **Implications and consequences** — trace where reasoning leads; search for negative as well as positive implications.
8. **Point of view** — all thinking is from some point of view; recognize your own, seek others, and consider them fairly.

### Intellectual standards (nine)

Criteria used to assess the *quality* of thinking:

1. **Clarity** — the gateway standard; without it, accuracy/relevance/logic cannot be judged.
2. **Accuracy** — represent things as they actually are. (We tend to believe our own thoughts are accurate just because they are ours, and fail to question statements that agree with what we already believe.)
3. **Precision** — provide the details needed to understand exactly what is meant; never sacrifice clarity for precision.
4. **Relevance** — stay connected to the question; set aside what is not pertinent.
5. **Depth** — get beneath the surface; address inherent complexities responsibly.
6. **Breadth** — consider the issue from every relevant viewpoint; empathize with contrary viewpoints.
7. **Logic** — combined thoughts should be mutually supporting and make sense.
8. **Significance** — concentrate on the most important information and ideas; not all relevant ideas are equally important.
9. **Fairness** — think in accordance with reason and take into account the views of others; counter self-deception, personal bias, and ego.

### Intellectual traits

Human traits that support critical thinking (associated paragraphs from the source index): **fair-mindedness**, **intellectual humility**, **intellectual courage**, **intellectual empathy**, **intellectual integrity**, **intellectual perseverance**, **confidence in reason**, and **intellectual autonomy.**

- **Confidence in reason** — belief that one's higher interests are best served by giving the freest play to reason; using standards of reasonability to judge whether to accept or reject any belief. *Questions:* Am I willing to change position when evidence leads to a more reasonable one? Do I adhere to sound reasoning, or distort matters to support my position? Is it more important to "win" or to see the most reasonable perspective? Do I encourage others to reach their own conclusions, or force my views?
- **Intellectual autonomy** — thinking for oneself while adhering to standards of rationality; thinking through issues using one's own reasoning rather than uncritically accepting others' viewpoints. *Questions:* To what extent am I a conformist? Do I think through issues on my own? Having reasoned through an issue, am I willing to stand alone despite irrational criticism?

## Logic fallacies

Errors in the reasoning process caused by failure to apply sound logic. Usually unintentional, but sometimes used deliberately to persuade, convince, or deceive. Three families: relevance, omission, assumption.

### Fallacies of relevance

Appeal to evidence or examples irrelevant to the argument.

- **Appeal to force** (*argumentum ad baculum*, "might-makes-right") — uses force, threat, or unpleasant backlash to make the audience accept a conclusion. Common as a last resort; logically irrelevant to the merits.
- **Genetic fallacy** — claims an idea, product, or person must be wrong because of its origin (e.g., "That car can't be good — it was made in Japan!"). Closely related to *ad hominem*.
- **Argumentum ad hominem** ("argument to the man"; "poisoning the well"; "personal attack") — discounts evidence before it is presented, usually by discrediting the source. Two subcategories:
  - *Abusive* — arguing assertions must be false/dangerous because of an irrational psychological transference with the originator.
  - *Circumstantial* — arguing opponents should accept/refute an argument only because of circumstances in their lives (e.g., telling an imam to accept an argument because rejecting it is incompatible with the Koran). Special circumstances do not affect truth.
- **Argumentum ad populum** ("argument to the people") — appeals to popular assent by arousing feelings rather than building an argument. Three approaches:
  - *Bandwagon* — "everybody is doing it"; majority belief makes it true/best.
  - *Patriotic* — "draping oneself in the flag"; a stance is correct because it is patriotic, dissent is unpatriotic (look for emotionally charged terms).
  - *Snob* — "all the best people are doing it"; dissenters are not equal to the elite who agree.
- **Appeal to tradition** (*argumentum ad traditio*) — a premise must be true because people have always believed/done it, or it has always worked and thus always will.

### Fallacies of omission

Leaving out necessary material in a conclusion or inference.

- **Oversimplification** — a generality that fails to account for all complex conditions (ignoring facts, using generalities, or applying an inadequately qualified generalization to a specific case).
- **Composition** — drawing a conclusion about a whole based on the features of its parts, without justification.
- **Division** — inferring that what is true of a whole must be true of its parts.
- **Post hoc ergo propter hoc** ("after this, therefore because of this") — omitting other factors that might account for the same result; common when establishing cause and effect.
- **False dilemma** (black-and-white thinking) — omitting consideration of more than two alternatives when more exist.
- **Hasty generalization** — conclusions drawn from samples too few or unrepresentative.
- **Special pleading** — creating a universal principle, then insisting it does not apply to the issue at hand (e.g., "Everything must have a creator. Except God.").

### Fallacies of assumption

Implicitly or explicitly involve assumptions that may or may not be true.

- **Begging the question** (circular reasoning) — the conclusion occurs as one of the premises; supporting a statement by repeating it in stronger terms (e.g., "Our suppressive fire was inadequate" to explain why the enemy wasn't pinned down — suppressive fire is *defined* as pinning down the enemy).
- **Stating hypotheses contrary to fact** — decisively stating what would have happened had circumstances been different, based on assumptions that are faulty or unprovable.
- **Misusing analogies** — generalizing indiscriminately from analogy to the real world. The strength of a conclusion drawn from similar situations is proportional to the degree of similarity. *Danger:* assuming that because things are alike in certain aspects, they are alike in all. A **counter-analogy** weakens the original by citing other comparisons on the same basis.

## Biases

A bias is a subjective viewpoint — a preconceived notion about someone or something. Biases generally have a detrimental impact because they obscure the true nature of information. Recognize cultural, organizational, personal, and cognitive biases.

### Cultural bias

The inability to see things through the eyes of someone from another country or culture. Also known as **mirror imaging** — attributing someone else's intentions, actions, or reactions to the same logic, cultural values, and thought processes as the analyst. Difficult to avoid; to lessen its impact:
- Locate individuals who understand the culture; include them, ask their opinions on likely responses to friendly actions — but take care, as their knowledge may be biased, dated, or inaccurate.
- Locate regional experts (foreign/regional area officers) who have lived/traveled in the area; assess the quality of their information against their level of knowledge and experience.

### Organizational bias

Organizations have policy goals or preconceived ideas; internal analysis may be less objective than the same analysis done externally.
- **Groupthink** — a judgment is unconsciously altered because of exposure to selective information and common viewpoints. Involving outsiders helps correct it.
- **Best case** — presenting good or bad news in the most optimistic light; deliberately altering judgment to tell the commander what they want to hear. Counter with the moral courage to tell the whole story.

### Personal bias

The tendency to base assessments on personal beliefs, leading to rejection of valid arguments that conflict with those beliefs. Three common types:
- **Confirmation bias** — undervaluing or ignoring evidence that contradicts an early judgment while valuing evidence that confirms existing assessments.
- **Assimilation bias** — modifying and elaborating new information to fit prior conceptions or hypotheses, favoring a preconceived answer.
- **Anchoring bias** — unwitting use of arbitrary values in decision-making, including reliance on conclusions developed by others.

### Cognitive bias

Affects how the analyst evaluates information whose reliability, completeness, and consistency vary from source to source:
- **Vividness** — clear, concise information has greater impact than abstract/vague information, even when the vague information may be more accurate. (An enemy may use deception to present vivid facts the friendly effort is meant to believe.)
- **Absence of evidence** — lack of information is the analyst's most common problem, especially tactically. Counter by: realizing information will be missing; identifying gaps and considering alternative conclusions; adjusting judgments as information arrives; and considering whether a lack of information is normal or is *itself* an indicator.
- **Oversensitivity to consistency** — consistent evidence drives confidence, but consistency may be due to redundancy, a small/biased sample, or deception. Counter by being receptive to other sources and alert for **circular reporting** (intelligence reformatted by other units, slightly modified, and fed back). Know the original source for all intelligence.
- **Persistence of impressions** — connections that explain evidence form impressions; even after the evidence is discredited, the connection and impression remain.
- **Dependency on memory** — readily available memory influences judgment; it is easy to rely on memory instead of seeking new information.
- **Acceptance of new intelligence** — new intelligence is often valued subjectively, as having more or less significance than current intelligence.

## Occam's Razor

A standard guideline for logical reasoning (named for William of Ockham). If two competing theories explain a single phenomenon, both reach the same conclusion, are equally persuasive, and satisfactorily explain the problem, choose the **less complex** one. The theory with fewer moving parts is most likely correct — "Thou shalt not multiply extra entities unnecessarily."

*Illustration:* a dog escaped its kennel and chewed the couch. **Theory One:** you forgot to latch the kennel (2 entities, 2 actions). **Theory Two:** a skilled intruder picked the lock, freed the dog, hid the evidence, and relocked the door (3+ entities, several actions, plus an absent motive). Without evidence such as strange fingerprints or missing possessions, the simpler theory is more likely correct. (This principle is also captured in [Rules for Verification](../techniques/rules-for-verification.md), Rule 7.)
