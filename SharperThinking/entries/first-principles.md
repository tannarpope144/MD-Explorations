## Definition
Breaking a problem down to the foundational truths you can directly verify —
axioms, physical laws, observed facts — and rebuilding the solution from there,
rather than reasoning by analogy to how the problem is usually solved.

## Steps
1. State the claim or goal plainly.
2. Ask what assumptions it rests on. Make them explicit.
3. For each assumption, ask: "Is this actually true, or just conventional?"
   Strip out anything that's borrowed authority rather than verified fact.
4. Rebuild from what remains. The new structure may look nothing like the old one.

## When to use
- The conventional answer feels expensive, slow, or wrong, but you can't say why
- Entering a field where incumbents share assumptions that may be obsolete
- Cost estimation: when a price quote reflects supply-chain markup rather than
  underlying material cost (Musk's rocket / battery example)
- Designing something new where existing templates would constrain you

## When NOT to use
- Reversible, low-stakes decisions — the overhead of deconstruction isn't justified
- Domains where the conventions encode hard-won wisdom you don't yet understand
  (Chesterton's fence: don't tear down what you can't first explain)
- When you lack the domain knowledge to recognize which "facts" are actually facts
  vs. plausible-sounding errors — first-principles reasoning amplifies bad inputs
- Time-critical decisions where reasoning by analogy is "good enough" and faster

## Examples
1. Musk on rocket cost: instead of accepting that rockets cost ~$65M (analogy to
   prior launches), he priced the raw materials — aluminum, copper, carbon fiber,
   titanium — at ~2% of that. The gap was supply-chain and process, not physics.
2. Euclid built plane geometry from five postulates. Every theorem traces back
   to those — that's first-principles construction in its purest form.
3. A startup pricing a SaaS product: instead of "what do competitors charge?"
   (analogy), ask "what's the value delivered per customer per month, and what
   margin do we need to be sustainable?" (first principles).

## Notes
The opposite stance is **reasoning by analogy** — "we should do X because X is
how it's usually done" or "X is what worked for Y, so it'll work here." Analogy
is faster and usually correct; first-principles reasoning is slower and
occasionally unlocks an order-of-magnitude improvement. The skill is knowing
when the gap between convention and reality is large enough to be worth the
effort of reconstruction.

A common failure mode: pseudo-first-principles. Stripping one layer of assumption,
declaring the remaining layer "fundamental," and rebuilding on top of *that* —
when the remaining layer was itself convention. Real first-principles reasoning
keeps asking "and why is that true?" until you hit something you can verify
directly (a measurement, a physical law, an axiom).
