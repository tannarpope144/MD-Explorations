# The Rules for Rulers: Power, Succession, and the Structure of Political Survival

Adapted from CGP Grey's *Rules for Rulers* and *Death & Dynasties*, themselves drawn from Bueno de Mesquita and Smith, *The Dictator's Handbook*.

---

## Abstract

This paper outlines a structural theory of political power applicable to both autocratic and democratic regimes. Power is reframed not as the capacity of a single ruler to act, but as the ability to direct others through the controlled distribution of resources. From this premise follow three operational rules governing the acquisition and retention of authority, as well as a corollary concerning succession and dynastic continuity.

---

## I. Introduction

Observers frequently characterize rulers as foolish, selfish, or short-sighted. Yet the same patterns of behavior recur across centuries and regime types, suggesting that the throne itself imposes constraints upon its occupant. To take the throne is to be acted upon by it. The following rules describe those constraints.

## II. The Foundational Premise

No ruler governs alone. A king cannot build roads, enforce laws, or defend the state without intermediaries. The power of a ruler lies not in personal action but in directing others to act on his behalf, financed by the treasure he controls.

The individuals required to execute these functions are termed the **keys to power**. Without their cooperation, the ruler's intentions remain inert.

Regimes lie on a spectrum defined by the number of keys required. Dictatorships rest on few — perhaps a dozen generals, bureaucrats, and regional officials. Democracies rest on many — voting blocs, donors, and coalition partners. The rules, however, remain constant.

## III. The Three Rules

### Rule 1: Secure the Key Supporters

The ruler must first obtain the loyalty of those whose cooperation is indispensable. With them, he possesses the capacity to act; without them, he possesses nothing. Displeased keys do not protest — they replace.

### Rule 2: Control the Treasure

The ruler must control the raising and distribution of resources. This is his principal occupation: ensuring that wealth flows to himself and to those whose loyalty sustains him.

A binding tradeoff follows. Every unit of treasure expended on the citizenry is a unit unavailable for the keys. Public spending — roads, universities, hospitals — therefore furnishes rivals with a recruiting instrument: they may promise that same treasure to the keys in exchange for defection. Loyalty, in regimes of concentrated power, is paramount.

### Rule 3: Minimize the Key Supporters

Reigns lengthen as the keyset contracts. Fewer keys mean larger individual rewards, simpler management of rivalries, and reduced exposure to coalition shifts.

A corollary explains the post-coup purge. The keys required to *seize* power are not those required to *retain* it. New rulers therefore discard their own revolutionaries and absorb the previous regime's functionaries. From the outside this resembles betrayal; structurally it is the elimination of unnecessary expenditure.

## IV. Representative Government

Democracies do not exempt themselves from these rules; they apply them at scale. Citizens are managed not as individuals but as blocks — the elderly, homeowners, farmers, business interests — that may be rewarded collectively. Tax codes, subsidies, and regulatory carve-outs are not legislative accidents but payments rendered to politically decisive blocks.

Blocks that do not vote are ignored, irrespective of their size. Once in office, the rational ruler facilitates voting among his blocks, obstructs it among others, redraws electoral boundaries, and constrains primary contests. The result: declining approval coupled with rising incumbency.

Influential individuals remain consequential even in democracies. They are compensated not in direct payments but in loopholes, contracts, drafted legislation, and selective enforcement. The mechanism is identical to that of dictatorship; only the legal framing differs.

Apparent contradictions in democratic behavior — publicly opposing what is privately enabled, injuring voters who elected the ruler — are best understood as coalition arithmetic rather than confusion.

## V. Taxation and Revolt

Tax burden correlates inversely with the number of keys. Democracies, requiring broad consent, tax at lower effective rates. Dictatorships, accountable to a narrow coalition, extract heavily — often through forced sale or direct seizure rather than formal taxation.

Democracies favor citizen productivity, since a smaller percentage of a larger output yields greater returns. Schools, infrastructure, and civil liberties thus emerge less from benevolence than from incentive alignment between ruler and populace.

The worst regimes are those whose wealth derives from extraction rather than labor — oil, gold, diamonds. Where citizens are economically unnecessary, they are politically disposable. Foreign capital extracts the resource; the ruler distributes the proceeds among a narrow elite; the populace is excluded from the cycle and may be safely neglected.

Stability is consequently bimodal. Mature productive democracies are stable because revolt would destroy the productivity it seeks to capture. Resource-rich dictatorships are stable because the population is excluded from the wealth flow. Between them lies a **valley of revolution**, occupied by middling states that depend on citizen labor yet cannot afford broad welfare. The reforms that might lift such a state across the valley — education, mobility, public health — simultaneously drain the keys and enable organized opposition.

Popular uprisings in such states are typically the army permitting the populace to remove a ruler whose keys have already abandoned him. The successor regime is often indistinguishable from, or worse than, its predecessor.

## VI. Succession and Dynasty

Mortality is the structural enemy of every ruler. Because the keys value treasure rather than the ruler personally, they begin planning for a successor at the first credible signal of decline. Once such planning begins, the ruler is functionally deposed even while alive. Rumors of illness are therefore destabilizing and must be concealed.

Family provides the standard hedge. Dynasties are not principally a matter of sentiment; the keys themselves prefer them, since a known family represents a more predictable continuation of the treasure flow than an open contest.

The resulting equilibrium resembles trust without requiring it. Keys support the family during transition to preserve their position; the family supports the keys to preserve the throne. The cycle is self-reinforcing.

Operationally, this implies installing relatives in military, commercial, and political offices regardless of qualification. Familial relationship *is* the qualification, since the function served is the maintenance of existing relationships with the keys. The cost is the perennial risk of intra-family deposition — a risk accepted in exchange for the longer and more secure reign a dynasty affords.

## VII. Conclusion

These rules apply not only to states but to corporations, academic institutions, homeowners' associations, and any structure in which authority is exercised through intermediaries. No actor escapes them by choosing virtue; rulers who ignore the rules are replaced by those who do not.

A zeroth rule governs all the others:

> Without power, nothing can be affected.

The reformer who declines to engage with these structures forfeits the capacity to alter them. Whether the result is better governance depends less on the character of the ruler than on the alignment of structural incentives with the welfare of the governed.
