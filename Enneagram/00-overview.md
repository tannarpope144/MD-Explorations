# The Enneagram as a Character-Simulation Tool

This pack distills the Enneagram into a working toolkit for building and
simulating fictional characters — their **thoughts**, **beliefs**, and
**actions**. Each of the nine type files (`type-1.md` … `type-9.md`) follows the
same structure so you can read one as a profile or drop it into an AI prompt.

The material synthesizes the standard frameworks: the Riso–Hudson Levels of
Development and core fear/desire model, the Naranjo/Chestnut instinctual
subtypes, and classic stress/growth arrow theory.

---

## What the Enneagram is (and isn't) for characters

The Enneagram is a map of **nine core motivations**. It does not describe what a
person does so much as *why* — the fear they are fleeing and the desire they are
chasing. Two characters can perform the same action (lie to a friend) for
completely different reasons, and the *reason* is what makes a character feel
real and consistent.

For simulation, the type answers one question above all others:

> **What is this character's engine?** What do they most deeply want, what do
> they most deeply fear, and what do they do — automatically, under pressure —
> to get the one and avoid the other?

Everything else (wing, subtype, health level, arrows) modulates that engine.

## The nine types at a glance

| # | Name | Core Fear | Core Desire |
|---|------|-----------|-------------|
| 1 | The Reformer / Perfectionist | Being corrupt, defective, wrong | To be good, right, with integrity |
| 2 | The Helper / Giver | Being unwanted, unworthy of love | To feel loved and needed |
| 3 | The Achiever / Performer | Being worthless without achievement | To feel valuable and admired |
| 4 | The Individualist / Romantic | Having no identity or significance | To be uniquely themselves |
| 5 | The Investigator / Observer | Being helpless, depleted, overwhelmed | To be capable and self-sufficient |
| 6 | The Loyalist / Skeptic | Being without support or guidance | To have security and certainty |
| 7 | The Enthusiast / Epicure | Being trapped in pain or deprivation | To be satisfied and free |
| 8 | The Challenger / Protector | Being controlled, harmed, violated | To be in control of their own life |
| 9 | The Peacemaker / Mediator | Loss, separation, fragmentation | Inner and outer peace, wholeness |

## The three Triads (Centers of Intelligence)

Each type belongs to a center that governs its dominant emotion and how it
processes the world. This is the fastest way to "feel" a character's baseline.

- **Gut / Body Triad — 8, 9, 1.** Core emotion: **anger**. Concerned with
  control, autonomy, and resistance to reality. They react first in the body.
  (8 turns anger outward, 1 turns it inward as self-criticism, 9 falls asleep
  to it.)
- **Heart / Feeling Triad — 2, 3, 4.** Core emotion: **shame**. Concerned with
  identity, image, and how they are seen. They are attuned to others'
  approval. (2 focuses on others' image of them, 3 on the image itself, 4 on
  the felt gap between self and ideal.)
- **Head / Thinking Triad — 5, 6, 7.** Core emotion: **fear/anxiety**.
  Concerned with security, certainty, and managing the unknown. They retreat
  into the mind. (5 withdraws to gather, 6 scans for threat, 7 flees forward
  into options.)

A villain from the Gut triad threatens; a villain from the Heart triad
manipulates and seduces; a villain from the Head triad schemes and outmaneuvers.

## Wings

A **wing** is one of the two types adjacent to the core type, which colors it.
Almost everyone leans toward one. A Type 1 is either **1w9** (more withdrawn,
idealistic, cool) or **1w2** (warmer, more interpersonal, advocacy-driven).

For characters: pick the wing that sharpens the contrast you want. The wing
rarely overrides the core engine — it flavors the *style* of pursuing it.

---

## The Arrows: Stress (Disintegration) & Growth (Integration)

Each type has two connected types it moves toward.

- **Stress / Disintegration arrow:** under pressure, the character takes on the
  *average-to-unhealthy* behaviors of another type. This is gold for plot —
  it's how a character cracks.
- **Growth / Integration arrow:** as the character grows or finds security,
  they adopt the *healthy* qualities of another type. This is the arc of
  redemption or maturation.

The full sequences:

- **1 → 4** (stress) / **1 → 7** (growth)
- **2 → 8** (stress) / **2 → 4** (growth)
- **3 → 9** (stress) / **3 → 6** (growth)
- **4 → 2** (stress) / **4 → 1** (growth)
- **5 → 7** (stress) / **5 → 8** (growth)
- **6 → 3** (stress) / **6 → 9** (growth)
- **7 → 1** (stress) / **7 → 5** (growth)
- **8 → 5** (stress) / **8 → 2** (growth)
- **9 → 6** (stress) / **9 → 3** (growth)

Each type file spells out what these movements actually look like in behavior.

## The three Instinctual Subtypes (sp / so / sx)

Underneath the type sits one of three survival instincts that dominates the
character's attention. The same type looks markedly different depending on
which instinct drives it.

- **Self-Preservation (sp):** focused on safety, comfort, health, resources,
  the home, physical security.
- **Social (so):** focused on the group, status, belonging, roles,
  contribution, how they fit into the collective.
- **Sexual / One-to-One (sx):** focused on intensity, attraction, chemistry,
  the charged bond with a specific person or pursuit.

Each type file gives the three subtype variants. Choosing one stops a character
from feeling like a generic "Type 4" and makes them a *particular* Type 4.

## The nine Levels of Development (health)

A type is not a fixed character — it's a range. Riso–Hudson describes nine
levels, which collapse usefully into three bands for fiction:

- **Healthy (Levels 1–3):** the type at its best. The gifts without the grip.
  The character is self-aware and acts from their virtue. **Use for heroes,
  mentors, characters mid-redemption.**
- **Average (Levels 4–6):** the type on autopilot. The defining patterns are
  fully visible, the fixation runs the show, but the character still
  functions socially. **Use for most protagonists and realistic people.**
- **Unhealthy (Levels 7–9):** the type in the grip of its fear. The defense
  mechanism has become destructive to self and others. **Use for villains,
  characters in crisis, tragic falls.**

The single most powerful lever for a character arc is moving them along this
range. A redemption arc climbs the levels; a tragedy descends them.

## How to use these files to drive a character

To simulate a character, assemble four dials, then act only from what that
combination would actually think and do:

1. **Core type** — the engine (fear + desire).
2. **Wing** — the flavor.
3. **Instinctual subtype** — what they pay attention to.
4. **Level of health** — where they are in their range (and which direction
   they're trending, via the arrows).

> Example prompt seed: *"Simulate a Type 6, 6w5, self-preservation subtype, at
> an average level and sliding toward stress (→ 3). They've just been told a
> secret they're not sure they can keep."*

When writing the character, keep returning to the engine. If an action doesn't
trace back to fleeing the core fear or chasing the core desire, it probably
isn't in character. Consistency of *motivation* — not consistency of behavior —
is what sells a fictional person.
